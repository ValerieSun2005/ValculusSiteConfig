In this section we review how to identify the features of quadratics, express quadratics in different forms, and solve quadratic equations and inequalities.

A quadratic is a second-degree polynomial that when graphed produces a parabola: a symmetrical, U-shaped curve. A quadratic expression can be written in standard form, vertex form, or intercept form. The standard form of a quadratic is \[ax^2 + bx + c \comma \] where \(a\) is called the leading coefficient and \((0, c)\) is the \(y\)-intercept, the point at which the graph of \(y = f(x)\) crosses the \(y\)-axis (Figure 1).

The vertex form is

\(\ds f(x) = a(x - h)^2 + k,\) \((2)\)

where \((h, k)\) is the vertex—the maximum or minimum—of the parabola. The vertical line \(x = h,\) called the axis of symmetry, is the line to which the graph of \(y = f(x)\) is symmetrical. Note that, for any of these three forms, if \(a\) is negative (as shown by Figure 2), then the parabola opens downward and the vertex is a maximum. Conversely, if \(a\) is positive, then the parabola opens upward and the vertex is a minimum.

Additionally, the vertex may be calculated from standard form by utilizing the property that the \(x\)-coordinate of the vertex, \(h,\) is given by \(h = \frac{-b}{2a}.\) It then follows that the \(y\)-coordinate of the vertex, \(k,\) is \(f(\frac{-b}{2a}).\) For example, the vertex of \[x^2 - 2x + 8\] has \(x\)-coordinate \[ \ba h = \frac{-b}{2a} &= \frac{-(-2)}{2(1)} \\ &= 1. \ea \] It then follows that its \(y\)-coordinate is \[k = (1)^2 - 2(1) + 8 = 7.\] Thus, the quadratic has a vertex of \((1 , 7).\)


The intercept form is

\(\ds f(x) = a(x - p)(x - q),\) \((3)\)

where \(p\) and \(q\) are zeros of \(y = f(x).\) (See Figure 3.)

FORMS OF A QUADRATIC EXPRESSION
  • Standard Form: \[y = ax^2 + bx + c,\] where \((0, c)\) is the \(y\)-intercept of the parabola.
  • Vertex Form: \[y = a(x - h)^2 + k,\] where \((h, k)\) is the vertex of the parabola.
  • Intercept Form: \[y = a(x - p)(x - q),\] where \(p\) and \(q\) are the zeros of the parabola.
EXAMPLE \(1\)
Classify the form in which each of the following quadratics is written.
  1. \(y = 4(x - 2)(x + 4)\)
  2. \(y = -2x^2 + 8x - 7\)
  3. \(y = 2(x + 8)^2 - 1\)
  1. Intercept form.
  2. Standard form.
  3. Vertex form.

Vertex form may also be obtained from standard form by completing the square. Consider, as an example, the quadratic expression \[y = 3x^2 - 9x + 2.\] Factoring out the leading coefficient, \(3,\) we obtain \[y = 3 \left(x^2 - 3x + \frac{2}{3}\right).\] In the parentheses, we can insert the term \(9/4,\) which is the square of half of \(-3;\) that is, \((-3/2)^2 = 9/4.\) However, we must also subtract it to maintain the equality of the expression: \[3 \left(x^2 - 3x + \underline{\frac{9}{4}} - \underline{\frac{9}{4}} + \frac{2}{3} \right).\] The term \(x^2 - 3x + 9/4\) creates the perfect square \((x - 3/2)^2,\) so we have \[ \ba 3 \left[\left(x - \frac{3}{2}\right)^2 - \frac{9}{4} + \frac{2}{3} \right] &= 3 \left[\left(x - \frac{3}{2}\right)^2 - \frac{19}{12} \right] \\ &= \boxed{3 \left(x - \frac{3}{2}\right)^2 - \frac{19}{4}.} \ea \] The strategic insertion of the square of half the coefficient of the \(x\)-term, or \(9/4,\) forces the expression into a perfect square that corresponds to vertex form. Completing the square is an alternative method of determining the vertex of a quadratic presented in standard form. Rewriting a standard-form quadratic into vertex form will also enable us, in some situations, to better utilize methods of calculus.

COMPLETING THE SQUARE
\((1)\)
Factor out the leading coefficient, \(a.\)
\((2)\)
Analyze the coefficient of the \(x\)-term, and insert the square of half the coefficient. Be sure to also subtract the quantity to maintain equality.
\((3)\)
You should now have a perfect square. Simplify the resulting expression to obtain vertex form.
EXAMPLE \(2\)
Complete the square for the following quadratic expressions.
  1. \(y = x^2 - 5x + 6\)
  2. \(y = 3x^2 - 7x + 5\)
  3. \(y = -2x^2 - 8x + 11\)
  1. There is no leading coefficient to factor out, so we can immediately analyze the coefficient of the \(x\)-term, \(-5.\) We need to insert \((-5/2)^2 = 25/4\) into the expression to force the existence of the perfect square \(\left(x - \frac{5}{2} \right)^2 \!:\) \[ \ba x^2 - 5x + 6 &= x^2 - 5x + \underline{\frac{25}{4}} - \underline{\frac{25}{4}} + 6 \\[1ex] &= \left(x - \frac{5}{2}\right)^2 + 6 -\frac{25}{4} \\[1ex] &= \boxed{\left(x - \frac{5}{2}\right)^2 - \frac{1}{4}.} \ea \]
  2. We must first factor out the leading coefficient, \(3,\) to get \[3 \left(x^2 - \frac{7}{3} x + \frac{5}{3}\right).\] The coefficient of the \(x\)-term is \(-7/3.\) Half of the coefficient is \(-7/6,\) whose square is \(49/36.\) Thus, we need to insert this quantity into the parentheses to create the perfect square \(\left(x - \frac{7}{6} \right)^2.\) \[ \ba 3 \left(x^2 - \frac{7}{3} x + \underline{\frac{49}{36}} - \underline{\frac{49}{36}} + \frac{5}{3} \right) &= 3 \left(x - \frac{7}{6} \right)^2 + 3 \left(\frac{5}{3} - \frac{49}{36} \right) \\ &= \boxed{3 \left(x - \frac{7}{6} \right)^2 + \frac{11}{12}.} \ea \]
  3. Let us factor out the leading coefficient, \(-2,\) to obtain \[-2 \left(x^2 + 4x - \frac{11}{2} \right).\] The coefficient of the linear term is \(4,\) so \((4/2)^2 = 4\) is the quantity we need to insert. We get \[ \ba -2 \left(x^2 + 4x + \underline{4} - \underline{4} - \frac{11}{2}\right) &= -2(x + 2)^2 - 2 \left(-4 - \frac{11}{2}\right) \\ &= \boxed{-2(x + 2)^2 + 19.} \ea \]

To find the zeros of a quadratic equation, we can either factor, complete the square, or use the quadratic formula. For the quadratic equation \[ax^2 + bx + c = 0,\] where \(a \ne 0,\) the quadratic formula states that \[x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}.\] A quadratic may have two, one, or no zeros, depending on the sign of the discriminant. The discriminant, \(\Delta = b^2 - 4ac,\) is the expression in the square root of the quadratic formula.

  • If \(\Delta \gt 0,\) then the quadratic has two real zeros.
  • If \(\Delta = 0,\) then the quadratic has one real zero.
  • If \(\Delta \lt 0,\) then the quadratic has no real zeros.

These facts should be intuitive because if \(\Delta \lt 0,\) then the quadratic formula produces a negative square root, which is undefined. (These cases produce imaginary solutions, but in calculus we only work with real numbers. They are outside of our scope for now.) Similarly, if \(\Delta = 0,\) then the quantity to which the \(\pm\) sign applies is \(0,\) so the only solution is \(x = \frac{-b}{2a}.\) Lastly, if \(\Delta \gt 0,\) then there exists a real quantity from the square root: One solution has the quantity added \(\Big(x = \frac{-b + \sqrt{\Delta}}{2a}\)\(\Big),\) and the other has it subtracted \(\Big(x = \frac{-b - \sqrt{\Delta}}{2a}\)\(\Big)\).

Consider Figure 4, in which three parabolas are shown: \(A,\) \(B,\) and \(C.\) Parabola \(A\) has a positive discriminant because it has two zeros, while parabola \(B\) has a discriminant equal to \(0\) because its graph only has one \(x\)-intercept. Conversely, parabola \(C\) never touches the \(x\)-axis, so its discriminant is negative.

SOLUTIONS OF A QUADRATIC
A quadratic equation \(ax^2 + bx + c\) has solutions given by the quadratic formula, \[x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}, \] for nonzero \(a.\) The discriminant is the quantity in the square root, \(\Delta = b^2 - 4ac.\)
  • If \(\Delta \gt 0,\) then the quadratic has two real zeros.
  • If \(\Delta = 0,\) then the quadratic has one real zero.
  • If \(\Delta \lt 0,\) then the quadratic has no real zeros.
EXAMPLE \(3\)
Solve the following equations.
  1. \(x^2 = 16\)
  2. \(x^2 - 4x + 3 = 0\)
  3. \(2x^2 - 4x = 6\)
  4. \(x^2 - 2x + 2 = 0\)
  5. \(3x^2 - 5x - 7 = 0\)
  1. Taking the square root of both sides, we get \[ \ba x^2 &= 16 \\ |x| &= \sqrt{16} \\ \boxed{x &= \pm 4.} \\ \ea \]
  2. We can factor the equation by identifying two numbers that multiply to \(3\) and add to \(-4.\) They are \(-1\) and \(-3,\) so we have \[(x - 1)(x - 3) = 0.\] By the zero-product property of multiplication, if one of these factors equals \(0,\) then the entire product \((x - 1)(x - 3)\) equals \(0.\) \(x = 1\) and \(x = 3\) each causes one factor—and, thus, the entire expression—to equal zero. Therefore, \(\boxed{x = 1}\) and \(\boxed{x = 3.}\)
  3. It is easiest to solve quadratic equations with all the terms on one side, so rearranging gives \[2x^2 - 4x - 6 = 0.\] Dividing everything by \(2,\) we get \[x^2 - 2x - 3 = 0,\] which we can factor to obtain \[(x - 3)(x + 1) = 0.\] Thus, \(\boxed{x = -1}\) and \(\boxed{x = 3.}\)
  4. The expression \(x^2 - 2x + 2\) cannot be factored, so we must instead resort to either completing the square or using the quadratic formula. There is no leading coefficient, so completing the square is likely the simplest method. The coefficient of the \(x\)-term is \(-2;\) therefore, we can insert the term \((-2/2)^2 = 1 \! :\) \[ \ba \underbrace{x^2 - 2x + \underline{1}}_{(x - 1)^2} - \underline{1} + 2 &= 0 \\ (x - 1)^2 + 1 &= 0 \\ (x - 1)^2 &= -1. \ea \] Note that the term \((x - 1)^2\) is never negative because a term squared is always positive or zero. Likewise, if we take the square root of both sides, we get \[|x - 1| = \sqrt{-1},\] where \(\sqrt{-1}\) is a nonreal quantity. Thus, this equation has no solution. The discriminant for this expression is \[\Delta = b^2 - 4ac = (-2)^2 - 4(1)(2) = -4,\] which is negative. Since a negative discriminant implies that a quadratic has no zeros, this result is affirmed.
  5. For \(3x^2 - 5x - 7 = 0,\) the leading coefficient of \(3\) and uneven numbers make it inconvenient to complete the square, as we did in part (iv). Instead, we will use the quadratic formula: \[ \ba x &= \frac{5 \pm \sqrt{(-5)^2 - 4(3)(-7)}}{2(3)} \\ &= \boxed{\frac{5 \pm \sqrt{109}}{6}.} \ea \]

Let us now discuss quadratic inequalities. Consider, as an example, the inequality \[x^2 - 5x + 6 \gt 0.\] Factoring, we get \[(x - 3)(x - 2) \gt 0.\] We need the factors \((x - 3)\) and \((x - 2)\) to both be positive or both be negative, so we have \[ \ba (x - 3) \gt 0 \quad &\textrm{and} \quad (x - 2) \gt 0 \\ x \gt 3 \quad &\textrm{and} \quad \hspace{5.7ex} x \gt 2, \ea \] and \[ \ba (x - 3) \lt 0 \quad &\textrm{and} \quad (x - 2) \lt 0 \\ x \lt 3 \quad &\textrm{and} \quad \hspace{5.7ex} x \lt 2. \ea \] Therefore, for \(\boxed{x \lt 2}\) and \(\boxed{x \gt 3,}\) the product \((x - 3)(x - 2) \gt 0.\) Alternatively, a shortcut is to consider two intervals: \(x\) between the zeros or \(x\) outside of this interval. That is, we have either \(2 \lt x \lt 3\) or \(x \lt 2, x \gt 3.\) To conveniently determine the correct interval, we can plug \(0\) into the quadratic expression: \[0^2 - 5(0) + 6 \gt 0,\] which is a true inequality. \(0\) is included in the interval \(x \lt 2, x \gt 3,\) so it satisfies the inequality.

SOLVING QUADRATIC INEQUALITIES
\((1)\)
Move all the terms to one side.
\((2)\)
Find the zeros of the quadratic expression.
\((3)\)
Identity whether the solution set of the inequality is between the zeros or beyond the zeros. It is easiest to plug in \(x = 0\) to determine the correct interval.
EXAMPLE \(4\)
Solve the following inequalities.
  1. \(x^2 - 2x - 3 \gt 0\)
  2. \(2x^2 - 5x + 3 \geq 0\)
  3. \(x^2 - 4x \leq -3\)
  4. \(2x^2 + 4 \lt 6x\)
  5. \(5x - 2 \gt -4x^2\)
  1. The factors of \[x^2 - 2x - 3 = 0\] are \(x = -1\) and \(x = 3.\) We need to consider two intervals: \(-1 \lt x \lt 3\) or \(x \lt -1, x \gt 3.\) We can plug \(0\) into the inequality: \[0^2 - 2(0) - 3 \gt 0,\] which is not true. Thus, the interval \(-1 \lt x \lt 3\) is invalid, so \(\boxed{x \lt -1, x \gt 3.}\)
  2. We can factor the expression \(2x^2 - 5x + 3\) as \((2x - 3)(x - 1),\) so its zeros are \(x = 1\) and \(x = 3/2.\) We must consider two intervals: \(1 \lt x \lt 3/2\) or \(x \lt 1, x \gt 3/2.\) At \(x = 0,\) the inequality becomes \[2(0)^2 - 5(0) + 3 \geq 0,\] which is true. Therefore, \(\boxed{x \lt 1, x \gt 3/2.}\)
  3. We need to move all the terms to one side; doing so gives \[x^2 - 4x + 3 \leq 0.\] The zeros of \(x^2 - 4x + 3\) are \(x = -3\) and \(x = -1.\) We need to test the intervals \(-3 \lt x \lt -1\) and \(x \lt -3, x \gt -1.\) At \(x = 0,\) we have \[(0)^2 - 4(0) + 3 \leq 0.\] This inequality is false, so the interval \(-3 \lt x \lt -1\) is invalid. Therefore, the interval \(\boxed{x \lt -3, x \gt -1}\) is correct.
  4. Moving all the terms to one side, we obtain \[2x^2 - 6x + 4 \lt 0.\] \(2x^2 - 6x + 4\) can be factored as \(2(x - 2)(x - 1)\) and therefore has zeros \(x = 1\) and \(x = 2.\) We must determine whether \(1 \lt x \lt 2\) or \(x \lt 1, x \gt 2\) is correct. Plugging in \(x = 0,\) we get \[2(0)^2 - 6(0) + 4 \lt 0,\] a false inequality. Thus, we have \(\boxed{1 \lt x \lt 2.}\)
  5. Let us rewrite the inequality as \[4x^2 + 5x - 2 \gt 0.\] By the quadratic formula, the zeros of \(4x^2 + 5x - 2\) are \[ \ba \frac{-5 \pm \sqrt{(5)^2 - 4(4)(-2)}}{2(4)} &= \frac{-5 \pm \sqrt{57}}{8} \implies \\ x = \underbrace{\frac{-5 - \sqrt{57}}{8}}_{\lt 0} \quad &\textrm{and} \quad x = \underbrace{\frac{-5 + \sqrt{57}}{8}}_{\gt 0}. \ea \] Plugging \(0\) into the inequality yields \[4(0)^2 + 5(0) - 2 \gt 0,\] which is false. Therefore, \[\boxed{x \lt \frac{-5 - \sqrt{57}}{8} \;, \; x \gt \frac{-5 + \sqrt{57}}{8}.}\]
SECTION SUMMARY
  • Quadratics are second-degree polynomials whose graphs are parabolas, which are U-shaped curves.
  • The standard form of a quadratic is \[ax^2 + bx + c,\] where \((0, c)\) is the \(y\)-intercept of the parabola.
  • The vertex form of a quadratic is \[a(x - h)^2 + k,\] where \((h, k)\) is the vertex of the parabola.
  • The intercept form of a quadratic is \[a(x - p)(x - q),\] where \(x = p\) and \(x = q\) are zeros of the parabola.
  • If the leading coefficient, \(a,\) is positive, then the parabola opens upward and the vertex is a minimum. If \(a\) is negative, then the parabola opens downward and the vertex is a maximum.
  • The \(x\)-coordinate of the vertex of the quadratic \[f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c\] is given by \(h = \frac{-b}{2a}.\) The \(y\)-coordinate is \(k = f(\frac{-b}{2a}).\)
  • Completing the square is a process to convert a standard-form quadratic into vertex form. The steps are:
    \((1)\)
    Factor out the leading coefficient, \(a.\)
    \((2)\)
    Analyze the coefficient of the \(x\)-term, and insert the square of half the coefficient. Be sure to also subtract the quantity to maintain equality.
    \((3)\)
    You should now have a perfect square. Simplify the resulting expression to obtain vertex form.
  • A quadratic equation can be solved either by factoring (if possible), utilizing the quadratic formula, or completing the square.
  • For the equation \(ax^2 + bx + c = 0,\) the quadratic formula provides the solutions \[x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} \] for nonzero \(a.\)
  • The discriminant, \(\Delta = b^2 - 4ac,\) is the quantity in the square root of the quadratic formula.
    • If \(\Delta \gt 0,\) then the quadratic has two real zeros.
    • If \(\Delta = 0,\) then the quadratic has one real zero.
    • If \(\Delta \lt 0,\) then the quadratic has no real zeros.
  • To solve a quadratic inequality:
    \((1)\)
    Move all the terms to one side.
    \((2)\)
    Find the zeros of the quadratic expression.
    \((3)\)
    Identity whether the solution set of the inequality is between the zeros or beyond the zeros. It is easiest to plug in \(x = 0\) to determine the correct interval.
