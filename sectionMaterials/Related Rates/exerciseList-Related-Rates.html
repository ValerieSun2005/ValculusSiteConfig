A right triangle has legs of lengths \(x\) and \(y.\) Length \(x\) is increasing at a rate of \(3\) inches per minute, while length \(y\) remains constant. At a certain moment, \(y = 4 \un{in}.\) Find the rate at which the triangle's area is changing at this time.
The area of the triangle is \begin{equation} A = \tfrac{1}{2} xy \pd \label{eq:triangle-area} \end{equation} Quantity \(x\) varies with time, so we treat it as a function of time. And \(y\) is constant, so we don't perceive it as depending on time. We want to find the rate at which the triangle's area is changing—this represents \(\textDeriv{A}{t}.\) And so we differentiate both sides of \(\eqRefer{eq:triangle-area}\) with respect to time, using the Chain Rule: \begin{equation} \deriv{A}{t} = \tfrac{1}{2} \deriv{x}{t} y \pd \label{eq:triangle-dA/dt} \end{equation} Since \(x\) is increasing at \(3 \undiv{in}{min},\) \(\textDeriv{x}{t} = 3 \undiv{in}{min};\) and we are interested in the moment at which \(y = 4.\) Plugging these values into \(\eqRefer{eq:triangle-dA/dt}\) gives \[\deriv{A}{t} = \tfrac{1}{2}(3)(4) = 6 \pd\] Thus, the triangle's area is increasing at a rate of \(6\) square inches per minute. In \(\eqRefer{eq:triangle-area}\) it is important not to plug in \(y = 4\) immediately; we substitute this value after differentiating, as in \(\eqRefer{eq:triangle-dA/dt}.\)

We have \[\frac{10 \cancel{\un{rev}}}{1 \un{min}} \times \frac{2 \pi \un{rad}}{1 \cancel{\un{rev}}} = \frac{20 \pi \un{rad}}{1 \un{min}} \pd \] Thus, \(\textDeriv{\theta}{t} = 20 \pi \undiv{rad}{min}.\) We relate \(x\) and \(\theta\) through \begin{equation} \tan \theta = \frac{x}{40} \pd \label{eq:camera} \end{equation} Differentiating both sides of \(\eqRefer{eq:camera}\) with respect to time, we get \begin{equation} \sec^2 \theta \deriv{\theta}{t} = \tfrac{1}{40} \deriv{x}{t} \pd \label{eq:camera-diff} \end{equation} We want to express all quantities in terms of \(x.\) Using the identity \(\sec^2 \theta = \tan^2 \theta + 1\) and \(\tan \theta = x/40\) [\(\eqRefer{eq:camera}\)], from \(\eqRefer{eq:camera-diff}\) we get \begin{align} \left[\par{\tfrac{x}{40}}^2 + 1\right] \deriv{\theta}{t} &= \tfrac{1}{40} \deriv{x}{t} \nonumber \nl \deriv{x}{t} &= 40 \left[\par{\tfrac{x}{40}}^2 + 1\right] \deriv{\theta}{t} \pd \label{eq:camera-diff-dx/dt} \end{align} In \(\eqRefer{eq:camera-diff-dx/dt}\) we plug in \(\textDeriv{\theta}{t} = 20 \pi\) and \(x = 20 \col\) \[\deriv{x}{t} = 40 \left[\par{\tfrac{20}{40}}^2 + 1 \right] \cdot 20 \pi = 1000 \pi \pd\] Thus, the camera sweeps the area \(20\) feet east of parking space \(\text{B}1\) at a speed of \(1000 \pi\) \(\approx 3141.593 \undiv{ft}{min}.\)
A lighthouse is \(5\) miles north of a straight shoreline. The lighthouse has a revolving beacon that spins at a speed of \(2\) revolutions per second. What is the speed of the spot of light along the shoreline when the spot of light is \(3\) miles away from the point nearest the lighthouse?
The figure shows a top-down view of the situation. The point nearest the lighthouse is \(P,\) \(5 \un{mi}\) to the south. When the lighthouse's beacon spins, light faces a certain spot on the shoreline—point \(Q,\) a distance \(x\) miles away from \(P.\) Let \(\theta\) be the angle shown in the figure. The beacon revolves at \(2 \undiv{rev}{sec};\) in radians per second this rate is \[\frac{2 \cancel{\un{rev}}}{1 \un{sec}} \times \frac{2 \pi \un{rad}}{1 \cancel{\un{rev}}} = \frac{4 \pi \un{rad}}{1 \un{sec}} \pd \] Thus, \(\textDeriv{\theta}{t} = 4 \pi \undiv{rad}{sec}.\) We want to find \(\textDeriv{x}{t}\) when \(x = 3.\) Quantities \(x\) and \(\theta\) are related by \begin{equation} \tan \theta = \frac{x}{5} \pd \label{eq:lighthouse-theta} \end{equation} We differentiate both sides of \(\eqRefer{eq:lighthouse-theta}\) with respect to time, obtaining \begin{equation} \sec^2 \theta \deriv{\theta}{t} = \tfrac{1}{5} \deriv{x}{t} \pd \label{eq:lighthouse-theta-diff} \end{equation} We use the identity \(\sec^2 \theta = \tan^2 \theta + 1\) combined with \(\tan \theta = x/5\) [\(\eqRefer{eq:lighthouse-theta}\)] to rewrite \(\eqRefer{eq:lighthouse-theta-diff}\) as \[\left[\par{\tfrac{x}{5}}^2 + 1\right] \deriv{\theta}{t} = \tfrac{1}{5} \deriv{x}{t} \pd\] Solving for \(\textDeriv{x}{t},\) and substituting \(x = 3\) and \(\textDeriv{\theta}{t} = 4 \pi,\) we have \[\deriv{x}{t} = 5 \left[\par{\tfrac{3}{5}}^2 + 1\right] \cdot 4 \pi = \frac{136 \pi}{5} \pd\] Thus, the light's speed is \(136 \pi/5\) \(\approx 85.451 \undiv{mi}{sec}\) on the shoreline \(3 \un{mi}\) away from the nearest point to the lighthouse.
A uniform, conducting wire has a linear charge density \(\lambda.\) The electric field experienced by a charged object \(r\) meters away due to the wire is \[E = \frac{\lambda}{2 \pi \varepsilon_0 r} \cma\] where \(\varepsilon_0\) is a physical constant called the permittivity of free space. The wire's charge density is decreasing at a rate of \(3\) coulombs per meter per second, and the object's distance from the wire is increasing at a rate of \(2\) meters per second. At a certain instant, the wire's charge density is \(1\) coulomb per meter and the object is \(9\) meters away from the wire. How fast is the electric field changing at this moment?
Quantities \(\lambda\) and \(r\) both vary with time. We are given that \(\textDeriv{\lambda}{t} = -3 \undiv{(C}{m})/\text{sec}\) and \(\textDeriv{r}{t} = 2 \undiv{m}{sec}.\) We want to find \(\textDeriv{E}{t}\) when \(\lambda = 1 \undiv{C}{m}\) and \(r = 9 \un{m}.\) Differentiating \(E\) with respect to time, we get \begin{equation} \deriv{E}{t} = \frac{1}{2 \pi \varepsilon_0} \frac{\deriv{\lambda}{t} r - \lambda \deriv{r}{t}}{r^2} \pd \label{eq:E-field} \end{equation} When \(\lambda = 1 \undiv{C}{m}\) and \(r = 9 \un{m},\) \(\eqRefer{eq:E-field}\) becomes \[\deriv{E}{t} = \frac{1}{2 \pi \varepsilon_0} \frac{-3(9) - 1(2)}{9^2} = \frac{-29}{162 \pi \varepsilon_0} \pd\] Thus, the electric field is decreasing at a rate of \(-29/(162 \pi \varepsilon_0).\)
In baseball the playing field consists of a home plate and three bases—bases \(1,\) \(2,\) and \(3.\) These points are the vertices of a diamond whose sides are \(90\) feet long. (Figure 2 shows a simplified diagram of the playing field.) A baseball player begins to run from second base to third base at a speed of \(20\) feet per second. After three seconds of running, find the rate at which the distance between the player and home plate is changing.
Let \(x\) be the distance the player runs. Since the player runs at a speed of \(20 \undiv{ft}{sec},\) this value is \(\textDeriv{x}{t}.\) After three seconds, the player will have run \(60 \un{ft}.\) Let \(z\) be the distance between the player and home base. We want to find \(\textDeriv{z}{t}\) when \(x = 60 \un{ft}.\) Quantities \(x\) and \(z\) are related by \begin{equation} (90 - x)^2 + 90^2 = z^2 \pd \label{eq:baseball-pythag} \end{equation} We differentiate both sides of \(\eqRefer{eq:baseball-pythag}\) with respect to time, obtaining \begin{align} -2(90 - x) \deriv{x}{t} &= 2z \deriv{z}{t} \nonumber \nl \deriv{z}{t} &= \frac{x - 90}{z} \deriv{x}{t} \pd \label{eq:baseball-dz/dt} \end{align} When \(x = 60,\) \(\eqRefer{eq:baseball-pythag}\) gives \[z = \sqrt{(90 - 60)^2 + 90^2} = \sqrt{9000} = 30 \sqrt{10} \pd\] Substituting this value, along with \(x = 60\) and \(\textDeriv{x}{t} = 20,\) in \(\eqRefer{eq:baseball-dz/dt}\) gives \[\deriv{z}{t} = \frac{60 - 90}{30 \sqrt{10}} \cdot 20 = -2 \sqrt{10} \pd \] Thus, the distance between the player and home base is decreasing at a rate of \(2 \sqrt{10}\) \(\approx 6.325 \undiv{ft}{sec}.\)
A batter hits a baseball from home plate straight to second base at a speed of \(122\) feet per second. After \(0.5\) second, how quickly is the distance between the baseball and first base changing? (See Figure 2.)
Let \(x\) be the distance the baseball travels. Let \(z\) be the distance between the baseball and first base. Since the ball travels at \(122 \undiv{ft}{sec},\) this value is \(\textDeriv{x}{t}.\) After \(0.5 \un{sec}\) the ball will have traveled \(x = 61 \un{ft}.\) We want to find \(\textDeriv{z}{t}\) when \(x = 61 \un{ft}.\) Note that the angle between the baseball's path and the path from home base to first base is \(45 \degree.\) By the Law of Cosines, \[z^2 = x^2 + 90^2 - 2(x)(90) \cos 45 \degree \cma\] or \begin{equation} z^2 = x^2 + 90^2 - 90 \sqrt 2 \, x \pd \label{eq:baseball-cos} \end{equation} We differentiate both sides of \(\eqRefer{eq:baseball-cos}\) to get \begin{align} 2z \deriv{z}{t} &= 2x \deriv{x}{t} - 90 \sqrt 2 \deriv{x}{t} \nonumber \nl \deriv{z}{t} &= \frac{x - 45 \sqrt 2}{z} \deriv{x}{t} \pd \label{eq:baseball-cos-dz/dt} \end{align} When \(x = 61,\) \(\eqRefer{eq:baseball-cos}\) gives \[z = \sqrt{61^2 + 90^2 - 90(61) \sqrt 2} \pd\] Substituting this value, as well as \(x = 61\) and \(\textDeriv{x}{t} = 122,\) in \(\eqRefer{eq:baseball-cos-dz/dt}\) gives \[\deriv{z}{t} = \frac{61 - 45 \sqrt 2}{\sqrt{61^2 + 90^2 - 90(61) \sqrt 2}} \cdot 122 \approx -5.056 \pd \] Thus, the distance between the baseball and first base is decreasing at a rate of \(5.056 \undiv{ft}{sec}.\)
