When you drive a car, the speedometer tells you your instantaneous speed—your change in position at an exact moment. A derivative is the tool we use in calculus to model this behavior. We might define a derivative as measuring a function's sensitivity to change at a certain moment. In this section we introduce a derivative and construct tangent lines.

Definition of a Derivative

The graph of a function \(f\) is shown in Figure 1. A line intersects the graph of \(y = f(x)\) at the point \(P(x, f(x)).\) We call this line a tangent line to \(y = f(x)\) at \(P.\) Loosely speaking, a tangent line "just touches" a curve at a given point. We can't say the tangent line only touches the graph at \(P,\) however, because the line could strike the curve anywhere farther away.

The slope of a tangent line is called the derivative. We calculate the derivative of \(f\) at \(x\) by constructing a secant line—a line that intersects the graph of \(y = f(x)\) at \(P\) and another nearby point \(Q;\) see Figure 2. If the horizontal distance between \(P\) and \(Q\) is \(h,\) then the vertical distance is \(f(x + h) - f(x).\) Therefore, the slope of the secant line is \[m_{PQ} = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \frac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{h} \period\]

If \(Q\) is made closer to \(P\)—that is, \(h\) is smaller—then the secant line better resembles the tangent line \(P.\) If \(h\) is infinitely close to \(0,\) then the secant line collapses into a tangent line; see Animation 1. Therefore, we find the slope of the tangent line by taking the limit of \(m_{PQ}\) as \(h \to 0.\) We denote a derivative as either \(\deriv{y}{x}\) or \(f'(x).\) Thus, \begin{equation} f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{h} \period \label{eq:lim-defn} \end{equation}

We can also write \[\deriv{y}{x} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{h} \period\] Loosely speaking, \(\dd x\) can be thought of as an infinitely small \(\Delta x,\) and \(\dd y\) is similar to an infinitely small \(\Delta y.\) We call these terms differentials. Thus, \(\deriv{y}{x}\) is, roughly speaking, the slope composed of these differentials.

LIMIT DEFINITION OF A DERIVATIVE
The slope of the line tangent to the graph of \(y = f(x)\) at \((x, f(x))\) is \[f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{h} \period \]
EXAMPLE 1
Let \(f(x) = 1/x.\)
  1. Find \(f'(2).\) Interpret this value.
  2. Write an equation of the line tangent to the graph of \(y = f(x)\) at \(x = 2.\)
  1. The expression \(f'(2)\) represents the derivative of \(f(x)\) at \(x = 2.\) We use \(\eqRefer{eq:lim-defn}\) to find \[ \ba f'(2) &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(2 + h) - f(2)}{h} \nl &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\frac{1}{2 + h} - \frac{1}{2}}{h} \ea \]
