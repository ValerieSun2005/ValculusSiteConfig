This section discusses the concept of continuity. We define Continuity at a Point, and we discuss Classifying Continuities and Continuity over an Interval. We also examine the Continuity of Combinations of Functions.

Continuity at a Point

A function \(f\) is continuous at a value \(a\) if the graph of \(y = f(x)\) is smooth at \(x = a.\) In other words, if we can trace the curve of \(y = f(x)\) at \(x = a\) without lifting our pen, then \(f(x)\) is continuous at \(x = a.\) Formally, we say \(f(x)\) is continuous at \(x = a\) if \[\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a) \period \]

DEFINITION OF CONTINUITY
The function \(f(x)\) is continuous at \(x = a\) if \[\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a) \period \]

Classifying Discontinuities

We now learn to classify discontinuities. We consider three types of discontinuities: removable discontinuities, jump discontinuities, and infinite discontinuities.

A function \(f(x)\) has a removable discontinuity—also called a hole—at at \(x = a\) if \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x)\) exists but does not equal \(f(a).\) Alternatively, \(f(a)\) may be undefined. In Figure 1, the graph of \(y = f(x)\) has a removable discontinuity at \(x = a\) because \[\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = K \ne f(a) \period \]

We call this discontinuity removable because we may fill in the hole at \((a, K)\) to restore continuity. It's also possible to define a new function \(g\) that matches \(f\) but is continuous at \(a;\) namely, we let \[g(x) = \begin{cases} f(x) &x \ne a \nl K &x = a \period \end{cases} \]

The function \(f(x)\) has a jump discontinuity at \(x = a\) if \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x)\) does not exist but \(f(a)\) exists and both \[\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) \and \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x)\] exist and are finite (Figure 2). The name is self-explanatory: a jump breaks the continuity in the graph of \(y = f(x)\) when \(x = a.\)

Lastly, the function \(f(x)\) has an infinite discontinuity at \(x = a\) if either \[\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) \or \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x)\] is infinite. At \(x = a,\) the graph of \(y = f(x)\) has a vertical asymptote: \(f\) is unbounded on either side of this asymptote (Figure 3).

TYPES OF DISCONTINUITIES

  1. \(f(x)\) has a removable discontinuity (or a hole) at \(x = a\) if \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x)\) exists but does not equal \(f(a).\)
  2. \(f(x)\) has a jump discontinuity at \(x = a\) if \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x)\) does not exist.
  3. \(f(x)\) has an infinite discontinuity at \(x = a\) if either \[\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) \or \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x)\] is infinite.

EXAMPLE 1
For each of (i)–(iii), classify the discontinuity of \(f(x)\) at the given value of \(x.\)
  1. \(\ds f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 16}{x + 4}\; \) at \(x = -4\)
  2. \(\ds f(x) = \frac{1}{x + 3} \; \) at \(x = -3\)
  3. \(\ds f(x) = \begin{cases} x - 2 &x \lt 2 \nl 3(3 - x)^2 &x \geq 2 \end{cases} \; \; \) at \(x = 2\)
  1. The numerator of \(f(x)\) is a difference of squares: \[x^2 - 16 = (x + 4)(x - 4) \period \] Thus, we rewrite \(f(x)\) as \[\frac{(x + 4)(x - 4)}{x + 4} = x - 4 \comma x \ne -4 \period\] We then see \[\lim_{x \to -4} f(x) = \lim_{x \to -4} (x - 4) = -8 \period\] Since \(f(-4)\) is undefined, \(\lim_{x \to -4} f(x) \ne f(-4) \) and so \(f(x)\) has a removable discontinuity at \(x = -4.\)
  2. The graph of \(y = 1/(x + 3)\) has a vertical asymptote of \(x = -3.\) Therefore, \(x = -3\) is an infinite discontinuity of \(f(x).\)
  3. We find \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 2^-} f(x) &= 2 - 2 \nl &= 0 \ea \] and \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 2^+} f(x) &= 3(3 - 2)^2 \nl &= 3 \period \ea \] Since these one-handed limits are not equivalent, \(\lim_{x \to 2} f(x)\) does not exist. Therefore, this discontinuity is a jump discontinuity.

Continuity over an Interval

Figure 4 shows the graph of \(y = f(x)\) over the open interval \(a \lt x \lt b.\) We say \(f\) is continuous over the over interval \((a, b)\) because \[\lim_{x \to c} f(x) = f(c)\] is satisfied by all \(c\) in \((a, b).\)

Conversely, Figure 5 shows the graph of \(y = f(x)\) over the closed interval \(a \leq x \leq b.\) We say \(f\) is continuous at the left endpoint \(x = a\) because \[\lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) = f(a) \period\] Likewise, \(f\) is continuous at the right endpoint \(x = b\) since \[\lim_{x \to b^-} f(x) = f(b) \period\] Hence, a function \(f\) is said to be continuous over the closed interval \([a, b]\) if

  • \(f\) is continuous over the open interval \((a, b),\) and
  • \(\ds \lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) = f(a)\) and \(\ds \lim_{x \to b^+} f(x) = f(b).\)

CONTINUITY OVER AN INTERVAL
If a function \(f(x)\) is continuous at every point in \([a, b],\) then \(f(x)\) is continuous over \([a, b].\) At the endpoints, \(x = a\) and \(x = b,\) \(f(x)\) is continuous from the right and left, respectively.

Continuity of Piecewise Functions

We now provide an addition to Part (iii) of Example 1. A piecewise function \[ f(x) = \bc g(x) &x \leq a \nl h(x) &x \gt a \ec \or f(x) = \bc g(x) &x \lt a \nl h(x) &x \geq a \ec \] is continuous at \(x = a\) if \[\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a) \period\] The limit \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x)\) exists if the left- and right-hand limits are equivalent—that is, if \[\lim_{x \to a^-} g(x) = \lim_{x \to a^+} h(x) \period\]

EXAMPLE 2
Let \[ f(x)= \bc kx + 4 &x \leq 3 \nl 2x - 5 &x \gt 3 \period \ec \] Find \(k\) such that \(f(x)\) is continuous at \(x = 3.\)

We see \[f(3) = 3k + 4 \comma \] the value \(\lim_{x \to 3} f(x)\) must equal if \(f(x)\) is continuous at \(x = 3.\) The limit \(\lim_{x \to 3} f(x)\) exists if its one-sided limits are equivalent—namely, if \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 3^-} f(x) &= \lim_{x \to 3^+} f(x) \nl 3k + 4 &= 6 - 5 \implies \boxed{k = -1} \ea \]

EXAMPLE 3
Let \[ g(t)= \bc t^2- 4 &t \lt a \nl -2 - t &t \geq a \period \ec \] Find \(a\) such that \(g(t)\) is continuous at \(t = a.\)
The function \(g(t)\) is continuous at \(t = a\) if \[\lim_{t \to a^-} g(t) = \lim_{t \to a^+} g(t) \period\] The left-hand expression is \((t^2 - 4),\) and the right-hand expression is \((-2 - t).\) Thus, we find \[ \ba a^2 - 4 &= -2 - a \nl a^2 + a - 2 &= 0 \nl (a - 2)(a + 1) &= 0 \comma \ea \] whose solutions are \[\boxed{a = -2} \and \boxed{a = 1}\]

Continuity of Combinations of Functions

If \(f\) and \(g\) are continuous at \(a,\) then \((f + g),\) \((f - g),\) \((f \cdot g),\) and [if \(g(a) \ne 0\)] \(f/g\) are all continuous at \(a.\)

THEOREM 1: CONTINUITY OF COMBINATIONS OF FUNCTIONS
If \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) are both continuous at \(x = a,\) then the following combinations of \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) are also continuous at \(x = a.\)
  1. \(f(x) + g(x)\)
  2. \(f(x) - g(x)\)
  3. \(f(x) \cdot g(x)\)
  4. \(f(x)/g(x) \;\) if \(\; g(a) \ne 0.\)

If \(f\) and \(g\) are functions, then composite functions of \(f\) and \(g\) are \[h(x) = f(g(x)) \and h(x) = g(f(x)) \period\] Let us denote \(h\) as either form. If \(h(x)\) is continuous at \(x = a,\) then we must have \begin{align} \lim_{x \to a} h(x) &= h(a) \nonumber \nl \lim_{x \to a} f(g(x)) &= f(g(a)) \label{eq:comp-funct-continuous} \period \end{align} The conditions to satisfy \(\eqRefer{eq:comp-funct-continuous}\) are as follows: \(g(x)\) is continuous at \(x = a\) and \(f(x)\) is continuous at \(x = g(a).\) Then \[\lim_{x \to a} f(g(x)) = f \left(\lim_{x \to a} g(x)\right) = f(g(a)) \comma \] thus showing \(h(x)\) is continuous at \(x = a.\)

THEOREM 2: CONTINUITY OF COMPOSITE FUNCTIONS
If \(g(x)\) is continuous at \(x = a\) and \(f(x)\) is continuous at \(x = g(a),\) then \(f(g(x))\) is continuous at \(x = a.\)

Many functions are continuous on their domain. For example, polynomials are continuous for all real numbers. Logarithms are defined and continuous for strictly positive inputs. Consider the example \[h(x) = \ln(x - 2) \comma \] which is in the form \(f(g(x))\) where \[f(x) = \ln x \and g(x) = x - 2 \period\] Since logarithms are only defined for positive inputs, \(\ln x\) is continuous for \(x \gt 0.\) Therefore, the nested function \(g(x)\) must follow this restriction and be greater than \(0.\) We find \[ \begin{aligned} g(x) &\gt 0 \\ x - 2 &\gt 0 \\ x &\gt 2 \period \end{aligned} \] The function \(g(x) = x - 2\) is continuous for all \(x,\) so \(\ln(x - 2)\) is continuous for all \(x \gt 2\) (Theorem 2).

EXAMPLE 4
For each of (i)–(iii), determine the interval on which \(f(x)\) is continuous.
  1. \(\ds f(x) = \frac{\cos x + x}{\ln x}\)
  2. \(\ds f(x) = \frac{\tan x}{x - 2}\)
  3. \(\ds f(x) = e^{\sqrt{3 - x}}\)
  1. The numerator is a sum of two continuous functions—\(\cos x\) and \(x\)—and is therefore continuous for all \(x.\) Conversely, the denominator \(\ln x\) is only continuous for \(x \gt 0.\) Therefore, \(f(x)\) is continuous for \(x \gt 0.\)
  2. We find the values of \(x\) such that the numerator and denominator of \(f(x)\) are both defined (and the denominator \(\ne 0\)). On this same interval we establish continuity for \(f(x)\) since \(\tan x\) and \((x - 2)\) are continuous for all \(x\) in their domains. The function \(\tan x\) is continuous for all \[ x \ne \pm \frac{\pi}{2} \comma \pm \frac{3 \pi}{2} \comma \pm \frac{5 \pi}{2} \comma \dots \period \] Also, \(x - 2 = 0\) at \(x = 2.\) Therefore, \(f(x)\) is discontinuous at \[x = -2 \and x = \frac{\pi}{2} \pm \pi n \comma n = 0, \pm 1, \pm 2, \dots \period \]
  3. The function \(e^x\) is continuous for all \(x.\) But we have \(e^{\sqrt{3 - x}},\) and \(\sqrt{3 - x}\) is continuous for \(x \leq 3.\) Thus, \(f(x)\) is continuous for all \(x \leq 3.\)
