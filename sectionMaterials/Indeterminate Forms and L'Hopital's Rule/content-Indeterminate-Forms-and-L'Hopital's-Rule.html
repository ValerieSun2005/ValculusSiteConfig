In Section , we used the methods of algebra to evaluate limits in the indeterminate form \(\indZero.\) For example, \[\lim_{x \to 3} \frac{x^2 - 9}{x - 3}\] is of the indeterminate form \(\indZero\) since both \((x^2 - 9)\) and \((x - 3)\) tend to \(0\) as \(x\) approaches \(3.\) But we factor the numerator to get \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 3} \frac{(x + 3)(x - 3)}{(x - 3)} &= \lim_{x \to 3} (x + 3) \nl &= 6 \period \ea \] Algebraic manipulation allows us to evaluate this limit, but for other limits we don't have this resource. In this section we present a systematic method to evaluate limits in indeterminate forms—L'Hôpital's Rule.

We cover seven indeterminate forms of limits. They are \(\indZero,\) \(\indInfty,\) \(0 \times \infty,\) \(\infty - \infty,\) \(0^\infty,\) \(1^\infty,\) and \(\infty^0.\) L'Hôpital's Rule applies to \(\indZero\) and \(\indInfty,\) but we manipulate the other indeterminate forms to fit these two.

L'HÔPITAL'S RULE
Let \(f\) and \(g\) be differentiable functions and \(g'(x) \ne 0\) (except possibly at \(x = a\)). If both \[\lim_{x \to a} f(x) \and \lim_{x \to a} g(x)\] equal \(0\) or \(\infty,\) then \[\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)} \period\]

In words, we differentiate the numerator and denominator of the fraction whose limit is of an indeterminate form \(\indZero\) or \(\indInfty,\) and re-evaluate the limit. The notations \(\indZero\) and \(\indInfty\) are forms to model a quotient of limits, each of which tends to \(0\) or \(\infty.\) These expressions are meaningless on their own, so it's incorrect to state that a limit equals \(\indZero\) or \(\indInfty.\) L'Hôpital's Rule also applies to one-sided limits and limits at infinity.

As an example, consider \(\lim_{x \to 0} \sin x/x.\) Using L'Hôpital's Rule, we differentiate the numerator and denominator to get \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\deriv{}{x}[\sin x]}{\deriv{}{x}[x]} &= \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\cos x}{1} \nl &= 1 \period \ea \]

It's easy to confuse the procedure of L'Hôpital's rule with the quotient rule. The procedure of L'Hôpital's rule is to differentiate the numerator and denominator separately—it is NOT the quotient rule. You may need to use L'Hôpital's rule multiple times before obtaining an answer, because the resulting limit may also be in the indeterminate form \(\indZero\) or \(\indInfty.\)

EXAMPLE 1
For each of (i)–(v), evaluate the limit if it exists.
  1. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{2x}{\sin 4x}\)
  2. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 1} \frac{\ln x}{x - 1}\)
  3. \(\ds \lim_{x \to -2} \frac{x + 2}{2 - 2 \cos \pi x}\)
  4. \(\ds \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\sqrt x}{\ln x}\)
  5. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 0} |x| \cot x\)
  1. Since \(2x\) and \(\sin 4x\) both tend to \(0\) as \(x\) approaches \(0,\) the limit \(\lim_{x \to 0} 2x/\sin 4x \) is in the indeterminate form \(\indZero.\) Both functions are differentiable at \(x = 0,\) so we apply L'Hôpital's Rule, differentiating the numerator and denominator: \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 0}\frac{2x}{\sin 4x} &= \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{ \deriv{}{x}[2x]}{ \deriv{}{x}[\sin 4x]} \nl &= \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{2}{4 \cos 4x} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{1}{2}} \ea \]
  2. The functions \(\ln x\) and \((x - 1)\) both tend to \(0\) as \(x\) approaches \(1.\) Therefore, the limit is in the indeterminate form \(\indZero.\) Since these functions are differentiable, we apply L'Hôpital's rule: \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 1}\frac{\ln x}{x - 1} &= \lim_{x \to 1} \frac{\deriv{}{x} [\ln x]}{\deriv{}{x} [x - 1]} \nl &= \lim_{x \to 1} \frac{1/x}{1} \nl &= \boxed{1} \ea \]
  3. The functions \((x + 2)\) and \((2 - 2 \cos \pi x)\) both approach \(0\) as \(x\) approaches \(-2,\) so the limit is in the indeterminate form \(\indZero.\) Both functions are differentiable, so we apply L'Hôpital's rule: \[ \ba \lim_{x \to -2} \frac{x + 2}{2 - 2 \cos \pi x} &= \lim_{x \to -2} \frac{\deriv{}{x}[x + 2]}{\deriv{}{x}[2 - 2 \cos \pi x]} \nl &= \lim_{x \to -2} \frac{1}{2 \pi \sin \pi x } \period \nl \ea \] The graph of \(y = \frac{1}{2 \pi \sin \pi x}\) has a vertical asymptote at \(x = -2,\) so the function is unbounded there. Thus, the limit does not exist.
  4. As \(x \to \infty,\) \[\sqrt x \to \infty \and \ln x \to \infty \period \] Therefore, \(\lim_{x \to \infty} \sqrt x/\ln x\) is in the indeterminate form \(\indInfty.\) And \(\sqrt{x}\) and \(\ln{x}\) are differentiable for all \(x \gt 0,\) so we apply L'Hôpital's rule: \[ \ba \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\sqrt{x}}{\ln x} &= \lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{\deriv{}{x}[\sqrt{x}]}{\deriv{}{x}[\ln x]} \nl &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\frac{1}{2\sqrt{x} }}{1/x} \nl &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\sqrt{x}}{2} \nl &= \boxed{\infty} \ea \]
  5. To consider the limit of \(|x|\) as \(x\) approaches \(0,\) we rewrite \(|x|\) as the piecewise function \[ |x|= \bc -x &x \lt 0 \nl 0 &x = 0 \nl x &x \gt 0 \period \ec \] Therefore, \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 0^-} |x| \cot x &= \lim_{x \to 0} -x \cot x \nl \lim_{x \to 0^+} |x| \cot x &= \lim_{x \to 0} x \cot x \period \ea \] The limit \(\lim_{x \to 0} |x| \cot x\) is defined if these one-sided limits are equivalent. The right-hand limit, \[ \lim_{x \to 0^+} |x| \cot x = \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{x \cos x}{\sin x} \comma \] is in the indeterminate form \(\indZero.\) Since \(x \cos x\) and \(\sin x\) are both differentiable, we use L'Hôpital's Rule to find \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{x \cos x}{\sin x} &= \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\deriv{}{x} [x \cos x]}{\deriv{}{x} [\sin x]} \nl &= \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\cos x - x \sin x}{\cos x} \nl &= 1 \period \ea \] But the left-hand limit is \[ \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{-x \cos x}{\sin x} = -1 \period \] The one-sided limits aren't equivalent, so the limit does not exist.
EXAMPLE 2
Let \(g\) be a twice-differentiable function. It is known that \(g(0) = 1,\) \(g'(0) = 0,\) and \(g''(0) = 5.\) Evaluate \[ \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{g(x) - \cos x}{x^2} \period \]

Performing direct substitution shows \[g(0) - \cos 0 = 0 \and 0^2 = 0 \comma \] so the limit is in the indeterminate form \(\indZero.\) Therefore, we apply L'Hôpital's Rule: Differentiating the numerator and denominator gives \[\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{g'(x) + \sin x}{2x} \period \] But direct substitution yields \[g'(0) - \cos 0 = 0 \and 2 \cdot 0 = 0 \period \] Since the resulting limit is still in the indeterminate form \(\indZero,\) we use L'Hôpital's Rule again to get \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{g''(x) + \cos x}{2} &= \frac{g''(0) + \cos 0}{2} \nl &= \frac{5 + 1}{2} \nl &= \boxed{3} \ea \]

Be prepared to perform multiple iterations of L'Hôpital's Rule before getting a determinate answer.

EXAMPLE 3
Let \(f(x) \gt 0\) be a function that has derivatives of all orders. Let \(k\) be a differentiable function defined by \[k(x) = \frac{4x^2 - [f(x)]^2}{\sin(x - 1)}\] that satisfies \(\ds \lim_{x \to 1} k(x) = 4.\) It is known that \(\ds \lim_{x \to 1} k(x)\) can be evaluated using L'Hôpital's Rule. Find \(f(1)\) and \(f'(1).\)

Since \(\lim_{x \to 1} k(x)\) can be evaluated using L'Hôpital's Rule, the limit must be in either of the indeterminate forms \(\indZero\) or \(\indInfty.\) The limit of the denominator is \[\lim_{x \to 1} \sin(x - 1) = 0 \comma\] so \(\lim_{x \to 1} k(x)\) is in the indeterminate form \(\indZero.\) Because \(k\) is differentiable, \(k\) is continuous and so \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 1} \left(4x^2 - [f(x)]^2 \right) = 4 - [f(1)]^2 &= 0 \nl \therefore f(1) &= 2 \period \ea \] We ignore the solution \(f(1) = -2\) because \(f\) is given to be positive. We now need \(f'(1),\) which we find by evaluating \(\lim_{x \to 1} k(x)\) using L'Hôpital's Rule. Therefore, we differentiate the numerator and denominator to find \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 1} \frac{8x - 2f(x)f'(x)}{\cos(x - 1)} &= 4 \nl \frac{8 - 2f(1) f'(1)}{\cos(1 - 1)} &= 4 \nl 8 - 4 f'(1) &= 4 \nl \therefore f'(1) &= 1 \period \ea \] Thus, \[ \boxed{ \ba f(1) &=2 \nl f'(1) &= 1 \nl \ea} \]

Relative Rates of Growth and Decay

L'Hôpital's Rule allows us to compare the relative rates of growth or decay of various functions. Consider two positive functions: \(f\) and \(g.\) To determine which function grows faster, we consider the limit \[L = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} \period \] This quotient initiates a battle between \(f\) and \(g:\) If \(f\) grows faster than does \(g,\) then \(L\) approaches infinity. But if \(g\) grows faster than does \(f,\) then \(L\) approaches \(0.\) Lastly, if \(f\) and \(g\) grow at the same rate, then \(L\) equals a nonzero constant.

For example, let's determinate which function grows faster: \(f(x) = x^{1/5}\) or \(g(x) = \ln x.\) We consider the limit \[L = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^{1/5}}{\ln x} \comma \] which is in the indeterminate form \(\indZero.\) Therefore, we apply L'Hôpital's Rule to get \[ \ba L &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\frac{1}{5} x^{-4/5}}{1/x} \nl &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1}{5}x^{1/5} \nl &= \infty \period \ea \] We conclude that \(f(x) = x^{1/5}\) grows faster than does \(g(x) = \ln x.\) As \(x\) approaches infinity, \(f(x)\) tries to force \(f(x)/g(x)\) to infinity, while \(g(x)\) tries to make the quotient decay to \(0.\) Because the quotient \(f(x)/g(x)\) approaches infinity, \(f\) overpowers \(g\) and therefore grows faster. We may also let \(f(x) = \ln x\) and \(g(x) = x^{1/5}.\) Then \(L = 0,\) also proving \(g(x) = x^{1/5}\) grows faster.

RELATIVE RATES
Let \(f\) and \(g\) be differentiable, nonzero functions. Consider \[L = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} \period \]
  1. If \(L = \infty,\) then \(f\) grows faster than does \(g.\)
  2. If \(L = 0,\) then \(g\) grows faster than does \(f.\)
  3. If \(L\) is a nonzero value, then \(f\) and \(g\) grow at the same rate.
EXAMPLE 4
As \(x\) approaches infinity, show that \(f(x) = e^{-x}\) decays to \(0\) faster than does \(g(x) = 1/x.\)

We consider \[L = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} \period \] If \(f\) decays faster than does \(g,\) then \(L = 0.\) But if \(g\) decays faster than does \(f,\) then \(L = \infty.\)

We have \[ \ba L &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{e^{-x}}{1/x} \nl &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x}{e^x} \comma \ea \] which is in the indeterminate form \(\indInfty.\) Then applying L'Hôpital's Rule gives \[ \ba \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\deriv{}{x}[x]}{\deriv{}{x}[e^x]} &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1}{e^x} \nl &= 0 \period \ea \]

Since \(L = 0,\) we conclude that \(f(x) = e^{-x}\) decays faster than does \(g(x) = 1/x.\)

We now discuss other indeterminate forms of limits: \(0 \times \infty,\) \(0^0,\) \(1^\infty,\) and \(\infty^0.\) We manipulate these forms into either \(\indZero\) or \(\indInfty\) and use L'Hôpital's Rule.

Indeterminate Form \(0 \times \infty\)

Consider the differentiable, nonzero functions \(p\) and \(q.\) If \[ \lim_{x \to a} p(x) = 0 \and \lim_{x \to a} q(x) = \infty \comma \] then \(\lim_{x \to a}[p(x) q(x)]\) is in the indeterminate form \(0 \times \infty.\) But we rewrite the limit in one of two possible ways: \[\lim_{x \to a} \frac{p(x)}{1/q(x)} \and \lim_{x \to a} \frac{q(x)}{1/p(x)} \comma \] which are in the indeterminate forms \(\indZero\) and \(\indInfty,\) respectively. This manipulation forces any limit of the indeterminate form \(0 \times \infty\) into either \(\indZero\) or \(\indInfty,\) for which we use L'Hôpital's Rule.

As an example, consider the limit \[ L = \lim_{x \to 0^+} [x \ln x] \period \] This limit represents a battle between \(x\) and \(\ln x:\) As \(x \to\) \(0^+,\) the term \(x\) tries to force the product \(x \ln x\) to \(0,\) while \(\ln x\) tries to push the product to negative infinity. If \(L = 0,\) then \(x\) overpowers \(\ln x;\) but if \(L = \infty,\) then \(\ln x \) overpowers \(x.\) We rewrite \(L\) as \[\lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{\ln x}{1/x} \comma \] which is in the indeterminate form \(\indInfty.\) We then apply L'Hôpital's Rule to obtain \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{1/x}{-1/x^2} &= \lim_{x \to 0^+} (-x) \nl &= \boxed{0} \ea \] This result concludes that as \(x \to 0^+,\) the term \(x\) overpowers \(\ln x\) and forces \(x \ln x\) to \(0.\)

INDETERMINATE FORM \(0 \times \infty\)
Let \(p\) and \(q\) be differentiable, nonzero functions. If \[L = \lim_{x \to a}[p(x) q(x)]\] is in the indeterminate form \(0 \times \infty,\) then force \(L\) into the indeterminate form \(\indZero\) or \(\indInfty\) by rewriting it as \[ \lim_{x \to a} \frac{p(x)}{1/q(x)} \or \lim_{x \to a} \frac{q(x)}{1/p(x)} \period \] Decide which form is easier to evaluate using L'Hôpital's Rule. (In some instances only one form leads to an answer.)
EXAMPLE 5
Evaluate \(\ds \lim_{x \to -\infty} xe^{x}\) if it exists.

The limit is in the indeterminate form \(0 \times \infty.\) But we rewrite the limit: \[ \lim_{x \to -\infty} xe^{x} = \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{x}{1/e^x} \comma \] which is in the indeterminate form \(\indInfty.\) Then using L'Hôpital's Rule, we obtain \[ \ba \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\deriv{}{x}[x]}{\deriv{}{x}[1/e^x]} &= \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{1}{-1/e^x} \nl &= \lim_{x \to -\infty} -e^x \nl &= \box{0} \ea \]

But if we write the quotient as \[\lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{e^x}{1/x},\] which is in the indeterminate form \(\indZero,\) then L'Hôpital's Rule gives \[\lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{e^x}{-1/x^2} \period \] Further applications of L'Hôpital's Rule then give \[\lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{e^x}{2/x^3} \comma \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{e^x}{-6/x^4} \comma \dots \comma\] leading nowhere because repeated iterations are still be in the indeterminate form \(\indZero.\) In this problem only one choice, writing the quotient as \(\indInfty,\) provides an answer.

Indeterminate Form \(\infty - \infty\)

If \[\lim_{x \to a} p(x) = \infty \and \lim_{x \to a} q(x) = \infty \comma \] then \(L = \lim_{x \to a} [p(x) - q(x)]\) To find \(L,\) we rewrite the difference \([p(x) - q(x)]\) as a quotient—by factoring, rationalizing, or combining fractions—and apply L'Hôpital's Rule.

As an example, consider the limit \[L = \lim_{x \to \infty} \Big(\ln x - \sqrt x \Big). \] As \(x\) approaches infinity, both \(\ln x\) and \(\sqrt x\) approach infinity. Thus, \(L\) is in the indeterminate form \(\infty - \infty.\) But we factor out the term \(\ln x\) to get \[L = \lim_{x \to \infty} \ln x \left(1 - \frac{\sqrt x}{\ln x} \right) \period \] As \(x \to \infty,\) you may use L'Hôpital's Rule to verify that \(\frac{\sqrt x}{\ln x} \to \infty.\) Also, as \(x \to \infty,\) \[\ln x \to \infty \and 1 - \frac{\sqrt x}{\ln x} \to - \infty \period \] The product of \(+\infty\) and \(-\infty\) is \(-\infty,\) so we conclude that \(L = \boxed{-\infty.}\)

INDETERMINATE FORM \(\infty - \infty\)
If \(p\) and \(q\) are differentiable, nonzero functions such that \[ L = \lim_{x \to a} [p(x) - q(x)] \] is in the indeterminate form \(\infty - \infty,\) then rewrite \(L\) as the limit of a quotient by factoring, rationalizing, or combining fractions. Then apply L'Hôpital's Rule if needed.
EXAMPLE 6
Evaluate \(\ds \lim_{x \to 0^+} \left(\frac{1}{x} - \ln x \right)\) if it exists.

As \(x \to 0^+,\) \[\frac{1}{x} \to \infty \and \ln x \to - \infty \period \] Therefore, this limit is in the indeterminate form \(\infty - \infty.\) We then combine \(1/x\) and \(\ln x\) as one fraction, and rewrite the limit as \[L = \lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{1 - x \ln x}{x} \period \] Then applying L'Hôpital's Rule to \(L\) produces \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{\deriv{}{x} \left[1 - x \ln x\right]}{\deriv{}{x}[x]} &= \lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{-\ln x - 1}{1} \nl &= \boxed{\infty} \ea \]

Indeterminate Forms with Exponents: \(0^0,\) \(1^\infty,\) and \(\infty^0\)

We now discuss indeterminate forms with exponents—namely \(0^0,\) \(1^\infty,\) and \(\infty^0.\) To re-express these forms into \(\indZero\) or \(\indInfty,\) we use logarithms. Let \(p\) and \(q\) be differentiable functions, and let \[y = [p(x)]^{q(x)} \period \] We take the natural logarithm of both sides to get \[\ln y = q(x) \ln[p(x)] \period \] If \(\lim_{x \to a} y\) is in the indeterminate form \(0^0,\) \(1^\infty,\) or \(\infty^0,\) then we consider \[ \ba L &= \lim_{x \to a} \ln y \nl &= \lim_{x \to a} q(x) \ln[p(x)] \period \ea \] We may need to manipulate \(L\) to be a quotient of the indeterminate form \(\indZero\) or \(\indInfty,\) as appropriate. Then \[\lim_{x \to a} y = e^L \period \]

As an example, consider \[\lim_{x \to 0^+} x^{1/x} \period \] As \(x\) approaches \(0,\) the base tends to \(0\) and the exponent tends to infinity. Thus, this limit is in the indeterminate form \(0^\infty.\) We first let \(y = x^{1/x}.\) Then we take the natural logarithm of both sides: \[ \ba \ln y &= \ln \left(x^{1/x}\right) \nl &= \frac{1}{x} \ln x \nl \lim_{x \to 0^+} \ln y &= \lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{1}{x} \ln x \period \ea \] As \(x \to 0^+,\) \[\frac{1}{x} \to \infty \and \ln x \to -\infty \period \] Since \(-\infty \times \infty = - \infty,\) we have \(\lim_{x \to 0^+} \ln y = -\infty.\) Then \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 0^+} y &= e^{\lim_{x \to 0^+} \ln y} \\ &= e^{-\infty} \\ &= \boxed{0} \ea \]

INDETERMINATE FORMS WITH EXPONENTS
Suppose \[\lim_{x \to a} [p(x)]^{q(x)}\] is in the indeterminate form \(0^0, 1^{\infty},\) or \(\infty^0.\)
  1. Let \(y = [p(x)]^{q(x)}.\) It then follows that \[\ln y = q(x) \ln[p(x)] \period \]
  2. Evaluate \[L = \lim_{x \to a} \ln y \period \] You may need to manipulate \(L\) into the indeterminate form \(\indZero\) or \(\indInfty\) and use L'Hôpital's Rule.
  3. It follows that \[\lim_{x \to a} y = e^L \period\]
EXAMPLE 7
Evaluate \[\lim_{x \to 0} \Big(3e^{2x} - 2\Big)^{1/({2x^2 - 5x})} \] if it exists.

As \(x \to 0,\) \[\par{3e^{2x} - 2} \to 1 \and \frac{1}{2x^2 - 5x} \to \infty \period\] This limit is therefore in the indeterminate form \(1^\infty.\) We let \[y = \par{3e^{2x} - 2}^{1/({2x^2 - 5x})}. \] Then \[ \ba \ln y &= \ln \left[ \left( 3e^{2x} - 2 \right)^{1/({2x^2 - 5x})} \right] \nl &= \frac{1}{2x^2 - 5x} \ln \left( 3e^{2x} - 2 \right) \nl \lim_{x \to 0} \ln y &= \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1}{2x^2 - 5x} \ln \left( 3e^{2x} - 2 \right) \period \nl \ea \] The limit \(\lim_{x \to 0} \ln y\) is in the indeterminate form \(\indZero,\) so we apply L'Hôpital's Rule to find \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 0} \ln y &= \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{6e^{2x} \cdot \dfrac{1}{3e^{2x} - 2}}{4x - 5} \nl &= -\frac{6}{5} \period \ea \]

Because \(\lim_{x \to 0} \ln y = -6/5,\) we have \[\ds \lim_{x \to 0} y = \boxed{e^{-6/5}}\]

EXAMPLE 8
Evaluate \(\ds \lim_{x \to \infty} (\ln x)^{1/x} \) if it exists.

This limit is in the indeterminate form \(\infty^0.\) We first let \[y = \left(\ln x \right)^{1/x} \period \] Then \[ \ba \ln y &= \frac{1}{x} \ln \left(\ln x \right) \nl \lim_{x \to \infty} \ln y &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1}{x} \ln \left(\ln x \right) \period \nl \ea \] Because \(\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\ln \left(\ln x \right)}{x}\) is in the indeterminate form \(\indInfty,\) we apply L'Hôpital's Rule rule to find \[ \ba \lim_{x \to \infty} \ln y &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\frac{1}{x\ln x}}{1} \nl &= 0 \period \ea \] Since \(\lim_{x \to \infty} \ln y = 0,\) we have \[\lim_{x \to \infty} y = e^0 = \boxed{1} \]
