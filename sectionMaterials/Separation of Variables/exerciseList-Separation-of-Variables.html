\(\ds \deriv{y}{x} = 3x^2 + 4\)
Performing Separation of Variables, we place all the \(y\)-terms on the left side and all the \(x\)-terms on the right. We multiply both sides by \(\dd x\) to get \[\dd y = (3x^2 + 4) \dd x \pd\] We now integrate both sides to get \[ \ba \int \di y &= \int (3x^2 + 4) \di x \nl y + C_1 &= x^3 + 4x + C_2 \pd \ea \] No reason exists to write two constants of integration, so we simply write one constant on the right-hand side: \[ y = \boxed{x^3 + 4x + C} \] where \(C = C_2 - C_1.\)
\(\ds \deriv{y}{x} = 4x^3 + 9x^2 - 8x + 4\)
Separating variables, we multiply both sides by \(\dd x\) and integrate to get \[ \ba \dd y &= (4x^3 + 9x^2 - 8x + 4) \di x \nl \int \di y &= \int (4x^3 + 9x^2 - 8x + 4) \di x \nl y + C_1 &= x^4 + 3x^2 - 4x^2 + 4x + C_2 \ea \] No reason exists to write two constants of integration, so for simplicity we let \(C = C_2 - C_1 \col\) \[y = \boxed{x^4 + 3x^2 - 4x^2 + 4x + C}\]
\(\ds y' = 2x \cma y(0) = 2\)
Performing Separation of Variables, we write \(y'\) as \(\textDeriv{y}{x}\) and multiply both sides by \(\dd x \col\) \[\dd y = 2x \di x \pd\] Integrating both sides gives \[y = x^2 + C \pd\] (We combine the constant of integration on the left with the constant on the right.) A specific solution has a value for the arbitrary constant \(C.\) We plug in the point \((0, 2) \col\) \[2 = 0^2 + C \implies C = 2 \pd\] Thus, the specific solution is \[y = \boxed{x^2 + 2}\]
\(\ds \deriv{y}{x} = 2y\)
We place all the \(y\)-terms on one side and the \(x\)-terms on the other side. Thus, on both sides we multiply \(\dd x\) and divide \(y \col\) \[\tfrac{1}{y} \di y = 2 \di x \pd\] We now integrate to attain \[\ln|y| + C_1 = 2x + C_2 \pd\] We simplify this equation by writing one constant \(C,\) \(C = C_2 - C_1,\) to get \[ \ba \ln|y| &= 2x + C \nl y &= e^{2x + C} = e^C e^{2x} \pd \ea \] Since \(C\) is an arbitrary constant, \(e^C\) is also an arbitrary constant. So we simply write \[y = \boxed{Ce^{2x}}\]
\(\ds \deriv{y}{x} = x - \sin x \)
Multiplying both sides by \(\dd x,\) we obtain \[ \dd y = (x - \sin x) \di x \pd \] Now we integrate both sides: \[ \ba \int \di y &= \int(x - \sin x) \di x \nl y &= \boxed{\tfrac{1}{2}x^2 + \cos x + C} \ea \]
\(\ds \deriv{y}{t} \, \cos y = 4t + 3 \cma y(1) = \pi\)
We multiply both sides by \(\dd t\) to segregate the variables: \[\cos y \di y = (4t + 3) \di t \pd\] Integrating both sides then gives \begin{equation} \sin y = 2t^2 + 3t + C \pd \label{eq:y'cosy} \end{equation} To solve for \(C,\) we plug in the point \((1, \pi) \col\) \[\sin \pi = 2(1)^2 + 3(1) + C \implies C = -5 \pd\] \(\eqRefer{eq:y'cosy}\) then becomes \[\sin y = 2t^2 + 3t - 5 \cma\] and we solve for \(y\) to obtain \[y = \boxed{\sin^{-1}(2t^2 + 3t - 5)}\] Alternatively, we may first solve for \(y\) in \(\eqRefer{eq:y'cosy}\) and then solve for \(C.\)
\(\ds t \, \deriv{y}{t} = 2\)
We need one side to have all the \(y\)-terms and the other side to contain all the \(t\)-terms. Thus, on both sides we divide by \(t\) and multiply by \(\dd t\) to find \[\dd y = \tfrac{2}{t} \di t \pd\] Integrating, we obtain \[ \ba \int \di y = \int \tfrac{2}{t} \di t \nl y &= \boxed{2 \ln|t| + C} \ea \]
\(t \, y' = 1 + y^2\)
We first rewrite \(y'\) as \(\textDeriv{y}{t}.\) Then on both sides we divide by \(t\) and divide by \(1 + y^2.\) This process yields \[\frac{1}{1 + y^2} \di y = \frac{1}{t} \di t \pd\] Integrating both sides gives \[ \ba \tan^{-1} y &= \ln|t| + C \nl y &= \boxed{\tan \par{\ln|t| + C}} \ea \]
\(\ds y \, y' \sec x = 2\)
We rewrite \(y'\) as \(\textDeriv{y}{x}.\) To separate the \(x\)- and \(y\)-terms, on each side we divide by \(y\) and multiply by \(\sec x \di x.\) We then get \[y \di y = 2 \cos x \pd\] Integrating both sides gives \[ \ba \tfrac{1}{2} y^2 &= 2 \sin x + C \nl y &= \boxed{2 \sqrt{\sin x + C}} \ea \]
\(\ds x^2 \, y' = y + 3\)
Treating \(y'\) as \(\textDeriv{y}{x},\) we isolate the \(x\)- and \(y\)-terms to find \[ \ba \frac{1}{y + 3} \di y &= \frac{1}{x^2} \di x \nl \ln|y + 3| &= -\frac{1}{x} + C \pd \ea \] To solve for \(y,\) we raise both sides to the power of \(e \col\) \[ \ba y + 3 &= e^{-1/x + C} \nl y &= \boxed{Ce^{-1/x} - 3} \ea \]
\(\ds y \, \deriv{y}{t} = t \, \sqrt{1 - y^2}\)
We divide both sides by \(\sqrt{1 - y^2}\) and multiply both sides by \(\dd t \col\) \begin{equation} \frac{y}{\sqrt{1 - y^2}} \di y = t \di t \pd \label{eq:yy'} \end{equation} We now integrate both sides. For \(\int (y/\sqrt{1 - y^2}) \di y,\) we substitute \[ \ba u &= 1 - y^2 \nl \dd u &= -2y \di y \pd \ea \] The integral then becomes \[-\frac{1}{2} \int \frac{1}{\sqrt{u}} \di u = - \sqrt{u} = -\sqrt{1 - y^2} \pd \] (We neglect the constant of integration.) \(\eqRefer{eq:yy'}\) then becomes \[-\sqrt{1 - y^2} = \tfrac{1}{2} t^2 + C \pd\] Now we solve for \(y\) as follows: \[ \ba 1 - y^2 &= \par{\tfrac{1}{2} t^2 + C}^2 \nl y &= \boxed{\pm\sqrt{1 - \par{\tfrac{1}{2} t^2 + C}^2}} \ea \]
By substituting \(y' = u,\) show that a general solution to \[y'' + y' = 0\] is \(y = C_1 e^{-x} + C_2.\)
The substitution \(y' = u\) gives \[u' + u = 0 \cma\] which we rewrite as a separable first-order differential equation: \[\deriv{u}{x} = -u \pd\] On both sides we multiply by \(\dd x\) and divide by \(u.\) We get \[ \ba \tfrac{1}{u} \di u &= - \di x \nl \ln|u| &= -x + C_1 \nl u &= C_1 e^{-x} \pd \ea \] Remember that \(u = y',\) so we substitute back to find \[ \ba \deriv{y}{x} &= C_1 e^{-x} \nl \int \di y &= \int C_1 e^{-x} \di x \nl y &= -C_1 e^{-x} + C_2 \pd \ea \] A final remark is that \(-C_1\) is equivalent to \(C_1\) because they are both arbitrary constants. We therefore make this change to obtain \[y = \boxed{C_1 e^{-x} + C_2}\] Just as solving a first-order differential equation confers one constant of integration, the solution of a second-order differential equation has two arbitrary constants.
A capacitor is a device that stores electrical energy on one of its conducting plates. Capacitance is the ability of a capacitor's plate to hold a charge \(Q.\) The apparatus of Figure 1 shows a capacitor of capacitance \(C\) connected to an appliance of resistance \(R.\) (This configuration is called an RC circuit.) As current travels through the circuit, the resistance dissipates the charge of the capacitor as modeled by the differential equation \[\frac{Q}{C} + R \frac{\dd Q}{\dd t} = 0 \pd \] Initially, the capacitor's plate holds charge \(Q_0.\) Find the specific solution \(Q(t),\) the function that represents the charge on the capacitor at a given time \(t.\)
We first isolate \(\textDeriv{Q}{t} \col\) \[\deriv{Q}{t} = -\frac{Q}{RC} \pd\] Now we perform Separation of Variables, placing all the \(Q\) terms on the left-hand side: \[ \ba \frac{1}{Q} \di Q &= -\frac{1}{RC} \di t \nl \ln|Q| &= -\frac{t}{RC} + K \nl \ea \] where \(K\) is the constant of integration. Raising both sides to the power of \(e\) yields \[Q = e^{(-t/RC) + K} = Ke^{-t/RC} \pd\] We now solve for \(K\) by noting that when \(t = 0,\) \(Q(t) = Q_0.\) So we get \[ Q_0 = Ke^{0} \implies K = Q_0 \pd \] Therefore, the solution becomes \[Q(t) = \boxed{Q_0 e^{-t/RC}}\] This model demonstrates that the charge on the capacitor's plate decreases exponentially with time.
Water flows out of a cylindrical tank of water level \(H\) (Figure 2). Torricelli’s Law states that the water level decreases with time at a rate proportional to the square root of the water level, as modeled by the differential equation \[\deriv{H}{t} = -k \sqrt{H} \pd\] Initially, the water level in the tank is \(16 \un{ft}.\) After \(10\) minutes, the water level is \(9 \un{ft}.\) Find the specific solution \(H(t),\) the function that represents the water level in the tank at time \(t.\)
Let's first find the general solution \(H(t).\) We isolate the variables, getting \begin{align} H^{-1/2} \di H &= -k \di t \nonumber \nl \tfrac{1}{2} \sqrt{H} &= -kt + C \pd \label{eq:tank-1} \end{align} We now solve for \(k\) and \(C \col\) When \(t = 0,\) \(H = 16.\) Thus, we get \[\tfrac{1}{2} \cdot \sqrt{16} = 0 + C \implies C = 2 \pd\] When \(t = 10,\) \(H = 9.\) We then find \[\tfrac{1}{2} \cdot \sqrt{9} = -10k + 2 \implies k = -\tfrac{1}{20} \pd \] Therefore, \(\eqRefer{eq:tank-1}\) becomes \[ \ba \tfrac{1}{2} \sqrt{H} &= -\tfrac{1}{20} t + 2 \nl H &= \boxed{\par{4 - \tfrac{1}{10}t}^2} \ea \]
