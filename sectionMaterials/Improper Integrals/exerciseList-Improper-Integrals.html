determine whether the integral is ordinary or improper. If it is improper, explain why

\(\ds \int_1^\infty \frac{1}{x^3} \di x\)
The upper bound is infinity, so we have a Type I improper integral.
\(\ds \int_1^3 \frac{2}{x - 3} \di x\)
The graph of \(y = 2/(x - 3)\) has a vertical asymptote at \(x = 3,\) the upper bound of the integral. Thus, the integral is a Type II improper integral.
\(\ds \int_0^4 \frac{1}{t - 5} \di t\)
The integral is not improper. The integral's bounds are finite, and \(1/(t - 5)\) is continuous over \(0 \leq t \leq 4.\)
\(\ds \int_0^2 \frac{1}{x^2 - 1} \di x\)
The graph of \(y = 1/(x^2 - 1)\) is undefined at \(x = 1.\) Thus, the integrand is not continuous on the interval \([0, 2]\) and so we have a Type II improper integral.

3

evaluate the integral or justify that it diverges

\(\ds \int_2^\infty \frac{1}{x + 9} \di x\)
We have a Type I improper integral with an upper bound of infinity. Therefore, we replace the upper bound with a variable \(k\) and consider \(k \to \infty.\) We then get \[ \ba \int_2^\infty \frac{1}{x + 9} \di x &= \lim_{k \to \infty} \int_2^k \frac{1}{x + 9} \di x \nl &= \lim_{k \to \infty} \ln \abs{x + 9} \Big|_2^k \nl &= \lim_{k \to \infty} \ln \abs{k + 9} - \ln 11 \period \ea \] The limit \(\lim_{k \to \infty} \ln(k + 9)\) approaches \(\infty\) and therefore does not exist, so \(\int_2^\infty \frac{1}{x + 9} \di x\) diverges.
\(\ds \int_1^\infty \frac{3}{2\sqrt{x}} \di x\)
This integral is a Type I improper integral. We replace the \(\infty\) with a variable \(k\) that approaches \(\infty.\) Also, we rewrite \(1/\sqrt{x}\) as \(x^{-1/2}.\) We then obtain \[ \ba \lim_{k \to \infty} \int_1^k \frac{3}{2} x^{-1/2} \di x &= \lim_{k \to \infty} 3 \sqrt{x} \Big|_1^k \nl &= \lim_{k \to \infty} 3 \sqrt{k} - 3 \period \ea \] The limit \(\lim_{k \to \infty} 3 \sqrt{k}\) doesn't exist, so the integral diverges.
\(\ds \int_3^\infty \frac{1}{x\sqrt[4]{x}} \di x\)
The denominator of the integrand is \(x \cdot x^{1/4} = x^{5/4}.\) Moreover, we replace the upper bound \(\infty\) with \(k.\) We have \[ \ba \lim_{k \to \infty} \int_3^k x^{-5/4} \di x &= \lim_{k \to \infty} -4 x^{-1/4} \Big|_3^k \nl &= \lim_{k \to \infty} -4k^{-1/4} + 4(3)^{-1/4} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{4}{\sqrt[4]{3}}} \ea \]
\(\ds \int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{1}{x^2 + 1} \di x\)
We must first establish that \(1/(x^2 + 1)\) is continuous over the entire interval \((-\infty, \infty).\) And it is, because the denominator \(x^2 + 1\) is never zero. We choose to break the integral at any point—say, \(x = 0.\) (Any point suffices, however.) This choice gives \[\int_{-\infty}^0 \frac{1}{x^2 + 1} \di x + \int_0^\infty \frac{1}{x^2 + 1} \di x \period\] Both integrals must converge; otherwise, \( \int_{-\infty}^\infty \dd x/(x^2 + 1)\) diverges. Recall that \(\int \dd x/(x^2 + 1) = \tan^{-1} x + C.\) Also note that \(\tan 0 = 0\) and \(\lim_{x \to \infty} \tan^{-1} x = \pi/2.\) Using this information, the first integral becomes \[\lim_{a \to -\infty} \int_a^0 \frac{1}{x^2 + 1} \di x = \lim_{a \to -\infty} \tan^{-1} x \Big|_a^0 = \frac{\pi}{2} \period \] The second integral is \[\lim_{b \to \infty} \int_0^b \frac{1}{x^2 + 1} \di x = \lim_{b \to \infty} \tan^{-1} x \Big|_0^b = \frac{\pi}{2} \period\] Therefore, \[\int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{1}{x^2 + 1} \di x = \frac{\pi}{2} + \frac{\pi}{2} = \boxed{\pi}\]
\(\ds \int_{-\infty}^{-1} \frac{1}{x^2} \di x\)
We rewrite the integral as the limit \[\lim_{k \to -\infty} \int_k^{-1} \frac{1}{x^2} \di x \period\] We antidifferentiate to find \[ \lim_{k \to -\infty} -\frac{1}{x} \Big|_k^{-1} = \lim_{k \to -\infty} 1 + \frac{1}{k} = \boxed{1} \]
\(\ds \int_0^\infty \sin x \di x\)
The graph of \(y = \sin x\) oscillates continuously. Thus, the area bounded by the graph and the \(x\)-axis never settles to a final value. As a result, the integral diverges.
\(\ds \int_2^5 \frac{1}{x - 4} \di x\)
Be careful—this integral is improper! The graph of \(y = 1/(x - 4)\) has a vertical asymptote at \(x = 4\), so we break the integral as \[\lim_{k \to 4^-} \int_2^k \frac{1}{x - 4} \di x + \lim_{k \to 4^+} \int_k^5 \frac{1}{x - 4} \di x \period \] The integral \(\int_2^5 \di x/(x - 4)\) converges if both limits exist. We therefore verify that each limit exists: The left limit becomes \[\lim_{k \to 4^-} \ln \abs{x - 4} \Big|_{2}^k = \lim_{k \to 4^-} \ln \abs{k - 4} - \ln 2 \period\] Because \(\lim_{k \to 4^-} \ln \abs{k - 4}\) does not exist, \(\int_2^5 \di x/(x - 4)\) diverges.
\(\ds \int_0^\infty \frac{1}{(t + 2)^{3/2}} \di t\)
We replace the upper bound \(\infty\) with \(k.\) We find \[ \ba \lim_{k \to \infty} \int_0^k (t + 2)^{-3/2} \di t &= \lim_{k \to \infty} -2(t + 2)^{-1/2} \Big|_0^k \nl &= \lim_{k \to \infty} -2(k + 2)^{-1/2} + 2(2)^{-1/2} \nl &= \boxed{\sqrt{2}} \ea \]
\(\ds \int_0^1 \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \di x\)
The function \(1/\sqrt{1 - x^2}\) is undefined at \(x = 1.\) Therefore, we write \[ \ba \lim_{k \to 1^-} \int_0^k \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \di x &= \lim_{k \to 1^-} \sin^{-1} x \Big|_0^k \nl &= \lim_{k \to 1^-} \sin^{-1} k - \sin^{-1} 0 \nl &= \boxed{\frac{\pi}{2}} \ea \]
\(\ds \int_{-2}^4 \frac{1}{(x - 1)^4} \di x\)
The graph of \(y = 1/(x - 4)^4\) has a vertical asymptote at \(x = 1.\) Thus, we break the integral into \[\lim_{k \to 1^-} \int_{-2}^k \frac{1}{(x - 1)^4} \di x + \lim_{k \to 1^+} \int_k^4 \frac{1}{(x - 1)^4} \di x \period \] Both limits must exist; otherwise, \(\int_{-2}^4 \di x/(x - 1)^4\) diverges. The first limit is \[\lim_{k \to 1^-} \frac{-1}{3(x - 1)^3} \Big|_{-2}^k = \lim_{k \to 1^-} \frac{-1}{3(k - 1)^3} - \frac{1}{81} \period \] Because \(\lim_{k \to 1^-} \frac{-1}{3(k - 1)^3}\) does not exist, \(\int_{-2}^4 \di x/(x - 1)^4\) diverges.
\(\ds \int_{0}^{\pi/2} \sec \theta \tan \theta \di \theta\)
The integrand has an infinite discontinuity at \(\theta = \pi/2.\) Thus, we consider \[\lim_{k \to (\pi/2)^-} \int_0^{k} \sec \theta \tan \theta \di \theta \pd \] Recall that \(\int \sec \theta \tan \theta \di \theta = \sec \theta + C.\) We therefore get \[ \lim_{k \to (\pi/2)^-} \sec \theta \Big|_0^k = \lim_{k \to (\pi/2)^-} \sec k - \sec 0 \pd \] Because \(\lim_{k \to (\pi/2)^-} \sec k\) does not exist, \(\int_{0}^{\pi/2} \sec \theta \tan \theta \di \theta\) diverges.
\(\ds \int_4^\infty \frac{3}{x \ln x} \di x\)
We substitute \[ \ba u &= \ln x \nl \dd u &= \frac{1}{x} \di x \period \ea \] Now we change the bounds: when \(x = 4,\) \(u = \ln 4;\) when \(x = \infty,\) \(u = \infty.\) Thus, the integral becomes \[\int_{\ln 4}^\infty \frac{3}{u} \di u \pd\] We consider \[ \ba \lim_{k \to \infty} \int_{\ln 4}^k \frac{3}{u} \di u &= \lim_{k \to \infty} 3 \ln \abs{u} \Big|_{\ln 4}^k \nl &= \lim_{k \to \infty} 3 \ln k - 3 \ln(\ln 4) \pd \ea \] Because \(\lim_{k \to \infty} 3 \ln k\) does not exist, \(\int_4^\infty \frac{3}{x \ln x} \di x\) diverges.
\(\ds \int_0^1 t \ln t \di t\)
The integrand is undefined at \(t = 0.\) Thus, we write \[\lim_{k \to 0^+} \int_k^1 t \ln t \di t \period\] We integrate by parts using \[ \baat{2} u &= \ln t \lspace \dd v &&= t \di t \nl \dd u &= \tfrac{1}{t} \di t \lspace v &&= \tfrac{1}{2} t^2 \period \eaat \] Thus, our antiderivative is \[ \ba uv - \int v \di u &= \frac{t^2}{2} \ln t - \int \frac{1}{2} t^2 \cdot \frac{1}{t} \di t \nl &= \frac{t^2}{2} \ln t - \frac{t^2}{4} + C \period \ea \] Consequently, the improper integral becomes \[ \ba \lim_{k \to 0^+} \par{\frac{t^2}{2} \ln t - \frac{t^2}{4}} \Big|_k^1 &= \par{0 - \frac{1}{4}} - \lim_{k \to 0^+} \par{\frac{k^2}{2} \ln k - \frac{k^2}{4}} \nl &= -\frac{1}{4} - \lim_{k \to 0^+} \frac{k^2}{2} \ln k \period \ea \] The limit \(\lim_{k \to 0^+} \frac{k^2}{2} \ln k\) is in the indeterminate form \(0 \times \infty,\) so we need L'Hôpital's Rule. We rewrite the expression as \[\lim_{k \to 0^+} \frac{\ln k}{2/k^2} \comma\] which is in the indeterminate form \(\indInfty.\) After differentiating, we obtain \[\lim_{k \to 0^+} \frac{1/k}{-4/k^3} = \lim_{k \to 0^+} \frac{-k^2}{4} = 0 \period\] Therefore, \[\int_0^1 t \ln t \di t = \boxed{-\frac{1}{4}}\]
\(\ds \int_0^\infty xe^{-x^2} \di x\)
We substitute \[ \ba u &= -x^2 \nl \dd u &= -2x \di x \period \ea \] When \(x = 0,\) \(u = 0;\) when \(x = \infty,\) \(u = -\infty.\) Thus, we have \[-\frac{1}{2} \int_0^{-\infty} e^u \di u = \frac{1}{2} \int_{-\infty}^0 e^u \di u \period\] We now replace the lower bound \(-\infty\) with a variable \(k.\) Doing so gives \[ \ba \lim_{k \to -\infty} \frac{1}{2} \int_k^0 e^u \di u &= \lim_{k \to -\infty} \frac{1}{2} e^u \Big|_k^0 \nl &= \frac{1}{2} e^0 - \frac{1}{2} \lim_{k \to -\infty} e^k \nl &= \boxed{\frac{1}{2}} \ea \]

find the values of \(n\) such that the integral converges

\(\ds \int_{1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{x^{1 - 3n}} \di x\)
The integral takes the form \(\int_1^\infty (1/x^p) \di x\) with \(p = 1 - 3n.\) If \(p \gt 1,\) then the integral converges. Thus, we find \[1 - 3n \gt 1 \implies \boxed{n \lt 0}\]
\(\ds \int_{2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{x^{5n - 1}} \di x\)
The integral is a \(p\)-integral with \(p = 5n - 1.\) If \(p \gt 1,\) then the integral converges. Thus, we find \[5n - 1 \gt 1 \implies \boxed{n \gt \frac{2}{5}}\]
\(\ds \int_{2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{x^{n^2 - 4n + 4}} \di x\)
We equate the exponent of \(x\) to be greater than \(1.\) We see \[ \ba n^2 - 4n + 4 &\gt 1 \nl n^2 - 4n + 3 &\gt 0 \nl (n - 3)(n - 1) &\gt 0 \period \ea \] The solutions to this inequality are \[\boxed{1 \lt n \lt 3}\]
Let \(f\) be a continuous function such that \(0 \leq f(x) \leq 1/x^{4/3}.\) Show that \(\int_1^\infty f(x) \di x\) converges and is no greater than \(3.\)
From the given information, we deduce that \[0 \leq \int_1^\infty f(x) \di x \leq \int_1^\infty \frac{1}{x^{4/3}} \di x \pd \] We evaluate the right-hand integral, as follows: \[ \ba \int_1^\infty \frac{1}{x^{4/3}} \di x &= \lim_{k \to \infty} \int_1^k x^{-4/3} \di x \nl &= \lim_{k \to \infty} -3 x^{-1/3} \Big|_1^k \nl &= \lim_{k \to \infty} -3 k^{-1/3} + 3 \nl &= 3 \period \ea \] Thus, \[0 \leq \int_1^\infty f(x) \di x \leq 3 \pd \]
It is known that \(0 \leq 1/x^{5/9} \leq g(x),\) where \(g\) is a continuous function. Show that \(\int_1^\infty g(x) \di x\) is divergent.
Since \[\ds 0 \leq \frac{1}{x^{5/9}} \leq g(x) \comma \] it follows that \[0 \leq \int_1^\infty \frac{1}{x^{5/9}} \di x \leq \int_1^\infty g(x) \di x \pd \] The integral \(\int_1^\infty (1/x^{5/9}) \di x\) diverges because \(p = 5/9 \leq 1.\) Therefore, using the Comparison Test, \(\int_1^\infty g(x) \di x\) also diverges.
Let \(g\) be a differentiable function that satisfies \(g(0) = 0\) and \(g(4) = 12.\) Evaluate \(\int_0^4 [g'(x)/g(x)] \di x\) or show that it diverges.
We substitute \(u = g(x);\) then \(\dd u = g'(x) \di x.\) When \(x = 0,\) \(u = g(0) = 0.\) When \(x = 4,\) \(u = g(4) = 12.\) Thus, the integral becomes \[\int_0^{12} \frac{1}{u} \di u \pd \] The integrand is undefined at \(u = 0,\) so we consider \[ \ba \lim_{k \to 0^+} \int_k^{12} \frac{1}{u} \di u &= \lim_{k \to 0^+} \ln u \Big|_k^{12} \nl &= \ln 12 - \lim_{k \to 0^+} \ln k \pd \ea \] Because \(\lim_{k \to 0^+} \ln k\) does not exist, \(\int_0^4 [g'(x)/g(x)] \di x\) diverges.

If \(f(x)\) is continuous, positive, and decreasing for all \(x \geq 1,\) and \(\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = 0,\) then must \(\int_1^\infty f(x) \di x\) be convergent?
No, the integral could diverge. For example, \(f(x) = 1/x\) is continuous, positive, and decreasing for all \(x \geq 1,\) and it satisfies \(\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = 0.\) However, \(\int_1^\infty (1/x) \di x\) diverges.
The rate at which people like a social media post is given by \(S(t) = 200e^{-t/10},\) where \(t\) is time in hours. How many likes will the post receive?
The total number of likes is \(\int_0^\infty S(t) \di t.\) We find \[ \ba \lim_{k \to \infty} \int_0^k 200e^{-t/10} \di t &= -2000 \lim_{k \to \infty} e^{-t/10} \Big]_0^k \nl &= -2000 \lim_{k \to \infty} e^{-k/10} + 2000 e^{0} \nl &= 2000 \period \ea \] Thus, the post will receive \(2000\) likes.
For \(t \gt 1,\) a particle moves indefinitely along a line at a velocity given by \(v(t) = 2/t^{2/3},\) where \(v(t)\) is measured in meters per second and \(t\) is measured in seconds. Show that the particle travels an infinite distance.
The distance traveled by the particle is \(\int_1^\infty \abs{v(t)} \di t.\) Since \(v(t) \gt 0,\) we drop the absolute value bars and get \[\int_1^\infty \frac{44}{t^{2/3}} \di t \pd \] If this improper integral converges, then the particle will cover a finite distance; if it approaches \(\infty,\) then the particle will travel an infinite distance. We get \[ \ba \lim_{k \to \infty} \int_1^k 2 t^{-2/3} \di t &= \lim_{k \to \infty} 6t^{1/3} \Big|_1^k \nl &= \lim_{k \to \infty} 6k^{1/3} - 6 \nl &= \infty \pd \ea \] Because the improper integral approaches \(\infty,\) the particle travels an infinite distance.
The density of trees in a forest surrounding a campsite is modeled by \[T(r) = \frac{290}{r^3 + 8r^2 + 16r} \comma \] where \(r\) is the distance, in meters, from the campsite and \(T(r)\) is measured in trees per square meter. The number of trees within \(K\) meters of the campsite is given by \(\int_0^K 2 \pi r \, T(r) \di r.\) Explain why the total number of trees in the forest is less than \(700.\)
We take \(K \to \infty\) in order to cover the entire forest. The integral \(\int_0^\infty 2 \pi r T(r) \di r\) is an upper bound to how many trees are truly in the forest. Thus, we get \[ \ba 2 \pi \lim_{K \to \infty} \int_0^K \frac{290}{r^2 + 8r + 16} \di r &= 580 \pi \lim_{K \to \infty} \int_0^K \frac{1}{(r + 4)^2} \di r \nl &= 580 \pi \lim_{K \to \infty} \frac{-1}{r + 4} \Big|_0^k \nl &= 580 \pi \lim_{K \to \infty} \frac{-1}{K + 4} - \frac{580 \pi}{4} \nl &= 145 \pi \lt 700 \pd \ea \] Thus, the maximum number of trees in the forest is \(145 \pi,\) which is less than \(700.\)
