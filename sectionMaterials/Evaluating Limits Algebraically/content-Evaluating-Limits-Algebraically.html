This section covers how to evaluate limits in the indeterminate forms \(\indZero\) and \(\indInfty.\) We discuss how to evaluate Limits of Rational Expressions at Infinity. We then learn the methods of Factoring, Rationalizing, and using trigonometric identities.

Limits of Rational Expressions at Infinity

Consider the rational function \[f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 4x + 9}{x^3 - 2} \period \] To find \(\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x),\) we divide the numerator and denominator by the highest-degree term of \(f(x)\)—namely, \(x^3,\) found in the denominator. We then get \[ \ba \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^2 - 4x + 9}{x^3 - 2} &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^2 - 4x + 9}{x^3 - 2} \cdot \frac{1/x^3}{1/x^3} \nl &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1/x - 4/x^2 + 9/x^3}{1 - 2/x^3} \nl &= 0 \period \ea \] Consequently, as \(x\) approaches infinity, \(f(x)\) approaches \(0.\) This result is intuitive since the graph of \(y = f(x)\) has the horizontal asymptote \(y = 0.\)

But we have a shortcut: As \(x\) grows infinitely large, only the highest-degree terms in the numerator and denominator are significant. In this case, as \(x\) grows to infinity, the behavior of \(f(x)\) matches the behavior of \(x^2/x^3 = 1/x,\) which decays to \(0.\) Therefore, \[\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^2 - 4x + 9}{x^3 - 2} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^2}{x^3} = 0 \period\] Thus, the limit of a rational function at infinity matches the function's end behavior.

LIMITS OF RATIONAL EXPRESSIONS

To determine the limit of a rational expression, take the limit of the highest-degree terms in the numerator and denominator.

EXAMPLE 1
For each of (i)–(iii), evaluate the limit.
  1. \(\ds \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{3x^3 - 4x^2 + 16}{5x^3 + 8x - 2} \)
  2. \(\ds \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{-8x^2 + \frac{3}{4}x + 16}{5x - 2}\)
  3. \(\ds \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{8 + 3x^2 - x^9}{10x^9} \)
  1. We consider only the highest-degree terms on the numerator and denominator. We then get \[ \ba \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{3x^3 - 4x^2 + 16}{5x^3 + 8x - 2} &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{3x^3}{5x^3} \nl &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{3}{5} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{3}{5}} \ea \]
  2. The highest-degree term on the numerator is \(-8x^2,\) while the highest-degree term on the denominator is \(5x.\) Therefore, \[ \ba \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{-8x^2 + \frac{3}{4}x + 16}{5x - 2} &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{-8x^2}{5x} \nl &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{-8x}{5} \nl &= \boxed{- \infty} \ea \] (Since the limit is unbounded, it's also correct to say that the limit does not exist.)
  3. We ignore the smaller-degree terms on the numerator and denominator. We then get \[ \ba \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{8 + 3x^2 - x^9}{10x^9} &= \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{-x^9}{10x^9} \nl &= \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{-1}{10} \nl &= \boxed{-\frac{1}{10}} \ea \]

Let's define an indeterminate form. An limit is of an indeterminate form if direct substitution doesn't yield an answer. For example, consider the limit \[L = \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{x^2}{x} \period \] If we try a direct substitution (that is, we plug \(x = 0\) into \(x^2/x\)), then we get \(\indZero,\) a meaningless expression. So we say \(L\) is of an indeterminate form. This section focuses on two indeterminate forms: \(\indZero\) and \(\indInfty.\)

INDETERMINATE FORMS
A limit whose value cannot be determined by direct substitution is of an indeterminate form. Two indeterminate forms are \(\indZero\) and \(\indInfty.\)

Canceling Factors

The primary tool we use to evaluate indeterminate limits is exposing common factors. As an example, consider the function \[f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 25}{x + 5} \period \] When evaluating \(\lim_{x \to -5} f(x),\) a direct substitution gives the indeterminate form \(\indZero.\) But the numerator of \(f(x)\) is a difference of squares: \[x^2 - 25 = (x + 5)(x - 5) \period\] Thus, \[ \ba \lim_{x \to -5} \frac{x^2 - 25}{x + 5} &= \lim_{x \to -5} \frac{(x + 5)(x - 5)}{x + 5} \nl &= \lim_{x \to -5} (x - 5) \nl &= \boxed{-10} \ea \]

The graph of \(y = f(x)\) is continuous for all \(x\) except at \(x = -5,\) the location of a removable discontinuity (Figure 1). When we cancel the common factor \((x + 5),\) we transform \(f(x)\) into an equivalent function whose limit as \(x \to 5\) may be found. This value is \(\lim_{x \to -5} f(x)\) \(= -10,\) the \(y\)-value of the removable discontinuity.

We work with differences of squares, differences of cubes, and sums of cubes. Remember the following formulas: \begin{align} a^2 - b^2 &= (a + b)(a - b) \label{eq:diff-of-squares} \nl a^3 - b^3 &= (a - b)(a^2 + ab + b^2) \label{eq:diff-of-cubes} \nl a^3 + b^3 &= (a + b)(a^2 - ab + b^2) \period \label{eq:sum-of-cubes} \end{align}

EXAMPLE 2
For each of (i)–(iii), evaluate the limit.
  1. \(\ds \lim_{x \to -2} \frac{x^2 - 4}{x + 2} \)
  2. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 4} \frac{x^3 - 64}{x - 4} \)
  3. \(\ds \lim_{x \to -3} \frac{x^3 + 27}{x + 3} \)
  1. Trying direct substitution immediately gives the indeterminate form \(\indZero,\) so we try to find any common factors. The numerator is a difference of squares, \(x^2 - 2^2,\) so we use \(\eqRefer{eq:diff-of-squares}\) to get \[x^2 - 4 = (x + 2)(x - 2) \period \] Re-expressing the numerator and canceling out the common factor \((x + 2)\) provide \[ \ba \lim_{x \to -2} \frac{(x + 2)(x - 2)}{x + 2} &= \lim_{x \to -2} (x - 2) \nl &= \boxed{4} \ea \]
  2. An immediate direct substitution yields the form \(\indZero,\) so we try to expose common factors. Since the numerator is a difference of cubes, \(x^3 - 4^3,\) we use \(\eqRefer{eq:diff-of-cubes}\) to re-express the numerator as \[ (x - 4)(x^2 + 4x + 16) \period \] Then we get \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 4} \frac{(x^3 - 64)}{x - 4} &= \lim_{x \to 4} \frac{(x - 4)(x^2 + 4x + 16)}{x - 4} \nl &= \lim_{x \to 4} (x^2 + 4x + 16) \nl &= \boxed{48} \ea \]
  3. Since the limit is in the indeterminate form \(\indZero,\) our goal is to expose any common factors. Using \(\eqRefer{eq:sum-of-cubes},\) we rewrite the numerator as \[(x + 3)(x^2 - 3x + 9) \period \] The limit then becomes \[ \ba \lim_{x \to -3} \frac{(x + 3)(x^2 - 3x + 9 )}{x + 3} &= \lim_{x \to -3} (x^2 - 3x + 9) \nl &= \boxed{27} \ea \]

Rationalization

A limit in an indeterminate form may have no exposed common factors. But if the expression has a radical, then rationalization may expose a common factor. As an example, consider \[g(x) = \frac{\sqrt{2x + 5} - 1}{x + 2} \period \] The limit \(\lim_{x \to -2} g(x)\) is in the indeterminate form \(\indZero.\) The function \(g(x)\) has a radical in the numerator, so we try to rationalize it: We multiply the numerator and denominator each by the conjugate of \((\sqrt{2x + 5} - 1),\) which is \((\sqrt{2x + 5} + 1).\) Doing so yields \[ \ba g(x) &= \frac{\sqrt{2x + 5} - 1}{x + 2} \nl &= \frac{\sqrt{2x + 5} - 1}{x + 2} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{2x + 5} + 1}{\sqrt{2x + 5} + 1} \nl &= \frac{(2x + 5) - 1}{(x + 2) \left(\sqrt{2x + 5} + 1\right)} \nl &= \frac{2(x + 2)}{(x + 2) \left(\sqrt{2x + 5} + 1\right)} \nl &= \frac{2}{\sqrt{2x + 5} + 1} \comma x \ne -2. \ea \] We cleared the common factor \((x + 2)\) in the numerator and denominator, so \[ \ba \lim_{x \to -2} g(x) &= \lim_{x \to -2} \frac{2}{\sqrt{2x + 5} + 1} \nl &= \boxed{1} \ea \] Rationalizing and canceling the common factor \((x + 2)\) express \(g(x)\) as an equivalent function that returns values for all \(x \gt -5/2.\) The graph of \(y = g(x)\) is smooth for all \(x \gt -5/2\) but has a hole at \(x = -2.\) But our new function provides the \(y\)-value of the hole, and this value is \(\lim_{x \to -2} g(x).\)

EXAMPLE 3
For each of (i) and (ii), evaluate the limit.
  1. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 9} \frac{3x - 27}{\sqrt{x - 8} - 1} \)
  2. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 4} \frac{x^3 - 64}{x - 4} \)
  1. The limit is in the indeterminate form \(\indZero.\) The denominator has a radical, so we try to rationalize the denominator. The conjugate of the denominator is \((\sqrt{x - 8} - 1),\) and we multiply the numerator and denominator each by this factor to get \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 9} \frac{3x - 27}{\sqrt{x - 8} + 1} &= \lim_{x \to 9} \frac{3x - 27}{\sqrt{x - 8} + 1} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{x - 8} - 1}{\sqrt{x - 8} - 1} \nl &= \lim_{x \to 9} \frac{(3x - 27)(\sqrt{x - 8} - 1)}{(x - 8) - 1} \nl &= \lim_{x \to 9} \frac{3(x - 9)(\sqrt{x - 8} - 1)}{x - 9} \nl &= \lim_{x \to 9} 3 \left(\sqrt{x - 8} - 1\right) \nl &= \boxed{0} \ea \]
  2. This limit is in the indeterminate form \(\indZero.\) The numerator has a radical, so we attempt to rationalize the numerator. We do so multiplying the numerator and denominator by \((\sqrt{4x - 5} + 2),\) obtaining \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 9/4} \frac{\sqrt{4x - 5} - 2}{16x - 36} &= \lim_{x \to 9/4} \frac{\sqrt{4x - 5} - 2}{16x - 36} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{4x - 5} + 2}{\sqrt{4x - 5} + 2} \nl &= \lim_{x \to 9/4} \frac{(4x - 5) - 4}{(16x - 36)(\sqrt{4x - 5} + 2) } \nl &= \lim_{x \to 9/4} \frac{4x - 9}{4(4x - 9)(\sqrt{4x - 5} + 2)} \nl &= \lim_{x \to 9/4} \frac{1}{4(\sqrt{4x - 5} + 2)} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{1}{16}} \ea \]

Trigonometric Identities

We now discuss limits of indeterminate forms that involve trigonometric expressions. We simplify limits using the following trigonometric identities: \begin{align} 1 &= \sin^2 x + \cos^2 x \label{eq:pythag-identity} \nl \sin 2x &= 2 \sin x \cos x \label{eq:sin-double-angle} \nl \cos 2x &= \cos^2 x - \sin^2 x \label{eq:cos-double-angle} \period \end{align}

EXAMPLE 4
For each of (i) and (ii), evaluate the limit.
  1. \(\ds \lim_{x \to \pi/2} \frac{\sin 2x }{\cos x} \)
  2. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1 - \cos x}{3 \sin x} \)
  3. \(\ds \lim_{x \to \pi/4} \frac{\cos 2x}{\cos x - \sin x} \)
  1. We rewrite \(\sin 2x\) as \(2 \sin x \cos x.\) Then \[ \ba \lim_{x \to \pi/2} \frac{\sin 2x}{\cos x} &= \lim_{x \to \pi/2} \frac{2 \sin x \cos x}{\cos x} \nl &= \lim_{x \to \pi/2} 2 \sin x \nl &= \boxed{2} \ea \]
  2. We multiply the numerator and denominator each by \((1 + \cos x),\) from which we find \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1 - \cos x}{3 \sin x} &= \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1 - \cos x}{3 \sin x} \cdot \frac{1 + \cos x}{1 + \cos x} \nl &= \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1 - \cos^2 x}{3 \sin x(1 + \cos x)} \nl &= \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin^2 x}{3 \sin x(1 + \cos x)} \nl &= \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{3 (1 + \cos x)} \nl &= \frac{\sin(0)}{3\left(1 + \cos(0)\right)} \nl &= \boxed{0} \ea \]
  3. We understand that \(\cos 2x = \cos^2 x - \sin^2 x\). Therefore, \[ \ba \lim_{x \to \pi/4} \frac{\cos 2x}{\cos x - \sin x} &= \lim_{x \to \pi/4} \frac{\cos^2 x - \sin^2 x}{\cos x - \sin x} \nl &= \lim_{x \to \pi/4} \frac{\left(\cos x + \sin x\right) \left(\cos x - \sin x\right)}{\cos x - \sin x} \nl &= \lim_{x \to \pi/4} \left(\cos x + \sin x \right) \nl &= \boxed{\sqrt{2}} \ea \]
We should now be comfortable with recognizing indeterminate forms. The examples in this section showcase methods for algebraically manipulating indeterminate limits into a form for which direct substitution yields a definite answer. Remain vigilant for any factors that cancel—this is the common goal of the algebraic manipulations featured in this section.

SECTION SUMMARY

  • A limit in the form \(\ds \indZero\) or \(\ds \frac{\infty}{ \infty}\) is indeterminate. However, a limit in the form \(\ds \frac{k}{0}\), where \(k \ne 0\), does not exist.
  • To find a limit of a rational expression at infinity, take the limit of the highest-degree terms only—they are the most significant. The result agrees with the end behavior of the function.
  • Try to algebraically manipulate an indeterminate limit into a form for which direct substitution yields a definite value.
  • If you notice a fraction with a radical in an indeterminate limit, try to rationalize it by multiplying the numerator and denominator by the conjugate of the radical expression.
  • Utilize trigonometric identities to simplify indeterminate limits involving trigonometric functions. Remember the Pythagorean identity as well as the double angle identities for sine and cosine, \(\sin(2x) = 2 \sin(x) \cos(x)\) and \(\cos(2x) = \cos^2(x) - \sin^2(x)\).
