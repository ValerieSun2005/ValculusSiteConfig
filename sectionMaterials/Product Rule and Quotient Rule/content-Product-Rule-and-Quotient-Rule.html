This section discusses the differentiation of products and quotients of functions.

Product Rule

The product rule enables us to differentiate functions in the form \(f(x)g(x).\) For convenience, we will write \(f(x)\) as \(f\) and \(g(x)\) as \(g.\) Also assume that these functions are differentiable.

The derivative of a product of two functions isn't the product of their derivatives. Thus, \((fg)' \ne f' g'.\)
If primes represent derivatives with respect to \(x,\) then the product rule states \[(fg)' = f' g + f g' \period\] Notice the alternating pattern of the primes.

PRODUCT RULE
If \(f\) and \(g\) are differentiable functions, then \[(fg)' = f' g + fg' \period\]
EXAMPLE 1
Let \(p(x) = x^2 \cdot 3x.\) Using the product rule, find \(p'(x).\)

We have \[ \baat{2} f(x) &= x^2 \lspace g(x) &&= 3x \nl f'(x) &= 2x \lspace g'(x) &&= 3 \period \eaat \] Then the product rule gives \[ \ba p'(x) &= f'(x) g(x) + f(x) g'(x) \nl &= 2x \cdot 3x + x^2 \cdot 3 \nl &= \boxed{9x^2} \ea \]

We confirm this answer to be correct if we rewrite \(p\) as \[p(x) = 3x^3 \period\] This is a polynomial, which we differentiate using the power rule: \[p'(x) = 3(3 \cdot x^2) = 9x^2 \period\]

EXAMPLE 2
Let \(a\) be a differentiable function that satisfies \(a(3) = 3\) and \(a'(3) = 2.\) Let \(k(x) = x^2 \cdot a(x).\) Find \(k'(3).\)
We have \[ \ba k'(x) &= 2x \cdot a(x) + x^2 \cdot a'(x) \nl k'(3) &= 6 a(3) + 9 a'(3) \period \ea \] Plugging in the given values of \(a(3)\) and \(a'(3)\) gives \[k'(3) = 6 \cdot 3 + 9 \cdot 2 = \boxed{36}\]
EXAMPLE 3
Find \(\ds \deriv{}{x} [e^x \sin x].\)
The derivative of \(e^x\) is \(e^x,\) and the derivative of \(\sin x\) is \(\cos x.\) Thus, we have \[ \baat{2} f(x) &= e^x \lspace g(x) &&= \sin x \nl f'(x) &= e^x \lspace g'(x) &&= \cos x \period \eaat \] So the product rule gives \[f(x) g'(x) + f'(x) g(x) = \boxed{e^x \cos x + e^x \sin x}\]

Quotient Rule

A derivative of a quotient isn't a quotient of derivatives; that is, \((f/g)' \ne f'/g'.\)

To differentiate expressions in the form \(f(x)/g(x),\) where \(f\) and \(g\) are differentiable functions, we need the quotient rule. The quotient rule states that \[\par{\frac{f}{g}}' = \frac{f'g - fg'}{g^2} \comma\] given that \(g \ne 0.\) A mnemonic for memorizing the quotient rule is "Low Dee High minus High Dee Low, all over the square of what's below," where "Dee High" references \(f'\) and "Dee Low" is \(g'.\)

EXAMPLE 4
Find \(\ds \deriv{}{x} \par{\frac{\sin x}{x}} \period \)

We have \[ \baat{2} f(x) &= \sin x \lspace g(x) &&= x \nl f'(x) &= \cos x \lspace g'(x) &&= 1 \period \eaat \] Thus, using the quotient rule, we have \[ \frac{g(x)f'(x) - f(x)g'(x)}{[g(x)]^2} = \boxed{\frac{x \cos x - \sin x}{x^2}} \]
