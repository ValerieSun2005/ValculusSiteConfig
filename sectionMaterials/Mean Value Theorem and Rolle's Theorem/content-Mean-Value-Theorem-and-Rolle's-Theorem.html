In this section, we introduce the Mean Value Theorem and Rolle's Theorem. Recall the definitions of the average rate of change and the instantaneous rate of change. The average rate of change of the function \(f(x)\) over the interval \([a, b]\) is \[\frac{\Delta f}{\Delta x} = \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a} \period\] This quantity represents the slope of the secant line to the graph of \(y = f(x)\) from \(x = a\) to \(x = b\) (Figure 1A). In contrast, the instantaneous rate of change of \(f(x)\) at \(x = c\) is \[f'(c) \comma \] the slope of the tangent line to \(y = f(x)\) at \(x = c\) (Figure 1B).

Now consider a function \(f\) that is continuous on \([a, b]\) and differentiable on \((a, b).\) If \(f(a) = f(b),\) then the average rate of change of \(f\) over \([a, b]\) is \(0.\) Then, by Rolle's Theorem, there must be a number \(c\) such that \[a \lt c \lt b \and f'(c) = 0 \period\]

ROLLE'S THEOREM
Let \(f\) be a function that satisfies the following three conditions:
  1. \(f\) is continuous on \([a, b].\)
  2. \(f\) is differentiable on \((a, b).\)
  3. \(f(a) = f(b).\)
Then Rolle's Theorem guarantees a number \(c\) in \((a, b)\) such that \(f'(c) = 0.\)

The graph of a function \(y = f(x)\) is shown in Figure 2. We see that \(f\) is continuous on \([a, b]\) and differentiable on \((a, b),\) therefore satisfying conditions (1) and (2). Moreover, \(f(a) = f(b) = k,\) so \(f\) meets condition (3). Thus, Rolle's theorem guarantees a number \(c,\) \(a \lt c \lt b,\) for which \(f'(c) = 0.\) At \(x = c,\) the graph of \(y = f(x)\) has a horizontal tangent. Notice that this tangent line is parallel to the secant line formed by the points \((a, k)\) and \((b, k).\)

EXAMPLE 1
Let \(f\) be the function defined by \(f(x) = \frac{1}{3}x^3 - 2x^2 + 2.\) Note that \(f(0) = 2\) and \(f(6) = 2.\) Find the value of \(c\) that satisfies Rolle's Theorem for \(f\) on \([0, 6].\)

Since \(f(x)\) is a polynomial, it's continuous and differentiable for all \(x.\) Moreover, \(f(0) = f(6)\) \(= 2.\) Therefore, \(f\) meets all three conditions of Rolle's Theorem, so there must be a number \(c\) in \((0, 6)\) such that \(f'(c) = 0\) (Figure 3). Differentiating \(f\) gives \[f'(x) = x^2 - 4x \period \] We then have \[ \ba f'(c) &= c^2 - 4c = 0 \implies \\ c &= 0 \and c = 4 \period \ea \] The solution in \((0, 6)\) is \(\boxed{c = 4,}\) so this value satisfies Rolle's Theorem for \(f\) on \([0, 6].\)

Let's now discuss the Mean Value Theorem. Suppose that a function \(f\) is continuous on \([a, b]\) and differentiable on \((a, b).\) The average rate of change of \(f(x)\) over \(a \leq x \leq b\) is \[\frac{\Delta f}{\Delta x} = \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a} \period \] The Mean Value Theorem then guarantees a value \(c\) in \((a, b)\) such that \[f'(c) = \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a} \period \]

Geometrically, the Mean Value Theorem states that at the point \((c, f(c)),\) the line tangent to the graph of \(y = f(x)\) is parallel to the secant line formed by the points \((a, f(a))\) and \((b, f(b))\) (Figure 4). Thus, Rolle's Theorem is a special case of the Mean Value Theorem for a horizontal tangent. Additionally, several values of \(c\) in \((a, b)\) may satisfy the Mean Value Theorem; that is, several tangent lines to the graph of \(y = f(x)\) may be parallel to the secant line formed by \((a, f(a))\) and \((b, f(b)).\)

MEAN VALUE THEOREM
If \(f\) is continuous on \([a, b]\) and differentiable on \((a, b),\) then the Mean Value Theorem guarantees a value \(c\) in \((a, b)\) such that \[f'(c) = \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a} \period \]
EXAMPLE 2
(Calculator required.) Let \(g\) be the function defined by \(g(x) = \frac{4}{3}x^3 + 6x^2 - 2x + 1.\) Find all values \(c\) that satisfy the Mean Value Theorem for \(g\) on \([0, 3].\)

Since \(g\) is a polynomial, \(g(x)\) is continuous on \([0, 3]\) and differentiable on \((0, 3).\) Thus, \(g\) satisfies the conditions of the Mean Value Theorem on \([0, 3].\) We note that \[g(0) = 1 \and g(3) = 85 \comma \] so the average rate of change of \(g\) on \([0, 3]\) is \[\frac{g(3) - g(0)}{3 - 0} = 28 \period \] The Mean Value Theorem then guarantees a value \(c\) in \((0, 3)\) such that \[g'(c) = 28 \period \] We then have \[ \ba g'(c) = 4c^2 + 12c - 2 &= 28\\ 4c^2 + 12c - 30 &= 0 \comma \ea \] whose only solution in \((0, 3)\) is \(\boxed{c = 1.622.}\) Thus, this value satisfies the Mean Value Theorem for \(g\) on \([0, 3].\)

An application of the Mean Value Theorem is convicting speeding drivers. For example, a driver who travels \(180\) miles in two hours has an average speed of \[\overline{v} = \frac{180 \; \textrm{miles}}{2 \; \textrm{hrs}} = 90 \; \textrm{mph} \period\] The driver's position function is continuous and differentiable. Thus, the Mean Value Theorem guarantees an instant in the driver's journey during which his or her instantaneous speed is \( \overline{v} = 90\) mph. This fact can then be used to charge the driver with speeding.

EXAMPLE 3
Rainwater accumulates in a container during a storm. The volume of the rainwater is given by the differentiable function \(R,\) where \(R(t)\) is measured in milliliters and \(t\) is measured in minutes. Selected values of \(R(t)\) are shown in the table below for \(0 \leq t \leq 12.\)
\(t\) \(1\) \(3\) \(6\) \(7\) \(8\) \(10\) \(12\)
\(R(t)\) \(1\) \(1.4\) \(1.9\) \(2.4\) \(3.1\) \(3.9\) \(5\)
Must there be a time for \(0 \leq t \leq 12\) such that the volume of rainwater is increasing at a rate of \(\frac{1}{3} \; \textrm{mL}/\textrm{min} \ques\)

The average rate of change of \(R(t)\) over \(0 \leq t \leq 12\) is \[ \ba \frac{R(12) - R(0)}{12 - 0} &= \frac{5 - 1}{12} \\ &= \frac{1}{3} \; \textrm{mL}/\textrm{min} \period \ea \] Since \(R(t)\) is continuous on \(0 \leq t \leq 12\) and differentiable on \(0 \lt t \lt 12,\) the Mean Value Theorem then guarantees a value \(c\) in \((0, 12)\) such that \[R'(c) = \frac{1}{3} \; \textrm{mL}/\textrm{min} \period \] Therefore, there is a time at which the water in the container is increasing at a rate of \(\frac{1}{3}\) mL/min.
