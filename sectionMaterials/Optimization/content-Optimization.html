Optimization is the process by which a quantity of interest is maximized or minimized. It is a mathematical procedure that applies absolute extrema to real-world problems. The emphasis on efficiency in the everyday world cultivates the need for optimization in a wide range of applications, including geometry, finance, and science. Examples of optimization problems include maximizing volume, minimizing surface area, minimizing materials used, minimizing time, and maximizing profit. In this section, we explore the strategy to solve these problems and see their diverse uses.

Often, the most difficult step is understanding the problem and setting up equations. Let's introduce some general problem-solving steps for optimization problems.

SOLVING OPTIMIZATION PROBLEMS
  1. Understand. Understand the situation by drawing diagrams and testing various possibilities.
  2. Notation. Assign a variable—say, \(Q\)—to the quantity to maximize or minimize. Introduce notation by assigning algebraic quantities to unknown values. It is helpful to use suggestive variables, such as \(A\) for area, \(d\) for distance, and \(L\) for length.
  3. Express. Express \(Q\) in terms of one variable—say, \(x\)—to obtain \(Q(x).\)
  4. Minimize or Maximize. Use calculus to to find the absolute minimum or maximum of \(Q(x).\) Find the critical numbers of \(Q\) and consider the endpoints of the domain in context.

The First Derivative Test and the Second Derivative Test allow us to determine whether a critical number corresponds to a relative minimum or maximum. To locate the absolute minimum or maximum, first establish the domain of \(Q.\) For example, if \(Q(x)\) represents area, then no side length \(x\) can result in a negative value of \(Q.\) Afterward, consider the endpoints or end behavior of the domain. Analyze extreme cases: What happens to \(Q(x)\) if \(x\) is made as small as possible? What happens if \(x\) is made as large as possible?

FIRST DERIVATIVE TEST
Let \(x = a\) be the location of a critical point of \(f(x).\)
  1. If \(f'(x)\) changes sign from positive to negative at \(x = a,\) then \(f(a)\) is a relative maximum of \(f.\)
  2. If \(f'\) changes sign from negative to positive at \(x = a,\) then \(f(a)\) is a relative minimum of \(f.\)
  3. If \(f'\) does not change sign at \(x = a,\) then \(f(a)\) is not a relative extremum of \(f.\)

SECOND DERIVATIVE TEST
Let \(x = a\) be the location of a critical point of \(f.\)
  1. If \(f''(a) \gt 0,\) then \(f(a)\) is a relative minimum of \(f.\)
  2. If \(f''(a) \lt 0,\) then \(f(a)\) is a relative maximum of \(f.\)
  3. If \(f''(a) = 0,\) then the test is inconclusive.
EXAMPLE 1
The sum of two numbers \(a\) and \(b\) is \(14.\) Find the values of \(a\) and \(b\) such that their product, \(P,\) is maximized.

To understand the problem, we experiment with some possibilities:
  • If \(a = 13\) and \(b = 1,\) then their product is \(P = 13.\)
  • If \(a = 12\) and \(b = 2,\) then their product is \(P = 24.\)
  • If \(a = 11\) and \(b = 3,\) then their product is \(P = 33.\)

We want to find \(a\) and \(b\) that maximize the product \(P = ab\) and adhere to the condition \(a + b = 14.\) Since \(a\) and \(b\) add to \(14,\) \begin{equation} a + b = 14 \pd \label{eq:product-step-1} \end{equation} We want to maximize \begin{equation} P = ab \pd \label{eq:product-step-2} \end{equation} Our goal is to express \(P\) in terms of one variable—for example, \(a.\) From \(\eqRefer{eq:product-step-1},\) we find \(b = 14 - a.\) We substitute this expression into \(\eqRefer{eq:product-step-2}\) to get \[P = a(14 - a) = 14a - a^2 \pd\] We may now write \(P\) as \(P(a).\) We find the critical numbers of \(P(a)\) by solving \(P'(a) = 0 \col\) \[ \ba P'(a) = 14 - 2a &= 0 \nl \implies a &= 7 \pd \ea \] For \(a \lt 7,\) \(P' \gt 0;\) for \(a \gt 7,\) \(P' \lt 0.\) Since \(P'(a)\) changes sign from positive to negative at \(a = 7,\) the First Derivative Test states that \(a = 7\) is the location of a relative maximum of \(P.\) Also note the following:

  • If \(a \lt 0,\) then \(b \gt 0\) and \(P \lt 0.\)
  • If \(a \gt 14,\) then \(b \lt 0\) and \(P \lt 0.\)
Thus, \(a = 7\) must also correspond to the absolute maximum of \(P\) (see Figure 1). If \(a = 7,\) then \[b = 14 - 7 = 7\] and \(P = 7 \cdot 7 = 49.\) So \(\boxed{a = 7}\) and \(\boxed{b = 7}\) maximize the product \(P.\)

EXAMPLE 2
A farmer has \(40\) feet of fence to build a closed, rectangular pen for her pumpkin farm. She uses the side of her barn as one side of the pen. What dimensions should she make the pen to maximize its area?

We create a sketch of the scenario (Figure 2). Let \(w\) denote the pen's width and \(\ell\) denote the pen's length. Also let \(A\) be the area of the pen. These suggestive variables help us avoid confusion.

Let's construct a system of equations. The equation for the pen's perimeter is \begin{equation} 2w + \ell = 40 \pd \label{eq:farmer-1} \end{equation} We want to maximize \begin{equation} A = w \ell\ \pd \label{eq:farmer-2} \end{equation}

Rearranging \(\eqRefer{eq:farmer-1}\) yields \(\ell = 40 - 2w,\) which we substitute into \(\eqRefer{eq:farmer-2}\) to acquire \[ A = w \ell = w(40 - 2w) \pd\] We now have a function of \(A\) composed solely of \(w,\) so we may differentiate \(A\) to find its critical numbers: \[ \ba A'(w) = 40 - 4w &= 0 \nl \implies w &= 10 \pd \ea \]

The width must satisfy \(0 \leq w \leq 20;\) otherwise, \(A \lt 0.\) The absolute maximum either of \(A\) lies either at the endpoints of \(0 \leq w \leq 20\) or at its critical numbers. By the First Derivative Test, \(w = 10\) corresponds to a relative maximum of \(A\) since \(A'(w)\) switches sign from positive to negative at \(w = 10.\) At \(w = 0\) and \(w = 20,\) \(A = 0\) because the pen collapses into a line. Thus, \(w = 10\) corresponds to the absolute maximum of \(A\) (Figure 3). For \(w = 10,\) we have \(\ell = 40 - 2(10) = 20.\) Thus, a pen of dimensions \(20\) feet by \(10\) feet produces the maximum area, \(200\) square feet.

EXAMPLE 3
A can is designed to hold \(40\) cubic inches of water. Find the dimensions that minimize the materials used to manufacture the can.

To understand the problem, we construct a picture (Figure 4). Let \(r\) be the can's radius and \(h\) be its height. The volume of the cylinder is then \begin{equation} 40 = \pi r^2 h \pd \label{eq:equation-cylinder-volume} \end{equation} We want to minimize the surface area: \begin{equation} S = 2 \pi r^2 + 2 \pi r h \pd \label{eq:equation-cylinder-SA} \end{equation} (To remember this formula, see that the bases of the can are circles of area \(\pi r^2,\) and a rectangular sheet of area \(2 \pi r h\) is wrapped around the can.)

We aim to express \(S\) in terms of one variable. From \(\eqRefer{eq:equation-cylinder-volume},\) we have \(h = 40/(\pi r^2),\) which we plug into \(\eqRefer{eq:equation-cylinder-SA}\) to get \[ \ba S &= 2 \pi r^2 + 2 \pi r \left(\frac{40}{\pi r^2}\right) \nl &= 2 \pi r^2 + \frac{80}{r} \pd \ea \] We solve \(S'(r) = 0,\) as follows: \[ \ba S'(r) = 4 \pi r - \frac{80}{r^2} &= 0 \nl \implies r &= \sqrt[3]{\frac{20}{\pi}} \pd \nl \ea \]

For \(r \lt \sqrt[3]{20/\pi},\) \(S' \lt 0;\) for \(r \gt \sqrt[3]{20/\pi},\) \(S' \gt 0.\) Since \(S'\) changes sign from negative to positive at \(r = \sqrt[3]{20/\pi},\) this value corresponds to a relative minimum of \(S\) by the First Derivative Test. Also, because \(\lim_{r \to 0^+} S(r) = \infty\) and \(\lim_{r \to \infty} S(r) = \infty,\) there needs to be a minimum value of \(S(r),\) which must exist at the critical number (Figure 5). So \(r = \sqrt[3]{20/\pi}\) corresponds to the absolute minimum of \(S.\) We then have \(r \approx 1.853 \un{in}\) and \[h = \frac{40}{\pi (20/\pi)^{2/3}} \approx 3.707 \un{in} \pd\] Thus, a can of radius \(1.853 \un{in}\) and height \(3.707 \un{in}\) minimizes the materials used while holding the required volume. With a radius of \(1.853 \un{in},\) the cylinder's diameter is \(3.707 \un{in}\)—the same dimension as the height.

In some situations, minimizing or maximizing the square of a function is more convenient. If \(f(x)\) has a relative minimum or maximum at \(x = a,\) then so does \(\sqrt{f(x)}.\) For example, \[p(x) = \sqrt{3 + (x - 2)^2} \and q(x) = 3 + (x - 2)^2\] each have a relative minimum at \(x = 2.\) This fact is convenient because we can choose to optimize the square of a function—a process that is sometimes easier.

EXAMPLE 4
Find two points on the parabola \(y = x^2\) that are closest to \((0, 1).\)

We sketch a picture of the situation (Figure 6). Let \(d\) be this shortest distance. The Distance Formula then provides \begin{equation} d = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2} \pd \label{eq:parabola-d} \end{equation} We have \((x_1, y_1)\) \(= (0, 1)\) and the point on the parabola, \((x_2, y_2)\) \(= (x, x^2).\) Thus, \(\eqRefer{eq:parabola-d}\) becomes \[d = \sqrt{x^2 + (x^2 - 1)^2} \pd \]

We need to find the critical numbers of \(d.\) But \(d^2\) has the same critical numbers, so we choose to optimize \(d^2\) for convenience: Let \(q(x) = d^2.\) Then \[q(x) = x^2 + (x^2 - 1)^2 \cma\] whose derivative is \[q'(x) = 4x^3 - 2x \pd\] Solving \(q'(x) = 0,\) we get \[ \ba 4x^3 - 2x &= 0 \nl 2x(2x^2 - 1) &= 0 \nl \implies x &= 0 \cma \tfrac{-1}{\sqrt 2} \cma \tfrac{1}{\sqrt 2} \pd \ea \]

If we differentiated \(d,\) then we would've gotten \[d'(x) = \frac{2x(2x^2-1)}{\sqrt{x^4 - x^2 + 1}} \cma\] which has the same zeros as \(q'(x),\) confirming that \(d\) and \(q(x) = d^2\) have the same critical numbers.

We see \(q'\) changes sign from positive to negative at \(x = 0,\) so this value corresponds to a relative maximum of \(q.\) But we want to minimize \(q,\) so we ignore this critical number. Conversely, \(q'\) changes sign from negative to positive at both \(x = -1/\sqrt 2\) and \(x = 1/\sqrt 2.\) So both values are relative minima of \(q.\) Because \(\lim_{x \to -\infty} q\) \(= \infty\) and \(\lim_{x \to \infty} q\) \(= \infty,\) the absolute minima of \(q\) (and therefore of \(d\)) must occur when \(x = -1/\sqrt 2\) and \(x = 1/\sqrt 2\) (Figure 7). At each value, \[y = \par{\frac{1}{\sqrt 2}}^2 = \frac{1}{2} \pd\] Thus, \[\boxed{\par{\frac{-1}{\sqrt 2} \cma \frac{1}{2}}} \and \boxed{\par{\frac{1}{\sqrt 2} \cma \frac{1}{2}}}\] are the closest points on \(y = x^2\) to \((0, 1).\)

EXAMPLE 5
Find the area of the largest rectangle that can be inscribed in the parabola \(y = 9 - x^2\) for \(-3 \leq x \leq 3.\)

The word inscribed means the rectangle has two vertices on the parabola and two vertices on the \(x\)-axis (Figure 8). Since the rectangle has length \(2x\) and height \(y,\) its area is \begin{equation} A = 2xy \pd \label{eq:inscribe-area} \end{equation} But \(y = 9 - x^2,\) so \(\eqRefer{eq:inscribe-area}\) becomes \[A(x) = 2x(9 - x^2) \pd\] We solve \(A'(x) = 0,\) finding \[ \ba A'(x) = 18 - 6x^2 &= 0 \nl \implies x &= \nspace -\sqrt 3 \cma \sqrt 3 \pd \ea \] The domain of \(A(x)\) is \(0 \leq x \leq 3,\) so we discard \(x = - \sqrt 3.\) The value \(x = \sqrt 3\) corresponds to a relative maximum of \(A\) because \(A'\) changes sign from positive to negative at that point. At each endpoint, \(A = 0\) since the rectangle collapses into a line. Thus, \(x = \sqrt 3\) is the location of the absolute maximum of \(A.\) Then \(y = 9 - (\sqrt 3)^2 = 6,\) so \(\eqRefer{eq:inscribe-area}\) gives the maximum area to be \[A = 2(\sqrt 3)(6) = \boxed{12 \sqrt 3}\]

Optimization in Business

Revenue is the amount of money a firm earns from sales. Revenue is the product of quantity and price. Let \(x\) be the number of units sold. If \(R(x)\) is a firm's revenue function and \(p(x)\) is the firm's price function, then \[R(x) = x \, p(x) \pd\] In many situations, \(p\) is a constant function because the price of each unit is the same. Profit is the positive difference between revenue and cost. If a firm produces \(x\) units, then its profit function \(P(x)\) is \[ \ba P(x) &= R(x) - C(x) \cma \ea \] where \(R(x)\) is the revenue function and \(C(x)\) is the cost function.

EXAMPLE 6
Sydney operates a food truck in which she sells hot dogs for \(12\) dollars each. The cost, in dollars, of her producing \(x\) hot dogs is modeled by the function \[C(x) = 3000 - 100x + x^2 \pd\] How many hot dogs should Sydney sell to maximize her profit from hot dog sales? What are her profit and average cost at that output level?

Profit is the positive difference between revenue and cost. The revenue function is \(12x\) and the cost function is \(C(x),\) so the profit function is \[ \ba P(x) &= 12x - (3000 - 100x + x^2) \nl &= -x^2 + 112x - 3000 \pd \ea \] We want to maximize \(P(x).\) We find the critical numbers of \(P \col\) \[ \ba P'(x) = -2x + 112 &= 0\nl \implies x &= 56 \pd \nl \ea \] For \(x \lt 56,\) \(P' > 0;\) for \(x \gt 56,\) \(P' \lt 0.\) So \(P\) has a relative maximum at \(x = 56.\) The domain of \(P(x)\) is \(x \geq 0.\) At the endpoints \(P(0) = -3000\) and \(\lim_{x \to \infty} P(x) = -\infty.\) This analysis asserts that \(P\) has a maximum value, which must correspond to \(x = 56.\) Sydney therefore should sell \(\boxed{56}\) hot dogs to maximize her profit.

EXAMPLE 7
A store sells makeup products. In a day, the store sells \(40\) eyeliner pencils if they are priced at \(\$10\) each. For every \(\$2\) increase in price, the manager predicts that the store will sell \(4\) fewer pencils. Determine the price of the pencils such that the store maximizes its revenue from eyeliner sales.

Let \(x\) be the number of dollars the store raises the price of eyeliner pencils. For every \(\$2\) increase in the pencil, the price function is \((10 + 2x)\) and the quantity function is \((40 - 4x).\) Revenue is the product of quantity and price, so we have \[ \ba R(x) &= (40 - 4x)(10 + 2x) \nl &= -8x^2 + 40x + 400 \pd \ea \] Its critical numbers are \[ \ba R'(x) = -16x + 40 &= 0 \nl x &= \tfrac{40}{16} = 2.5 \pd \ea \] For \(x \lt 2.5,\) \(R' \gt 0;\) for \(x \gt 2.5,\) \(R' \lt 0.\) Thus, the First Derivative Test states that \(x = 2.5\) corresponds to a relative maximum of \(R(x).\) The domain of \(R(x)\) is \(x \geq 0,\) and we note the following:

  • \(R(0) = 400.\)
  • \(R(2.5) = 450.\)
  • \(\ds \lim_{x \to \infty} R(x) = -\infty.\)
Thus, \(x = 2.5\) corresponds to the absolute maximum of \(R(x).\) The store should raise the price of the eyeliner pencil by \(\boxed{\$2.5}.\)

EXAMPLE 8
A lifeguard on the shoreline runs to rescue a drowning swimmer located \(20\) feet east of the lifeguard and \(8\) feet from the shoreline. The lifeguard can run at \(10\) feet per second and swim at \(6\) feet per second. She runs a distance \(x\) before jumping into the water and swimming to the drowning person (Figure 9). At what distance \(x\) should the lifeguard enter the water in order to minimize the time to rescue the swimmer?

We need to derive an expression for the time, \(t,\) the lifeguard takes to rescue the swimming person. The distance she runs is \(x,\) and the distance she swims is \(\sqrt{(20 - x)^2 + 64}.\) The relationship between time, distance, and speed is \[\textrm{time} = \frac{\textrm{distance}}{\textrm{speed}} \pd\] The lifeguard's speed is \(10\) feet per second onshore and \(6\) feet per second in water. Therefore, her time for the first part of the trip, running onshore, is \[t_1 = \frac{x}{10} \pd\] Her time for the second part of the trip, swimming, is \[t_2 = \frac{\sqrt{(20 - x)^2 + 64}}{6} \pd\] The total time, \(t,\) is the sum of these times: \[ \ba t &= t_1 + t_2 \nl &= \frac{x}{10} + \frac{\sqrt{(20 - x)^2 + 64}}{6} \pd \ea \] This expression is in terms of \(x,\) so we differentiate to locate its critical numbers: \[ \ba t'(x) = \frac{1}{10} + \frac{x - 20}{6 \sqrt{x^2 - 40x + 464}} &= 0 \nl \implies x &= 14 \pd \ea \]

Since \(t'(x)\) changes sign from negative to positive at \(x = 14,\) this value corresponds to a relative minimum of \(t.\) You may verify that this value also corresponds to the absolute minimum of \(t\) (Figure 10). So the lifeguard should run \(\boxed{14 \un{ft}}\) before jumping in the water.

EXAMPLE 9
Figure 11 shows an arrangement of right triangles that subtend an angle \(\theta.\) Find \(x\) such that \(\theta\) is maximized. (Calculator required.)


The three angles \[\andThree{\theta}{\tan^{-1} \par{\frac{x}{4}}}{\tan^{-1} \par{\frac{3}{x + 1}}}\] add to \(\pi.\) Thus, we have \[\theta = \pi - \tan^{-1} \left( \frac{x}{4} \right) - \tan^{-1} \left(\frac{3}{x + 1}\right) \pd\] The absolute maximum of \(\theta\) occurs when \(\boxed{x = 0.899}.\)

EXAMPLE 10
Two power towers—one of height \(10\) meters and the other of height \(15\) meters—are \(30\) meters apart. Wire is anchored from the ground to the top of each tower (Figure 12). Where on the ground should the wires be anchored to use the least amount of wire?

Let Tower A be the \(10 \un{m}\) tower and Tower B be the \(15 \un{m}\) tower. Let \(x\) be the distance from the base of Tower A to the anchor point. The amount of wire needed to connect top of Tower A to the anchor point is then \[w_1 = \sqrt{x^2 + 15^2} = \sqrt{x^2 + 225} \pd\] Likewise, the amount of wire needed to connect the anchor point to the top of Tower B is \[w_2 = \sqrt{(30 - x)^2 + 10^2} = \sqrt{x^2 - 60x + 1000} \pd\] Therefore, the total amount of wire required is \[ \ba w &= w_1 + w_2 \nl &= \sqrt{x^2 + 225} + \sqrt{x^2 - 60x + 1000} \pd \ea \] We differentiate \(w\) and equate it to \(0\) to find \[ \ba \frac{\dd w}{\dd x} = \frac{x - 30}{\sqrt{x^2 - 60x + 1000}} + \frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2 + 225}} &= 0 \nl \implies x &= 18 \pd \ea \] This critical number corresponds to the absolute minimum of \(w.\) Thus, the optimal spot to anchor the wires is \(\boxed{18 \un{m}}\) east of Tower A.
