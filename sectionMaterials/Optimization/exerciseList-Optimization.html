A triangle has sides of lengths \(a\) and \(b,\) and the angle between them is \(\theta\) (see Figure 1). The area of the triangle is \[A = \tfrac{1}{2} ab \sin \theta \pd\] Using calculus, show that the area is maximized when \(\theta = 90 \degree.\)
We differentiate \(A\) with respect to \(\theta\) to obtain \[A'(\theta) = \tfrac{1}{2} a b \cos \theta \pd\] The domain of \(A\) is \(0 \degree \leq \theta \leq 180 \degree;\) we find \(A'(\theta) = 0\) when \(\theta = 90 \degree.\) For \(0 \lt \theta \lt 90 \degree,\) \(A' \gt 0;\) for \(90 \degree \lt \theta \lt 180 \degree,\) \(A' \lt 0.\) Thus, the First Derivative Test asserts that \(A\) has a relative maximum when \(\theta = 90 \degree\) because at that point \(A'\) switches sign from positive to negative. At the endpoints—\(\theta = 0 \degree\) and \(\theta = 180 \degree\)—the triangle collapses into a line, and so \(A = 0.\) We therefore conclude that \(\theta = 90 \degree\) corresponds to the absolute maximum of \(A,\) as requested. This problem shows that arranging two sides perpendicular to each other maximizes the area of the corresponding triangle.
Find two numbers whose sum is \(30\) such that their product is maximized.
Let \(x\) and \(y\) be the two numbers. We want to find the maximum of \begin{equation} P = xy \pd \label{eq:sum-30-prod} \end{equation} We have \begin{equation} x + y = 30 \cma \label{eq:sum-30-sum} \end{equation} from which we solve for one variable—for example, \(y = 30 - x.\) We substitute this expression into \(\eqRefer{eq:sum-30-prod}\) to express \(P\) solely in terms of \(x \col\) \[P = x(30 - x) = 30x - x^2 \pd\] We now find the critical numbers of \(P\) by equating \(P'(x) = 0.\) We find \[ \ba P'(x) = 30 - 2x &= 0 \nl \implies x &= 15 \pd \ea \] We see \(P' \gt 0\) for \(x \lt 15\) and \(P' \lt 0\) for \(x \gt 15.\) Thus, the First Derivative Test asserts that \(x = 15\) corresponds to a relative maximum of \(P.\) Because \(\lim_{x \to -\infty} P = -\infty\) and \(\lim_{x \to \infty} P = -\infty,\) \(P\) needs to have a maximum value, which must occur when \(x = 15.\) We therefore conclude that \(x = 15\) corresponds to the absolute maximum of \(P.\) When \(x = 15,\) \(\eqRefer{eq:sum-30-sum}\) gives \(y = 15.\) The two numbers therefore are \(\boxed{15}\) and \(\boxed{15}.\)
Find two numbers whose difference is \(20\) such that their product is minimized.

Thus, we want to maximize \(\rho(r).\) Its derivative is \[\rho'(r) = 400r e^{-4r} - 800r^2 e^{-4r} \pd\] Letting \(\rho'(r) = 0,\) we find \(r = 0\) and \(r = 0.5.\) At \(r = 0,\) \(\rho'(r)\) does not change sign, so \(\rho\) does not have an extremum there. At a party, identical speakers are on either side of a room that is \(8\) meters wide. If a dancer stands \(x\) meters from one speaker, then the sound intensity she experiences is \[I = k \par{\frac{1}{x^2} + \frac{1}{(8 - x)^2}} \cma\] where \(k\) is a constant of proportionality. Where in the room should the dancer stand to experience the loudest sound?
We must maximize \(I.\) Differentiating gives \[\deriv{I}{x} = k \par{\frac{-2}{x^3} + \frac{2}{(8 - x)^3}} \pd\] We solve for \(\textDeriv{I}{x} = 0,\) as follows: \[ \ba k \par{\frac{-2}{x^3} + \frac{2}{(8 - x)^3}} &= 0 \nl \frac{2}{x^3} &= \frac{2}{(8 - x)^3} \nl x^3 &= (8 - x)^3 \nl x &= 4 \pd \ea \] The sign of \(\textDeriv{I}{x}\) switches from negative to positive when \(x = 4,\) so this value corresponds to a relative minimum of \(I.\) We want to maximize \(I,\) so we have no further consideration of this extremum. The domain of \(I\) is \(0 \lt x \lt 8;\) we see \[\lim_{x \to 0^+} I = \infty \qquad \lim_{x \to 8^-} I = \infty \pd\] Thus, sound intensity is maximized at either \(x = 0\) or \(x = 8.\) The dancer therefore should stand right next to either speaker.
Farmer Ana has \(24\) feet of fence to build a closed, rectangular pen for her animals. She uses the side of her farmhouse as one side of the pen. Find the maximum possible area of the pen.
Drawing a sketch, we call \(\ell\) the pen's length and \(w\) pen's width. The perimeter is \(2w + \ell\) and must equal \(24 \col\) \begin{equation} 2w + \ell = 24 \pd \label{eq:pen-p} \end{equation} Our goal is to maximize \begin{equation} A = w \ell \cma \label{eq:pen-a} \end{equation} which we express in terms of one variable. From \(\eqRefer{eq:pen-p},\) we find \(\ell = 24 - 2w.\) We substitute this expression into \(\eqRefer{eq:pen-a}\) to obtain \begin{equation} A = w(24 - 2w) = 24w - 2w^2 \pd \label{eq:pen-Aw} \end{equation} Since \(A\) is now expressed in terms of \(w,\) we differentiate to find \[A'(w) = 24 - 4w \pd\] At \(w = 6\) we find a critical point of \(A.\) For \(w \lt 6,\) \(A' \gt 0;\) for \(w \gt 6,\) \(A' \lt 0.\) Thus, \(w = 6\) corresponds to a relative maximum of \(A.\) The question is now whether \(w = 6\) corresponds to the absolute maximum of \(A.\) And it does, because the domain of \(A\) is \(0 \leq w \leq 12;\) at each endpoint \(A = 0\) by \(\eqRefer{eq:pen-Aw}.\) Then \(\eqRefer{eq:pen-p}\) gives \(\ell = 24 - 2(6) = 12,\) meaning the pen's area is maximized by instating a width of \(6 \un{feet}\) and length of \(12 \un{feet}.\) The area is then \[6 \un{ft} \cdot 12 \un{ft} = \boxed{72 \un{ft}^2}\]
Find the point on the line \(y = 2x\) that is closest to the point \((4, 3).\)
Let \((x, y)\) be a point on the line. The horizontal distance from \((x, y)\) to \((4, 3)\) is \(4 - x.\) The vertical distance between \((x, y)\) and \((4, 3)\) is \(y - 3.\) Thus, the distance from the point to the line is \begin{equation} d = \sqrt{(4 - x)^2 + (y - 3)^2} \pd \label{eq:line-distance} \end{equation} We want to express \(d\) entirely in terms of one variable, \(x.\) Since \(y = 2x,\) \(\eqRefer{eq:line-distance}\) becomes \[d = \sqrt{(4 - x)^2 + (2x - 3)^2} \cma\] the expression we want to minimize. The critical numbers of \(d\) and \(d^2\) are the same, so for simplicity we find the critical numbers of \(d^2.\) Let \(q(x) = d^2;\) then \[q'(x) = -2(4 - x) + 4(2x - 3) \pd\] We solve \(q'(x) = 0,\) finding \(x = 2.\) Since \(q'\) changes sign from negative to positive at \(x = 2,\) this value corresponds to a relative minimum of \(q\) (and, therefore, of \(d\)). The domain of \(q\) is \(-\infty \lt x \lt \infty;\) as \(x\) approaches each endpoint, \(q \to \infty.\) Thus, \(x = 2\) must be the absolute minimum of \(q\) (and, hence, of \(d\)).   • \(p(3) = 7.\)
  • \(\ds \lim_{x \to \infty} p(x) = -\infty.\)

Show that \[\frac{\sin \theta_1}{v_1} = \frac{\sin \theta_2}{v_2} \pd\] This formula is Snell's Law, a fundamental equation in the study of optics.
Recall that \(\text{time} = \text{distance}/\text{speed}.\) When the light ray travels from \(P\) to \(O,\) the time is \[t_1 = \frac{\sqrt{x^2 + a^2}}{v_1} \pd\] Likewise, traveling from \(O\) to \(Q\) takes time \[t_2 = \frac{\sqrt{(\ell - x)^2 + b^2}}{v_2} \pd\] Then the total time is \[ \ba T &= t_1 + t_2 \nl &= \frac{\sqrt{x^2 + a^2}}{v_1} + \frac{\sqrt{(\ell - x)^2 + b^2}}{v_2} \pd \ea \] Differentiating, we obtain \begin{equation} T'(x) = \frac{x}{v_1 \sqrt{x^2 + a^2}} - \frac{\ell - x}{v_2 \sqrt{(\ell - x)^2 + b^2}} \pd \label{eq:snell} \end{equation} We see \[\sin \theta_1 = \frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2 + a^2}} \qquad \sin \theta_2 = \frac{\ell - x}{\sqrt{(\ell - x)^2 + b^2}} \pd \] Then \(\eqRefer{eq:snell}\) becomes \[T'(x) = \frac{\sin \theta_1}{v_1} - \frac{\sin \theta_2}{v_2} \pd\] Solving \(T'(x) = 0\) then yields \[\frac{\sin \theta_1}{v_1} = \frac{\sin \theta_2}{v_2} \cma\] as requested. We assume that this situation confers the absolute minimum of \(T.\)
