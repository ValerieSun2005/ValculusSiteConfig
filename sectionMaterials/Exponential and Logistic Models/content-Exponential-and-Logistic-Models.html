In this section we learn the mathematical basis of exponential growth and decay, and discuss the applications of these models.

Exponential Models

Exponential growth is a phenomenon that confers very fast growth. Numerically, it represents repeated multiplication, which has applications in population growth and continuous compounding. Conversely, in exponential decay a function rapidly shrinks, usually to \(0.\)

The definition of an exponential model is represented by the differential equation \begin{equation} \deriv{y}{x} = k y \comma \label{eq:exp-diff-eq} \end{equation} where \(k\) is a proportionality constant. In exponential growth, \(k \gt 0\) and \(y\) increases (Figure 1); in exponential decay, \(k \lt 0\) and \(y\) decreases (Figure 2). \(\eqRefer{eq:exp-diff-eq}\) shows that rate of change of \(y\) is directly proportional to itself, meaning that a small \(y\) changes slowly and a large \(y\) changes quickly. Solving for \(y\) in \(\eqRefer{eq:exp-diff-eq}\) using Separation of Variables, we get

\[ \ba \frac{1}{y} \di y &= k \di x \nl \ln \abs{y} &= kx + C_1 \nl y &= e^{C_1} e^{kx} \comma \ea \] where we let \(C = e^{C_1}\) for notational convenience. We now have \[y = Ce^{kx} \period\] If \(x = 0,\) then \(e^{kx} = 1\) and so \(C\) is the value of \(y\) at \(x = 0.\)

EXPONENTIAL MODELS
If \(k\) is a constant, then the differential equation for an exponential model is \[\deriv{y}{x} = ky \period\] The solution is \[y = C e^{kx} \comma\] where \(C\) is the value of \(y\) when \(x = 0.\)
  • In exponential growth, \(k \gt 0\) and \(y\) increases.
  • In exponential decay, \(k \lt 0\) and \(y\) decreases.
EXAMPLE 1
A population of sheep as a function of time is modeled by the differentiable function \(N(t),\) where \(N(t)\) is the number of sheep and \(t\) is years. The population exhibits continuous, exponential growth at a rate of \(2\%.\)
  1. Write an expression for \(\textDeriv{N}{t}.\)
  2. How quickly is the population changing when it has \(500\) sheep?
  3. The population initially has \(300\) sheep. After how many years does the population exceed \(700\) sheep?
  1. The population grows continuously at a rate of \(2\%.\) Thus, by \(\eqRefer{eq:exp-diff-eq}\) \(k = 0.02\) and the differential equation is \[\boxed{\deriv{N}{t} = 0.02N}\]
  2. When the population has \(500\) sheep, the population is growing at a rate of \[\deriv{N}{t} = 0.02(500) = \boxed{10 \undiv{sheep}{year}}\]
  3. The population of sheep is initially \(300.\) Therefore, by \(\eqRefer{eq:exp-solution},\) \[N = 300e^{0.02t} \period\] We solve for \(t\) when \(N = 700 \col\) \[ \ba 700 &= 300e^{0.02t} \nl \ln \frac{700}{300} &= 0.02t \nl t &\approx 42.364 \period \ea \] Therefore, after \(42.364\) years (or \(43\) years), the population contains more than \(700\) sheep.
EXAMPLE 2
A deposit of \(\$500\) is made to a bank account. This amount is compounded continuously at a rate of \(3\%.\) The amount of money in the account is given by \(M(t),\) where \(M(t)\) is measured in dollars and \(t\) is measured in years.
  1. Write a differential equation for \(\textDeriv{M}{t}.\)
  2. Find the expression for \(M(t).\)
  1. We have \(k = 0.03,\) so from \(\eqRefer{eq:exp-diff-eq}\) the differential equation is \[\boxed{\deriv{M}{t} = 0.03M}\]
  2. Initially, \(M(t) = \$500.\) We can either solve the differential equation in part (a) or use \(\eqRefer{eq:exp-solution}.\) Either method produces \[\boxed{M(t) = 500e^{0.03t}}\]

Logistic Models

The differential equation that defines a logistic model is \begin{equation} \deriv{y}{x} = ky \par{1 - \frac{y}{L}} \comma \label{eq:logistic-diff-eq} \end{equation} where \(L\) is the carrying capacity. The solution to \(\eqRefer{eq:logistic-diff-eq}\) is \begin{equation} y = \frac{L}{1 + Ce^{-kx}} \comma \label{eq:logistic-eq} \end{equation} whose derivation is at the end of this section. Similar to exponential models, logistic growth occurs if \(k \gt 0\) and logistic decay occurs if \(k \lt 0.\)

Logistic growth is highly intuitive: As \(y\) grows, the term \((1 - y/L)\) acts as an inhibitor that brings \(\textDeriv{y}{x}\) to \(0\) as \(y\) approaches \(L.\) Therefore, the graph of \(\eqRefer{eq:logistic-eq}\) for \(k \gt 0\) has the horizontal asymptote \(y = L\) because \(\lim_{x \to \infty} y = L;\) see Figure 3. Conversely, the graph of logistic decay (\(k \lt 0\) in \(\eqRefer{eq:logistic-eq}\)) has the property \(\lim_{x \to -\infty} y = L;\) see Figure 4.
