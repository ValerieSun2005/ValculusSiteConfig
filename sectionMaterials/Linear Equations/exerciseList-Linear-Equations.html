The standard form of a first-order linear differential equation is \[y' + P(x) y = Q(x) \pd\] Find an expression for an integrating factor, \(\mu(x).\) How do we use this function?
An integrating factor is \[\mu(x) = e^{\int P(x) \di x} \pd\] We only require one integrating factor, so we omit the constant of integration in \(\int P(x) \di x.\) We use the integrating factor to express the left side of \[y' + P(x) y = Q(x)\] as the derivative of the product \(y e^{\int P(x) \di x}.\) We then integrate and solve for \(y.\)

\(\ds y' - 4xy = e^{2x^2}\)
This differential equation is in the form \[y' + P(x) y = Q(x) \cma\] with \(P(x) = -4x\) and \(Q(x) = e^x.\) To find an integrating factor, we use \[\mu(x) = e^{\int -4x \di x} = e^{-2x^2} \pd\] We multiply the differential equation by this factor to obtain \begin{equation} e^{-2x^2} y' - 4xe^{-2x^2}y = 1 \pd \label{eq:-4xy-mult-expand} \end{equation} In \(\eqRefer{eq:-4xy-mult-expand}\) the left side is the Product Rule expansion of \(\par{ye^{-2x^2}}'.\) Thus, \(\eqRefer{eq:-4xy-mult-expand}\) is \[\par{ye^{-2x^2}}' = 1 \pd\] Integrating both sides then gives \[ye^{-2x^2} = x + C \implies \boxed{y = e^{2x^2}(x + C)}\]
\(\ds xy' - y = x\)
The differential equation is linear and non-separable. We divide the equation by \(x\) to get \begin{equation} y' - \frac{1}{x} y = 1 \pd \label{eq:-y-standard} \end{equation} \(\eqRefer{eq:-y-standard}\) is in the form \[y' + P(x) y = Q(x) \cma\] with \(P(x) = -1/x\) and \(Q(x) = 1.\) Thus, we choose an integrating factor to be \[\mu(x) = e^{\int P(x) \di x} = e^{\int (-1/x) \di x} = e^{-\ln|x|} = x^{-1} = \frac{1}{x} \pd\] Multiplying \(\eqRefer{eq:-y-standard}\) by \(\mu(x) = 1/x\) then yields \begin{equation} \frac{1}{x} y' - \frac{1}{x^2} y = \frac{1}{x} \pd \label{eq:-y-multiplied} \end{equation} In \(\eqRefer{eq:-y-multiplied}\) the left side is the Product Rule expansion of \(\par{\frac{1}{x} y}'.\) Thus, we rewrite \(\eqRefer{eq:-y-multiplied}\) as \[\par{\frac{1}{x} y}' = \frac{1}{x} \pd\] Integrating both sides then gives \[ \frac{1}{x} y = \ln|x| + C \implies \boxed{y = x \ln|x| + Cx} \]
\(\ds xy' - y = 1 \cma y(1) = 1\)
We divide the differential equation by \(x \col\) \begin{equation} y' - \frac{1}{x} y = \frac{1}{x} \pd \label{eq:xy'-y=1-standard} \end{equation} The coefficient of \(y\) is \(-1/x,\) so we select an integrating factor to be \[\mu(x) = e^{\int (-1/x) \di x} = e^{-\ln x} = \frac{1}{x} \pd\] We multiply \(\eqRefer{eq:xy'-y=1-standard}\) by \(\mu(x) = 1/x\) to get \begin{equation} \frac{1}{x} y' - \frac{1}{x^2} y = \frac{1}{x^2} \pd \label{eq:xy'-y=1-expanded} \end{equation} In \(\eqRefer{eq:xy'-y=1-expanded}\) the left side is, by the Product Rule, the derivative of \(\tfrac{1}{x} y.\) We then condense \(\eqRefer{eq:xy'-y=1-expanded}\) as \[\par{\frac{1}{x} y}' = \frac{1}{x^2} \pd\] Integrating both sides then gives \begin{equation} \frac{1}{x} y = -\frac{1}{x} + C \pd \label{eq:xy'-y=1-C} \end{equation} Plugging the initial condition, \(y(1) = 1,\) into \(\eqRefer{eq:xy'-y=1-C}\) shows \[1 = -1 + C \implies C = 2 \pd\] Thus, \(\eqRefer{eq:xy'-y=1-C}\) becomes \[\frac{1}{x} y = -\frac{1}{x} + 2 \implies \boxed{y = -1 + 2x}\]
\(\ds y' + 2xy = 4x \cma y(0) = 3\)
This differential equation is in the form \[y' + P(x) y = Q(x) \cma\] with \(P(x) = 2x\) and \(Q(x) = 4x.\) To find an integrating factor, we use \[\mu(x) = e^{\int P(x) \di x} = e^{\int 2x \di x} = e^{x^2} \pd\] We ignore the constant of integration because we only need one integrating factor. We multiply the differential equation by \(\mu(x) = e^{x^2},\) finding \begin{equation} e^{x^2} y' + 2x e^{x^2} y = 4x e^{x^2} \pd \label{eq:2xy-expanded} \end{equation} In \(\eqRefer{eq:2xy-expanded}\) the left side is, by the Product Rule, the derivative of \(e^{x^2} y.\) We therefore rewrite \(\eqRefer{eq:2xy-expanded}\) as \[\par{e^{x^2} y}' = 4x e^{x^2} \pd \] Integrating both sides gives \begin{equation} e^{x^2} y = \int 4x e^{x^2} \di x \label{eq:2xy-integrated} \end{equation} We evaluate the integral on the right-hand side of \(\eqRefer{eq:2xy-integrated}\) by substituting \[u = x^2 \lspace \dd u = 2x \di x \pd \] The integral then becomes \[\int 2 e^u \di u = 2 e^u + C = 2 e^{x^2} + C \pd\] Hence, \(\eqRefer{eq:2xy-integrated}\) is \begin{equation} ye^{x^2} = 2 e^{x^2} + C \pd \label{eq:2xy-constant} \end{equation} We are given that \(y(0) = 3;\) substituting this point into \(\eqRefer{eq:2xy-constant}\) shows \[3 = 2 + C \implies C = 1 \pd\] Then we solve for \(y\) in \(\eqRefer{eq:2xy-constant} \col\) \[y = \boxed{2 + e^{-x^2}}\]
\(\ds (\csc x) y' - y = 3 \cma y(0) = 1\)
We first manipulate the coefficient of \(y'\) to be \(1,\) so we multiply the equation by \(\sin x.\) We get \begin{equation} y' - (\sin x) y = 3 \sin x \pd \label{eq:cscx-standard} \end{equation} We then select the integrating factor \[\mu(x) = e^{\int (-\sin x) \di x} = e^{\cos x} \pd\] We multiply \(\eqRefer{eq:cscx-standard}\) by this factor, obtaining \begin{equation} e^{\cos x} \, y' - (\sin x) e^{\cos x} \, y = 3 (\sin x) e^{\cos x} \pd \label{eq:cscx-mu-expanded} \end{equation} In \(\eqRefer{eq:cscx-mu-expanded}\) the left side is the Product Rule expansion of \(\par{y e^{\cos x}}'.\) We thus rewrite \(\eqRefer{eq:cscx-mu-expanded}\) and integrate: \begin{equation} ye^{\cos x} = \int 3(\sin x) e^{\cos x} \di x \pd \label{eq:cscx-mu-int} \end{equation} The right side of \(\eqRefer{eq:cscx-mu-int}\) requires substituting \[u = \cos x \lspace \dd u = - \sin x \di x \scol\] the integral then becomes \[\int - 3e^u \di u = -3e^u + C = -3e^{\cos x} + C \pd\] Then \(\eqRefer{eq:cscx-mu-int}\) is \begin{equation} ye^{\cos x} = -3e^{\cos x} + C \pd \label{eq:cscx-mu-C} \end{equation} Plugging the given initial condition, \(y(0) = 1,\) into \(\eqRefer{eq:cscx-mu-C}\) then gives \[e = -3e + C \implies C = 4e \pd\] Hence, \(\eqRefer{eq:cscx-mu-C}\) becomes \[ye^{\cos x} = - 3e^{\cos x} + 4e \implies \boxed{y = -3 + 4e^{1 -\cos x}}\]
\(\ds y' + y = x \cma y(0) = 0\)
The coefficient of \(y\) is \(1,\) so we select an integrating factor to be \[\mu(x) = e^{\int 1 \di x} = e^x \pd\] Multiplying the differential equation by \(\mu(x)\) gives \begin{equation} e^x \, y' + e^x \, y = xe^x \pd \label{eq:y'+y-expanded} \end{equation} The left side of \(\eqRefer{eq:y'+y-expanded}\) is the Product Rule expansion of \(\par{ye^x}'.\) We thus rewrite \(\eqRefer{eq:y'+y-expanded}\) and integrate, finding \begin{equation} y \, e^x = \int x e^x \di x \pd \label{eq:y'+y-int} \end{equation} The right side of \(\eqRefer{eq:y'+y-int}\) requires Integration by Parts. The formula for Integration by Parts is \[\int u \di v = uv - \int v \di u \pd\] We select \[ \baat{2} u &= x \lspace \dd v &&= e^x \di x \nl \dd u &= \dd x \lspace v &&= e^x \pd \eaat \] Then \[\int xe^x \di x = xe^x - \int e^x \di x = xe^x - e^x + C \pd\] Thus, \(\eqRefer{eq:y'+y-int}\) becomes \begin{equation} y \, e^x = xe^x - e^x + C \pd \label{eq:y'+y-C} \end{equation} We substitute the initial condition into \(\eqRefer{eq:y'+y-C},\) finding \[0 = 0 - 1 + C \implies C = 1 \pd\] Thus, \(\eqRefer{eq:y'+y-C}\) becomes \[y \, e^x = xe^x - e^x + 1 \implies \boxed{y = x - 1 + e^{-x}}\]
\(\ds y' = 2x - y\)
We rewrite the differential equation as \begin{equation} y' + y = 2x \pd \label{eq:2x-y-standard} \end{equation} The coefficient of \(y\) is \(1;\) we then choose an integrating factor to be \[\mu(x) = e^{\int 1 \di x} = e^x \pd\] Multiplying \(\eqRefer{eq:2x-y-standard}\) by this factor, we get \begin{equation} e^x \, y' + e^x \, y = 2x e^x \pd \label{eq:2x-y-mu-expanded} \end{equation} In \(\eqRefer{eq:2x-y-mu-expanded}\) the left side is the Product Rule expansion of \(\par{ye^x}'.\) We thus rewrite \(\eqRefer{eq:2x-y-mu-expanded}\) and integrate: \begin{equation} ye^x = \int 2x e^x \di x \pd \label{eq:2x-y-int} \end{equation} The right side of \(\eqRefer{eq:2x-y-int}\) requires Integration by Parts; in \(\int 2x e^x \di x\) \(= \int u \di v\) we choose \[ \baat{2} u &= 2x \lspace \dd v &&= e^x \di x \nl \dd u &= 2 \di x \lspace v &&= e^x \pd \eaat \] The integral then becomes \[ \ba uv - \int v \di u &= 2x e^x - \int e^x (2) \di x \nl &= 2xe^x - 2e^x + C \pd \ea \] Thus, \(\eqRefer{eq:2x-y-int}\) becomes \[ye^x = 2xe^x - 2e^x + C \implies \boxed{y = 2x - 2 + Ce^{-x}}\]
\(\ds y' + 2xy = x^3\)
The coefficient of \(y\) is \(2x,\) so an integrating factor is \[\mu(x) = e^{\int 2x \di x} = e^{x^2} \pd\] We then multiply the differential equation by this factor to obtain \begin{equation} e^{x^2} \, y' + 2x e^{x^2} \, y = x^3 e^{x^2} \pd \label{eq:-2xy=x^3} \end{equation} In \(\eqRefer{eq:-2xy=x^3}\) the left side is the Product Rule expansion of \(\par{ye^{x^2}}'.\) We then condense \(\eqRefer{eq:-2xy=x^3}\) and integrate: \begin{equation} y \, e^{x^2} = \int x^3 e^{x^2} \di x \pd \label{eq:-2xy=x^3-int} \end{equation} We rewrite the integral on the right side of \(\eqRefer{eq:-2xy=x^3-int}\) as \[\int x \, x^2 \, e^{x^2} \di x \pd\] Substituting \(a = x^2,\) we get \(\dd a = 2x \di x\) and so the integral becomes \begin{equation} \int \tfrac{1}{2} ae^a \di a \pd \label{exp:-2xy=x^3-int-asub} \end{equation} The Integration by Parts formula is \[\int u \di v = uv - \int v \di u \pd\] In \(\eqref{exp:-2xy=x^3-int-asub}\) we perform Integration by Parts with \[ \baat{2} u &= \tfrac{1}{2} a \lspace \dd v &&= e^a \di a \nl \dd u &= \tfrac{1}{2} \di a \lspace v &&= e^a \pd \eaat \] Thus, \(\eqref{exp:-2xy=x^3-int-asub}\) becomes \[ \ba \tfrac{1}{2} a e^a - \int \tfrac{1}{2} e^a \di a &= \tfrac{1}{2} ae^a - \tfrac{1}{2} e^a + C \nl &= \tfrac{1}{2} x^2 e^{x^2} - \tfrac{1}{2} e^{x^2} + C \pd \ea \] Thus, \(\eqRefer{eq:-2xy=x^3-int}\) is \[y \, e^{x^2} = \tfrac{1}{2} x^2 e^{x^2} - \tfrac{1}{2} e^{x^2} + C \implies \boxed{y = \tfrac{1}{2} x^2 - \tfrac{1}{2} + Ce^{-x^2}}\]
A ball of mass \(m\) is dropped from rest. Let \(v(t)\) be the ball's velocity at any time \(t.\) The ball experiences two forces: gravitational force and drag force. Gravitational force is \(mg,\) where \(g\) is the acceleration due to gravity. Drag force is \(bv(t),\) where \(b\) is a drag coefficient. Newton's Second Law gives a differential equation for the ball's velocity to be \[m \deriv{v}{t} + bv(t) = mg \pd\] Using an integrating factor, show that \[v(t) = \frac{mg}{b} \par{1 - e^{-bt/m}} \pd\] Terminal velocity, \(v_T,\) is the maximum velocity the ball can exhibit. Show that \(v_T = mg/b.\)
For simplicity we write \(v(t)\) as \(v.\) We divide the given differential equation by \(m\) to get \begin{equation} \deriv{v}{t} + \frac{b}{m} v = g \pd \label{eq:drag-linear} \end{equation} We then choose an integrating factor to be \[\mu(t) = e^{\int (b/m) \di t} = e^{bt/m} \pd\] Multiplying \(\eqRefer{eq:drag-linear}\) by \(\mu(t) = e^{bt/m}\) yields \[e^{bt/m} \deriv{v}{t} + \frac{b}{m} e^{bt/m} v = g e^{bt/m} \pd\] The left side is the Product Rule expansion of \(\par{ve^{bt/m}}',\) so we rewrite the equation as \begin{equation} \par{ve^{bt/m}}' = g e^{bt/m} \pd \label{eq:drag-condensed} \end{equation} Integrating both sides of \(\eqRefer{eq:drag-condensed}\) then gives \begin{equation} ve^{bt/m} = \frac{mg}{b} e^{bt/m} + C \cma \label{eq:drag-constant} \end{equation} where \(C\) is a constant of integration. We solve for \(C\) by noting that \(v(0) = 0;\) substituting this point into \(\eqRefer{eq:drag-constant}\) shows \[0= \frac{mg}{b} + C \implies C = - \frac{mg}{b} \pd\] Then \(\eqRefer{eq:drag-constant}\) becomes \[ \ba ve^{bt/m} &= \frac{mg}{b} e^{bt/m} - \frac{mg}{b} \nl \implies v(t) &= \frac{mg}{b} \par{1 - e^{-bt/m}} \cma \ea \] as requested.
We then find the terminal velocity to be \[ \ba v_T &= \lim_{t \to \infty} v(t) \nl &= \lim_{t \to \infty} \frac{mg}{b} \par{1 - e^{-bt/m}} \nl &= \frac{mg}{b} \pd \ea \]

A Bernoulli differential equation is of the form \[\deriv{y}{x} + P(x) y = Q(x) y^n \pd\] To solve this differential equation, we first rewrite the equation by substituting \(y = u^{1/(1 - n)}.\) Use this method to solve \[\deriv{y}{x} + \frac{1}{x} y = 2x y^3 \pd\]
We see \(n = 3.\) Thus, we substitute \begin{equation} y = u^{1/(1 - 3)} = u^{-1/2} \pd \label{eq:bernoulli-sub} \end{equation} Then we use the Chain Rule to get \[\deriv{y}{x} = -\frac{1}{2} u^{-3/2} \, \deriv{u}{x} \pd\] The differential equation then becomes \begin{align} -\frac{1}{2} u^{-3/2} \deriv{u}{x} + \frac{1}{x} u^{-1/2} &= 2x u^{-3/2} \nonumber \nl \deriv{u}{x} - \frac{2}{x} u &= -4x \pd \label{eq:bernoulli-linear} \end{align} We now have a linear equation in terms of \(u.\) In \(\eqRefer{eq:bernoulli-linear}\) the coefficient of \(u\) is \(-2/x,\) so we choose an integrating factor \[e^{\int -(2/x) \di x} = e^{-2 \ln x} = \frac{1}{x^2} \pd\] Multiplying both sides of \(\eqRefer{eq:bernoulli-linear}\) by this factor gives \begin{equation} \frac{1}{x^2} \deriv{u}{x} - \frac{2}{x^3} u = -\frac{4}{x} \pd \label{eq:bernoulli-mu-expanded} \end{equation} The left side of \(\eqRefer{eq:bernoulli-mu-expanded}\) is the Product Rule expansion of \(\par{\frac{1}{x^2}u}',\) so we rewrite \(\eqRefer{eq:bernoulli-mu-expanded}\) and integrate to get \begin{align} \frac{1}{x^2}u &= -4 \ln|x| + C \nonumber \nl u &= -4 x^2 \ln|x| + Cx^2 \pd \label{eq:bernoulli-u} \end{align} From \(\eqRefer{eq:bernoulli-sub}\) we have \(u = y^{-2},\) so \(\eqRefer{eq:bernoulli-u}\) becomes \[ \ba y^{-2} &= -4 x^2 \ln|x| + Cx^2 \nl y &= \boxed{\par{-4 x^2 \ln|x| + Cx^2}^{-1/2}} \ea \]
