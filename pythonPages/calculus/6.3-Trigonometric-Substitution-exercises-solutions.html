QUESTION 1
For \[\sqrt{36 - x^2}\comma \] use a trigonometric substitution to remove the root sign.
SOLUTION We substitute \(x = 6 \sin \theta.\) The expression then becomes \[\sqrt{36 - (6 \sin \theta)^2} = \sqrt{36 - 36 \sin^2 \theta} = 6 \sqrt{1 - \sin^2 \theta} \period\] Using the Pythagorean identity, we get \(1 - \sin^2 \theta = \cos \theta.\) Thus, \[6 \sqrt{1 - \sin^2 \theta} = 6 \abs{\cos \theta} \period\]
QUESTION 2
For \[\sqrt{100 + 9x^2}\comma \] use a trigonometric substitution to remove the root sign.
SOLUTION We substitute \(x = \frac{10}{3} \tan \theta,\) giving \[ \ba \sqrt{100 + 9 \par{\frac{10}{3} \tan^2 \theta }^2 } &= \sqrt{100 + 100 \tan^2 \theta } \\ &= 10 \sqrt{1 + \tan^2 \theta} \\ &= 10 \abs{\sec \theta} \period \ea \]
QUESTION 3
For \[\sqrt{25x^2 - 144}\comma \] use a trigonometric substitution to remove the root sign.
SOLUTION We substitute \(x = \frac{12}{5} \sec \theta\) to get \[ \ba \sqrt{25 \par{\frac{12}{5} \sec \theta}^2 - 144} &= \sqrt{144 \sec^2 \theta - 144} \\ &= 12 \sqrt{\sec^2 \theta - 1} \\ &= 12 \abs{\tan \theta} \period \ea \]
QUESTION 4
For \[\ds \int \sqrt{9 - x^2} \di x\comma \] evaluate the integral using a trigonometric substitution.
SOLUTION To remove the root sign, we substitute \(x = 3 \sin \theta:\) \[ \ba \sqrt{9 - x^2} &= \sqrt{9 - (3 \sin \theta)^2} \\ &= 3 \sqrt{1 - \sin^2 \theta} \\ &= 3 \abs{\cos \theta} \period \ea \] Then \(\dd x = 3 \cos \theta \di \theta,\) and the integral becomes \[\int 3 \abs{\cos \theta} \cdot 3 \cos \theta \di \theta \period \] Since we're working with an indefinite integral, we drop the absolute value bars for convenience. Thus, we have \[ \ba 9 \int \cos^2 \theta \di \theta &= 9 \int \frac{1}{2}(1 + \cos 2 \theta) \di \theta \\ &= \frac{9}{2} \theta + \frac{9}{4} \sin 2 \theta + C \\ &= \frac{9}{2} \theta + \frac{9}{2} \sin \theta \cos \theta + C \period \ea \] We now convert this answer to be in terms of \(x.\) Construct a right triangle for reference. Since \(x = 3 \sin \theta,\) it follows that \(\sin \theta = x/3.\) This triangle has a hypotenuse of length \(3\) and an angle \(\theta\) whose opposite side has length \(x.\) Consequently, the side adjacent to \(\theta\) has length \(\sqrt{3^2 - x^2} = \sqrt{9 - x^2}.\) Then \(\cos \theta = \sqrt{9 - x^2}/3\) and \(\theta = \sin^{-1}(x/3).\) Our final answer is then \[\boxed{\frac{9}{2} \sin^{-1} \par{\frac{x}{3}} + \frac{x \sqrt{9 - x^2}}{2} + C} \]
QUESTION 5
For \[\ds \int_0^{1/2} \frac{x^2}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \di x \comma \] evaluate the integral using a trigonometric substitution.
SOLUTION We substitute \(x = \sin \theta\) to remove the root sign, since \[\sqrt{1 - \sin^2 \theta} = \abs{\cos \theta} \period\] Then \(\dd x = \cos \theta \tan \theta \di \theta.\) We now change the bounds: When \(x = 0,\) we have \[0 = \sin \theta \implies \theta = 0 \period\] Likewise, when \(x = 1/2,\) we have \[\frac{1}{2} = \sin \theta \implies \theta = \frac{\pi}{6} \period\] Therefore, our new integral is \[\int_0^{\pi/6} \frac{\sin^2 \theta}{\abs{\cos \theta}} \, \cos \theta \di \theta \period \] For \(0 \lt \theta \lt \pi/6,\) \(\cos \theta \gt 0.\) Therefore, on this interval, \(\abs{\cos \theta} = \cos \theta.\) Consequently, we drop the absolute value bars on \(\abs{\cos \theta}\) because they are superfluous. We then have \[ \ba \int_0^{\pi/6} \sin^2 \theta \di \theta &= \int_0^{\pi/6} \frac{1}{2}(1 - \cos 2 \theta) \di \theta \\ &= \par{\frac{1}{2} \theta - \frac{1}{4} \sin 2 \theta} \Big|_0^{\pi/6} \\ &= \par{\frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{\pi}{6} - \frac{1}{4} \sin \frac{\pi}{3}} - \par{0 - \frac{1}{4} \sin 0} \\ &= \boxed{\frac{\pi}{12} - \frac{\sqrt 3}{8}} \\ \ea \]
QUESTION 6
For \[\ds \int_{-1/2}^{-1/4} \frac{\sqrt{16x^2 - 1}}{2x} \di x\comma \] evaluate the integral using a trigonometric substitution.
SOLUTION We substitute \(x = \frac{1}{4} \sec \theta\) to remove the root sign in the numerator: \[\sqrt{16 \par{\frac{1}{4} \sec \theta}^2 - 1} = \sqrt{\sec^2 \theta - 1} = \abs{\tan \theta} \period\] As a result, \(\dd x = \frac{1}{4} \sec \theta \tan \theta \di \theta.\) Now let's consider the bounds: The lower bound, \(x = -1/2,\) gives \[-\frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{4} \sec \theta \implies \theta = \frac{2 \pi}{3} \period\] Additionally, the upper bound, \(x = -1/4,\) yields \[-\frac{1}{4} = \frac{1}{4} \sec \theta \implies \theta = \pi \period\] Our new integral is \[\int_{2 \pi/3}^{\pi} \frac{\abs{\tan \theta}}{2 \par{\frac{1}{4} \sec \theta}} \cdot \frac{1}{4} \sec \theta \tan \theta \di \theta \period\] For \(2 \pi/3 \lt \theta \lt \pi, \tan \theta \lt 0.\) Therefore, \(\abs{\tan \theta} = - \tan \theta\) on this interval. Substituting in this new expression, we obtain \[ \ba - \frac{1}{2} \int_{2 \pi/3}^{\pi} \tan^2 \theta \di \theta &= - \frac{1}{2} \int_{2 \pi/3}^{\pi} \sec^2 \theta - 1 \di \theta \\ &= - \frac{1}{2} \par{\tan \theta - \theta} \Big|_{2 \pi/3}^{\pi} \\ &= - \frac{1}{2} \par{\tan \pi - \pi} + \frac{1}{2} \par{\tan \par{\frac{2 \pi}{3}} - \frac{2 \pi}{3} } \\ &= \boxed{\frac{\pi}{6} - \frac{\sqrt 3}{2}} \ea \]
QUESTION 7
For \[\ds \int \frac{1}{x \sqrt{x^2 - 25}} \di x\comma \] evaluate the integral using a trigonometric substitution.
SOLUTION We let \(x = 5 \sec \theta\) to clear the root sign in the denominator: \[\sqrt{(5 \sec \theta)^2 - 25} = 5 \sqrt{\sec^2 \theta - 1} = 5 \abs{\tan \theta} \period\] Moreover, \(\dd x = 5 \sec \theta \tan \theta \di \theta.\) Thus, the new integral is \[\int \frac{1}{(5 \sec \theta)(5 \abs{\tan \theta})} \, 5 \sec \theta \tan \theta \di \theta \period\] As usual, we ignore the absolute value bars of \(\abs{\tan \theta}\) because we have an indefinite integral. Accordingly, the integral becomes \[ \frac{1}{5} \int \di \theta = \frac{1}{5} \theta + C \period \] Because \(x = 5 \sec \theta,\) it follows that \(\theta = \sec^{-1}(x/5).\) Therefore, our final answer is \[\boxed{\frac{1}{5} \sec^{-1} \frac{x}{5} + C}\]
QUESTION 8
For \[\ds \int_{3 \sqrt 3 /2}^{3/2} \frac{1}{x \sqrt{4x^2 + 9}} \di x\comma \] evaluate the integral using a trigonometric substitution.
SOLUTION To clear the root sign in the denominator, we let \(x = \frac{3}{2} \tan \theta:\) \[\sqrt{4 \par{\frac{3}{2} \tan \theta}^2 + 9} = 3 \sqrt{\tan^2 \theta + 1} = 3 \abs{\sec \theta} \period\] Also, \(\dd x = \frac{3}{2} \sec^2 \theta \di \theta.\) We now change the integral's bounds: When \(x = 3 \sqrt 3 /2,\) we have \[\frac{3 \sqrt 3}{2} = \frac{3}{2} \tan \theta \implies \theta = \frac{\pi}{3} \period\] Likewise, when \(x = 3/2,\) we have \[\frac{3}{2} = \frac{3}{2} \tan \theta \implies \theta = \frac{\pi}{4} \period\] Thus, our new integral is \[\int_{\pi/3}^{\pi/4} \frac{1}{(\frac{3}{2} \tan \theta) (3 \abs{\sec \theta})} \cdot \frac{3}{2} \sec^2 \theta \di \theta \period \] For \(\pi/3 \lt \theta \lt \pi/4,\) \(\abs{\sec \theta} \gt 0. \) Accordingly, on this interval \(\abs{\sec \theta} = \sec \theta.\) We then substitute this expression to attain \[ \ba \frac{1}{3} \int_{\pi/3}^{\pi/4} \frac{\sec \theta}{\tan \theta} \di \theta &= \frac{1}{3} \int_{\pi/3}^{\pi/4} \csc \theta \di \theta \\ &= -\frac{1}{3} \ln \abs{\csc \theta + \cot \theta} \Big|_{\pi/3}^{\pi/4} \\ &= \frac{1}{3} \ln \abs{\csc \theta + \cot \theta} \Big|_{\pi/4}^{\pi/3} \\ &= \frac{1}{3} \ln \abs{\csc \frac{\pi}{3} + \cot \frac{\pi}{3}} - \frac{1}{3} \ln \abs{\csc \frac{\pi}{4} + \cot \frac{\pi}{4}} \\ &= \boxed{\frac{1}{3} \ln \par{\frac{\sqrt 3}{1 + \sqrt 2}}} \ea \]
QUESTION 9
For \[\ds \int \frac{x^5}{\sqrt{4 - x^2}} \di x\comma \] evaluate the integral using a trigonometric substitution.
SOLUTION We clear the root sign in the denominator by letting \(x = 2 \sin \theta:\) \[\sqrt{4 - (2 \sin \theta)^2} = \sqrt{4 - 4 \sin^2 \theta} = 2 \sqrt{1 - \sin^2 \theta} = 2 \abs{\cos \theta} \period \] Also, \(\dd x = 2 \cos \theta \di \theta.\) Then the integral becomes \[\int \frac{(2 \sin \theta)^5}{2 \abs{\cos \theta}} \cdot 2 \cos \theta \di \theta.\] Since the integral is indefinite, we drop the absolute value bars of \(\abs{\cos \theta}\) for simplicity. We then get \[32 \int \sin^5 \theta \di \theta \period\] But since \(\sin^5 \theta = \sin \theta \cdot \sin^4 \theta\) and \(\sin^4 \theta = (1 - \cos^2 \theta)^2, \) we have \begin{equation} \label{eq:4-x^2-theta} 32 \int \sin \theta (1 - \cos^2 \theta)^2 \di \theta \period \end{equation} We now perform the substitution \[ \ba u &= \cos \theta \\ \dd u &= - \sin \theta \di \theta \period \ea \] \(\eqRefer{eq:4-x^2-theta}\) then becomes \begin{align} -32 \int (1 - u^2)^2 \di u &= -32 \int 1 - 2u^2 + u^4 \di u \nonumber \\ &= -32 \par{u - \frac{2}{3} u^3 + \frac{1}{5} u^5} + C \nonumber \\ &= -32 \par{\cos \theta - \frac{2}{3} \cos^3 \theta + \frac{1}{5} \cos^5 \theta} + C \period \label{eq:4-x^2-u} \end{align} At this stage, we convert from \(\theta\) back to \(x.\) Because \(x = 2 \sin \theta,\) \(\sin \theta = x/2.\) Construct a right triangle whose hypotenuse has length \(2\) and that has an angle \(\theta\) whose opposite side is \(x.\) Then the adjacent side to \(\theta\) has side of length \(\sqrt{4 - x^2}.\) As a result, \(\cos \theta = \sqrt{4 - x^2}/2.\) We then substitute this expression into \(\eqRefer{eq:4-x^2-u}\) to obtain \[\boxed{-32 \par{ \frac{\sqrt{4 - x^2}}{2} - \frac{2}{3} \par{\frac{\sqrt{4 - x^2}}{2}}^3 + \frac{1}{5} \par{\frac{\sqrt{4 - x^2}}{2}}^5} + C}\]
QUESTION 10
For \[\ds \int_{4/ \sqrt 3}^4 \frac{x^3}{\sqrt{x^2 - 4}} \di x\comma \] evaluate the integral using a trigonometric substitution.
SOLUTION To eliminate the radical in the denominator, we let \(x = 2 \sec \theta:\) \[\sqrt{(2 \sec \theta)^2 - 4} = \sqrt{4 \sec^2 \theta - 4} = 2 \sqrt{\sec^2 \theta - 1} = 2 \abs{\tan \theta} \period\] Then \(\dd x = 2 \sec \theta \tan \theta \di \theta.\) When \(x = 4/ \sqrt 3,\) we find \[\frac{4}{\sqrt 3} = 2 \sec \theta \implies \theta = \frac{\pi}{6} \period\] Moreover, when \(x = 4,\) we find \[4 = 2 \sec \theta \implies \theta = \frac{\pi}{3} \period\] Thus, our new integral is \[\int_{\pi/6}^{\pi/3} \frac{(2 \sec \theta)^3}{2 \abs{\tan \theta}} \cdot 2 \sec \theta \tan \theta \di \theta \period\] For \(\pi/6 \lt \theta \lt \pi/3,\) \(\tan \theta \gt 0,\) so on this interval \(\abs{\tan \theta} = \tan \theta.\) Substituting this expression yields \[ \ba &\int_{\pi/6}^{\pi/3} \frac{8 \sec^3 \theta}{2 \tan \theta} \cdot 2 \sec \theta \tan \theta \di \theta \\ = 8 &\int_{\pi/6}^{\pi /3} \sec^4 \theta \di \theta \period \\ = 8 &\int_{\pi/6}^{\pi/3} (\tan^2 \theta + 1) \sec^2 \theta \di \theta \period \ea \] We now substitute \(u = \tan \theta\) because then \(\dd u = \sec^2 \theta \di \theta.\) Let's now change the bounds: When \(\theta = \pi/6,\) \(u = \tan \frac{\pi}{6} = 1/ \sqrt 3.\) Also, when \(\theta = \pi/3,\) \(u = \tan \frac{\pi}{3} = \sqrt 3.\) Our integral is then \[ \ba 8 \int_{1/ \sqrt 3}^{\sqrt 3} u^2 + 1 \di u &= 8 \par{\frac{1}{3} u^3 + u} \Big|_{1 / \sqrt 3}^{\sqrt 3} \\ &= \boxed{10 \sqrt{3} - \frac{80}{9 \sqrt 3}} \ea \]
QUESTION 11
For \[\ds \int \frac{x^3}{\sqrt{4 + x^2}} \di x \comma \] evaluate the integral using a trigonometric substitution.
SOLUTION We rewrite the denominator with no radical by substituting \(x = 2 \tan \theta:\) \[\sqrt{4 + (2 \tan \theta)^2} = \sqrt{4 + 4 \tan^2 \theta} = \sqrt{4(1 + \tan^2 \theta)} = 2 \sec \theta \period\] (We ignore the absolute value bars because we have an indefinite integral.) Then \(\dd x = 2 \sec^2 \theta \di \theta\) and the integral becomes \[ \ba \int \frac{8 \tan^3 \theta}{2 \sec \theta} \, \sec^2 \theta \di \theta &= 4 \int \tan^3 \theta \sec \theta \di \theta \\ &= 4 \int \tan \theta (\sec^2 \theta - 1) \sec \theta \di \theta \period \ea \] We now substitute \(u = \sec \theta\) because \(\dd u = \sec \theta \tan \theta \di \theta.\) We then attain \[ \ba 4 \int u^2 - 1 \di u &= 4 \par{\frac{u^3}{3} - u} + C \\ &= 4 \par{\frac{1}{3} \sec^3 \theta - \sec \theta} + C \period \ea \] Because \(x = 2 \tan \theta,\) we have \(\tan \theta = x/2.\) If you construct a right triangle of legs of lengths \(x\) and \(2,\) you may verify that \(\sec \theta = \sqrt{x^2 + 4}/2.\) Therefore, our final answer is \[\boxed{\frac{4}{3} \par{\frac{\sqrt{x^2 + 4}}{2}}^3 - 2 \sqrt{x^2 + 4} + C}\]
QUESTION 12
For \[\ds \int \frac{1}{\sqrt{x^2 - 4x + 1}} \di x \comma \] evaluate the integral using a trigonometric substitution.
SOLUTION We rewrite the denominator of the integrand as \[\sqrt{(x - 2)^2 - 3} \comma\] from which we substitute \(x - 2 = \sqrt 3 \sec \theta.\) This substitution results in a new denominator of \[\sqrt{(\sqrt 3 \sec \theta)^2 - 3} = \sqrt{3} \abs{\tan \theta} \period\] But since we have an indefinite integral, we drop these absolute value bars for convenience. Moreover, to solve for \(\dd x,\) we first write \(x\) explicitly: \[ \ba x &= 2 + \sqrt 3 \sec \theta \\ \dd x &= \sqrt 3 \sec \theta \tan \theta \di \theta \period \ea \] Now the integral becomes \[ \ba &\int \frac{1}{\sqrt 3 \tan \theta} \cdot \sqrt 3 \sec \theta \tan \theta \di \theta \\ = \; &\int \sec \theta \di \theta \\ = \; &\ln \abs{\sec \theta + \tan \theta} + C \period \ea \] Because \(x - 2 = \sqrt 3 \sec \theta,\) it follows that \(\sec \theta = (x - 2)/ \sqrt 3.\) You may verify that \(\tan \theta =\) \(\sqrt{(x - 2)^2 - 3}/ \sqrt 3.\) Consequently, we obtain \[ \ba &\ln \abs{\frac{x - 2}{\sqrt 3} + \sqrt{\frac{(x - 2)^2 - 3}{3}}} + C_1 \\ = \; &\ln \abs{x - 2 + \sqrt{(x - 2)^2 - 3}} - \ln \sqrt 3 + C_1 \period \ea \] Since \(- \ln \sqrt 3\) is a constant, we rewrite \(- \ln \sqrt 3 + C_1\) as simply one constant: \(C.\) Thus, our final answer is \[\boxed{\ln \abs{\frac{x - 2}{\sqrt 3} + \sqrt{\frac{(x - 2)^2 - 3}{3}}} + C} \]
QUESTION 13
For \[\ds \int \frac{1}{\sqrt{13 - 2x - x^2}} \di x\comma \] evaluate the integral using a trigonometric substitution.
SOLUTION We rewrite the denominator of the integrand as \[\sqrt{14 - (x + 1)^2} \period\] We now substitute \(x + 1 = \sqrt{14} \sin \theta\) to clear the radical: \[\sqrt{14 - (\sqrt{14} \sin \theta)^2} = \sqrt{14} \abs{\cos \theta} \period\] But we ignore the absolute value bars of \(\abs{\cos \theta}\) because we have an indefinite integral. Also, \(\dd x = \sqrt{14} \cos \theta \di \theta.\) Thus, our integral is \[ \ba \int \frac{1}{\sqrt{14} \cos \theta} \cdot \sqrt{14} \cos \theta \di \theta &= \int \di \theta \\ &= \theta + C \period \ea \] Since \(x + 1 = \sqrt{14} \sin \theta,\) it follows that \(\theta = \sin^{-1} \par{\frac{x + 1}{\sqrt{14}}}.\) Therefore, our final answer is \[\boxed{\sin^{-1} \par{\frac{x + 1}{\sqrt{14}}} + C}\]
QUESTION 14
For \[\ds \int \frac{x^2}{(4 - 9x^2)^{3/2}} \di x\comma \] evaluate the integral using a trigonometric substitution.
SOLUTION We let \(x = \frac{2}{3} \sin \theta\) to simplify the denominator of the integrand: \[\par{4 - 9 \par{\frac{2}{3} \sin \theta}^2}^{3/2} = (4 - 4 \sin^2 \theta)^{3/2} = 8 \cos^3 \theta \period \] Also, \(\dd x = \frac{2}{3} \cos \theta \di \theta.\) The integral then becomes \[ \ba \int \frac{\par{\frac{2}{3} \sin \theta}^2}{8 \cos^3 \theta} \cdot \frac{2}{3} \cos \theta \di \theta &= \frac{1}{27} \int \frac{\sin^2 \theta}{\cos^2 \theta} \di \theta \\ &= \frac{1}{27} \int \tan^2 \theta \di \theta \\ &= \frac{1}{27} \int \sec^2 \theta - 1 \di \theta \\ &= \frac{1}{27} \tan \theta - \frac{1}{27} \theta + C \period \ea \] Since \(x = \frac{2}{3} \sin \theta,\) it follows that \(\sin \theta = 3x/2.\) Construct a triangle whose hypotenuse has length \(2\) and that has an angle \(\theta\) whose opposite side is \(3x.\) You may then verify that \(\tan \theta = 3x/\sqrt{4 - 9x^2}.\) Moreover, \(\theta = \sin^{-1}(3x/2).\) Therefore, our final answer is \[\boxed{\frac{x}{9 \sqrt{4 - 9x^2}} - \frac{1}{27} \sin^{-1} \par{\frac{3x}{2}} + C }\]
QUESTION 15
Show that the area bounded by ellipse \(x^2/a^2 + y^2/b^2 = 1\) is \(\pi ab.\)
SOLUTION Let's call this bounded area "\(A.\)" We then solve for \(y,\) the function that denotes the upper-half of the ellipse: \[y = b \sqrt{1 - \frac{x^2}{a^2}} \period\] Then \[ \ba A &= 4 \int_0^a y \di x \\ &= 4b \int_0^a \sqrt{1 - \frac{x^2}{a^2}} \di x \\ &= \frac{4b}{a} \int_0^a \sqrt{a^2 - x^2} \di x \period \ea \] We then substitute \(x = a \sin \theta\) to rewrite the radical as \[\sqrt{a^2 - (a \sin \theta)^2} = \sqrt{a^2 - a^2 \sin^2 \theta} = a \abs{\cos \theta} \period\] Additionally, \(\dd x = a \cos \theta \di \theta.\) We now change the bounds: When \(x = 0,\) we find \[0 = a \sin \theta \implies \theta = 0 \period\] When \(x = a,\) we find \[a = a \sin \theta \implies \theta = \frac{\pi}{2} \period\] Thus, the integral becomes \[\frac{4b}{a} \int_0^{\pi/2} a \abs{\cos \theta} \cdot a \cos \theta \di \theta \period \] Since \(\cos \theta \gt 0\) for \(0 \lt \theta \lt \pi/2,\) we replace \(\abs{\cos \theta}\) with \(\cos \theta.\) Then we have \[ \ba \frac{4b}{a} \int_0^{\pi/2} a^2 \cos^2 \theta \di \theta &= 4ab \int_0^{\pi/2} \frac{1}{2} (1 - \cos 2 \theta) \di \theta \\ &= 2ab \par{\theta - \frac{1}{2} \sin 2 \theta} \Big|_0^{\pi/2} \\ &= 2ab \par{\frac{\pi}{2} - \frac{1}{2} \sin \pi} - 2ab \par{0 - \frac{1}{2} \sin 0} \\ &= \pi ab \comma \ea \] as requested. Note that if \(a = b,\) then the ellipse is a circle and we get our famous \(A = \pi a^2.\)
