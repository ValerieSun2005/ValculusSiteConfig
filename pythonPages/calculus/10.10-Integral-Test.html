This section discusses the Integral Test along with a proof. We use this test to establish that the Harmonic Series, \(\sum 1/n,\) diverges.

Consider the sum \(\sum_{n = k}^\infty a_n.\) Let \(f\) be a function that satisfies \(f(n) = a_n.\) We name three conditions for the Integral Test: \(f(x)\) must be continuous, positive, and decreasing for all \(x \geq k.\) Then the Integral Test states that \[ \ba \textrm{if} \; \int_k^\infty f(x) \di x \; \text{diverges,} \; &\textrm{then} \sum_{n = k}^\infty a_n \; \text{diverges} \\ \textrm{if} \; \int_k^\infty f(x) \di x \; \text{diverges,} \; &\textrm{then} \sum_{n = k}^\infty a_n \; \text{diverges.} \ea \] In other words, the convergence or divergence of the improper integral \(\int_k^\infty f(x) \di x\) matches the convergence or divergence of the infinite sum \(\sum_{n = k}^\infty a_n.\)

INTEGRAL TEST
Let \(f\) be a function that satisfies \(f(n) = a_n.\) If \(f(x)\) is continuous, positive, and decreasing for all \(x \geq k,\) then:
  • If \(\int_k^\infty f(x) \di x\) converges, then \(\sum_{n = k}^\infty a_n\) also converges.
  • If \(\int_k^\infty f(x) \di x\) diverges, then \(\sum_{n = k}^\infty a_n\) also diverges.
EXAMPLE 1
Determine whether \(\ds \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} n^{2}e^{-n^3}\) converges or diverges.
Let \(f(x) = x^{2}e^{-x^3}.\) Then we find \[ \ba f'(x) &= 2xe^{-x^3} - 3x^{4} e^{-x^3} \\ &= xe^{-x^3}(2-3x^3) \period \ea \] Since \(f'(x) \lt 0\) for all \(x \geq 2,\) \(f(x)\) is decreasing on this interval. Consequently, the series meets the conditions of the Integral Test: \(f(x)\) is continuous, positive, and decreasing for all \(x \geq 2.\) Thus, we consider \(\int_2^\infty f(x) \di x:\) If this improper integral diverges, then \(\sum_{n=2}^{\infty} n^{2}e^{-n^3}\) also diverges. Likewise, if this improper integral converges, then \(\sum_{n=2}^{\infty} n^{2}e^{-n^3}\) also converges. We write \[\int_2^\infty x^2 e^{-x^3} \di x = \lim_{b \to \infty} \int_2^b x^2 e^{-x^3} \di x \period \] Now we substitute \[ \ba u &= x^3 \\ \di u &= 3x^2 \di x \period \ea \] When \(x = 2,\) \(u = 2^3 = 8;\) when \(x = \infty,\) \(u = \infty.\) Thus, the integral becomes \[ \ba \lim_{b \to \infty} \int_8^b e^{-u} \di u &= -\lim_{b \to 8} \frac{1}{e^u} \Big]_8^b \\ &= 1/8 \period \ea \] Since \(\int_2^\infty f(x) \di x\) converges (is finite), \(\sum_{n = 2}^\infty n^2 e^{-n^3} \) converges.
EXAMPLE 2
Determine whether \(\ds \sum_{n=3}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n \ln n}\) converges or diverges.

We let \[f(x) = \frac{1}{x \ln x} \comma\] which is positive and continuous for \(x \geq 3.\) To determine whether \(f(x)\) is decreasing on this interval, we find \[f'(x) = \frac{-1 - \ln x }{(x \ln x)^2} \comma \] which is negative for \(x \geqslant e.\) As a result, \(f(x)\) is decreasing for \(x \geqslant 3,\) so \(\sum_{n = 3}^\infty \frac{1}{n \ln n} \) meets all three criteria of the Integral Test.

Now we evaluate $$\int_3^\infty f(x) \di x = \int_{3}^{\infty} \frac{1}{x \ln x} \, \textrm{d} x \period $$ We substitute \[ \ba u &= \ln x \\ \di u &= \frac{1}{x} \di x \period \ea \] When \(x = 3,\) \(u = \ln 3;\) when \(x = \infty,\) \(u = \infty.\) Thus, the integral becomes \[ \int_{\ln 3}^{\infty} \frac{1}{u} \di u = \ln u \Big|_{\ln 3}^{\infty} \implies \textrm{divergent.} \]

Because \(\int_{3}^{\infty} f(x) \di x\) diverges, so does \(\sum_{n = 3}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n \ln n}\) by the Integral Test.
