1.3 — Evaluating Limits Algebraically

Introduction

This section defines indeterminate forms and covers how to evaluate limits of the indeterminate forms \(\indZero\) and \(\indInfty.\) We learn to evaluate Limits of Rational Functions at Infinity and Limits at Vertical Asymptotes. We then discuss Factorization, Rationalization, and using Trigonometric Identities as methods to evaluate limits of the indeterminate form \(\indZero.\)

Defining an Indeterminate Form

We begin by defining an indeterminate form. An limit is of an indeterminate form if direct substitution doesn't yield an answer. This section focuses on two indeterminate forms: \(\indZero\) and \(\indInfty.\) Consider the family of limits \begin{equation} L = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} \comma g(x) \ne 0 \period \label{eq:family-of-limits} \end{equation} If both \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) approach \(0\) as \(x\) tends to \(a,\) then we say \(L\) is of the indeterminate form \(\indZero.\) Similarly, if both \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) tend to \(\infty\) as \(x\) approaches \(a,\) then \(L\) is of the indeterminate form \(\indInfty.\) For example, the limit \(\lim_{x \to 0} x^2/x\) is of the indeterminate form \(\indZero\) because as \(x \to 0,\) \[x^2 \to 0 \and x \to 0 \period \] Performing a direct substitution—that is, plugging \(x = 0\) into \(x^2/x\)—yields \(\indZero,\) a meaningless expression. In contrast, the limit \(\lim_{x \to \infty} x^2/x\) is of the indeterminate form \(\indInfty\) since as \(x \to \infty,\) \[x^2 \to \infty \and x \to \infty \period\] It's important to distinguish between a function that approaches \(0\) and a function that equals \(0.\) In \(\eqRefer{eq:family-of-limits},\) if \(L\) is of an indeterminate form \(\indZero\) or \(\indInfty,\) then both \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) are changing; neither function is static. For example, the limit \(\lim_{x \to 0} 0/x\) is not of the indeterminate form \(\indZero,\) since the numerator is fixed at \(0.\)

INDETERMINATE FORMS
Consider the limit \[L = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} \comma g(x) \ne 0 \period \]
  • If both \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) approach \(0\) as \(x\) approaches \(a,\) then \(L\) is in the indeterminate form \(\indZero.\)
  • If both \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) approach \(\infty\) as \(x\) approaches \(a,\) then \(L\) is in the indeterminate form \(\indInfty.\)

Limits of Rational Functions at Infinity

Consider the rational function \[f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 4x + 9}{x^3 - 2} \period \] To find \(\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x),\) we divide the numerator and denominator by the highest-degree term of \(f(x)\)—namely, \(x^3,\) found in the denominator. We then get \[ \ba \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^2 - 4x + 9}{x^3 - 2} &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^2 - 4x + 9}{x^3 - 2} \cdot \frac{1/x^3}{1/x^3} \nl &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1/x - 4/x^2 + 9/x^3}{1 - 2/x^3} \nl &= 0 \period \ea \] Consequently, as \(x\) approaches infinity, \(f(x)\) approaches \(0.\) This result is intuitive since the horizontal asymptote to the graph of \(y = f(x)\) is \(y = 0.\)

But we have a shortcut: As \(x\) grows infinitely large, only the highest-degree terms in the numerator and denominator are significant. In this case, as \(x\) grows to infinity, the behavior of \(f(x)\) matches the behavior of \(x^2/x^3 = 1/x,\) which decays to \(0.\) Therefore, \[\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^2 - 4x + 9}{x^3 - 2} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^2}{x^3} = 0 \period\] Thus, the limit of a rational function at infinity matches the function's end behavior.

LIMITS OF RATIONAL EXPRESSIONS
To determine the limit of a rational expression, take the limit of the highest-degree terms in the numerator and denominator.

Let's now generalize this fact. Suppose \(P\) and \(Q\) are positive polynomials. Consider the limit \[L = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{P(x)}{Q(x)} \period\] We denote the degree of \(P\) as \(\deg(P)\) and the degree of \(Q\) as \(\deg(Q).\)

  • If \(\deg(P) \gt \deg(Q),\) then \(L = \infty.\)
  • If \(\deg(P) = \deg(Q),\) then \(L = C,\) where \(C\) is the coefficient of the highest-degree term of \(P\) divided by the coefficient of the highest-degree term of \(Q.\)
  • If \(\deg(P) \lt \deg(Q),\) then \(L = 0.\)
LIMITS OF RATIONAL EXPRESSIONS AT INFINITY
Let \(P\) and \(Q\) be positive polynomials. Consider the limit \[L = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{P(x)}{Q(x)} \period\] Let \(\deg(P)\) denote the degree of \(P,\) and \(\deg(Q)\) denote the degree of \(Q.\)
  • If \(\deg(P) \gt \deg(Q),\) then \(L = \infty.\)
  • If \(\deg(P) = \deg(Q),\) then \(L = C,\) where \(C\) is the coefficient of the highest-degree term of \(P\) divided by the coefficient of the highest-degree term of \(Q.\)
  • If \(\deg(P) \lt \deg(Q),\) then \(L = 0.\)
EXAMPLE 1
For each of (i)–(iii), evaluate the limit.
  1. \(\ds \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{3x^3 - 4x^2 + 16}{5x^3 + 8x - 2} \)
  2. \(\ds \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{-8x^2 + \frac{3}{4}x + 16}{5x - 2}\)
  3. \(\ds \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{8 + 3x^2 - x^9}{10x^9} \)
  1. We consider only the highest-degree terms on the numerator and denominator. We then get \[ \ba \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{3x^3 - 4x^2 + 16}{5x^3 + 8x - 2} &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{3x^3}{5x^3} \nl &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{3}{5} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{3}{5}} \ea \]
  2. The highest-degree term on the numerator is \(-8x^2,\) while the highest-degree term on the denominator is \(5x.\) Therefore, \[ \ba \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{-8x^2 + \frac{3}{4}x + 16}{5x - 2} &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{-8x^2}{5x} \nl &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{-8x}{5} \nl &= \boxed{- \infty} \ea \] (Since the limit is unbounded, it's also correct to say that the limit does not exist.)
  3. We ignore the smaller-degree terms on the numerator and denominator. We then get \[ \ba \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{8 + 3x^2 - x^9}{10x^9} &= \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{-x^9}{10x^9} \nl &= \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{-1}{10} \nl &= \boxed{-\frac{1}{10}} \ea \]
EXAMPLE 2
Let \[f(x) = \frac{\sqrt{x^6 + 3x^3 + x}}{5x^3 + x^2 + 7} \period\] Find \(\ds \lim_{x \to -\infty} f(x)\) and \(\ds \lim_{x \to \infty} f(x).\)

To find limits at infinity, we consider only the highest-degree terms of \(f(x):\) \[\frac{\sqrt{x^6}}{5x^3} = \frac{\abs{x^3}}{5x^3} \period \] In other words, as \(x\) approaches \(\pm \infty,\) the behavior of \(f(x)\) matches the behavior of \(\abs{x^3}/5x^3. \) Then \[ \ba \lim_{x \to -\infty} f(x) &= \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{\abs{x^3}}{5x^3} \nl &= \boxed{-\frac{1}{5}} \ea \] Similarly, \[ \ba \lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\abs{x^3}}{5x^3} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{1}{5}} \ea \]

Limits at Vertical Asymptotes

We now discuss how to determine limits at vertical asymptotes. Consider the function \[f(x) = \frac{1}{x - p} \period\] The graph of \(y = f(x)\) has a vertical asymptote at \(x = p,\) so the curve approaches \(-\infty\) or \(+\infty\) on either side of the asymptote. To the left of the vertical asymptote, \[ x - p \lt 0 \for x \lt p \period \]

On the interval \(x \lt p,\) the numerator of \(f(x)\) is positive and the denominator is negative. Therefore, \(f(x)\) is negative as \(x\) approaches the vertical asymptote. We then conclude that \(\lim_{x \to p^-} f(x) = -\infty.\) Conversely, to the right of the vertical asymptote, \[ x - p \gt 0 \for x \gt p \comma \] so the sign of \(f(x)\) positive because of the following sign configuation: \[\frac{(+)}{(+)} \to (+) \period\] Thus, \(\lim_{x \to p^+} f(x) = + \infty.\) (See Figure 1.)

LIMITS AT VERTICAL ASYMPTOTES
Consider a function \(f(x)\) whose graph has vertical asymptote \(x = p.\) To determine whether \(f(x)\) approaches \(-\infty\) or \(+\infty\) on one side of \(x = p,\) consider the sign of \(f(x):\)
  • If \(f(x) \lt 0\) as \(x \to p^-,\) then \(\lim_{x \to p^-} f(x) = -\infty.\)
  • If \(f(x) \gt 0\) as \(x \to p^+,\) then \(\lim_{x \to p^+} f(x) = + \infty.\)
EXAMPLE 3
Let \[g(x) = \frac{1}{x(x - 4)} \period\] The graph of \(y = g(x)\) has vertical asymptotes at \(x = 0\) and \(x = 4.\)
  1. Describe the behavior of \(g(x)\) at the vertical asymptote \(x = 0.\)
  2. Describe the behavior of \(g(x)\) at the vertical asymptote \(x = 4.\)

  1. For \(x \lt 0,\) both factors in the denominator of \(g(x)\) are negative. Consequently, as \(x \to 0^-\) the sign of \(g(x)\) is \[ \frac{(+)}{(-)(-)} \to (+) \period \] We then conclude that \(\lim_{x \to 0^-} g(x) = +\infty.\)

    When \(0 \lt x \lt 4,\) \[x \gt 0 \and (x - 4) \lt 0 \period\] Therefore, as \(x \to 0^+\) the sign of \(g(x)\) is \[ \frac{(+)}{(+)(-)} \to (-) \period \] Then \(\lim_{x \to 0^+} g(x) = - \infty.\)

    From these limits, we conclude that \(g(x)\) approaches \(+\infty\) from the left and \(-\infty\) from the right of the vertical asymptote \(x = 0.\)

  2. For \(0 \lt x \lt 4,\) \[x \gt 0 \and (x - 4) \lt 0 \period\] Thus, the sign of \(g(x)\) as \(x \to 4^-\) is \[\frac{(+)}{(+)(-)} \to (-) \comma\] so \(\lim_{x \to 4^-} g(x) = -\infty.\)

    When \(x \gt 4,\) \[x \gt 0 \and (x - 4) \gt 0 \period\] Then the sign of \(g(x)\) as \(x \to 4^+\) is \[\frac{(+)}{(+)(+)} \to (+),\] so \(\lim_{x \to 4^+} g(x) = + \infty.\)

    These limits reveal that \(g(x)\) approaches \(-\infty\) from the left and \(+\infty\) from the right of the vertical asymptote \(x = 4.\)

Factorization

When we have the indeterminate form \(\indZero,\) our primary resource is to cancel common factors. As an example, consider the function \[f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 25}{x + 5} \period \] The limit \(\lim_{x \to -5} f(x),\) is of the indeterminate form \(\indZero.\) But the numerator of \(f(x)\) is a difference of squares: \[x^2 - 25 = (x + 5)(x - 5) \period\] Thus, \[ \ba \lim_{x \to -5} \frac{x^2 - 25}{x + 5} &= \lim_{x \to -5} \frac{(x + 5)(x - 5)}{x + 5} \nl &= \lim_{x \to -5} (x - 5) \nl &= \boxed{-10} \ea \]

The graph of \(y = f(x)\) is continuous for all \(x\) except at \(x = -5,\) the location of a removable discontinuity (Figure 2). When we cancel the common factor \((x + 5),\) we transform \(f(x)\) into an equivalent function whose limit as \(x \to 5\) may be found. This value is \(\lim_{x \to -5} f(x)\) \(= -10,\) the \(y\)-value of the removable discontinuity.

When factoring polynomials, we often have differences of squares, differences of cubes, and sums of cubes. Therefore, remember the following formulas: \begin{align} a^2 - b^2 &= (a + b)(a - b) \label{eq:diff-of-squares} \nl a^3 - b^3 &= (a - b)(a^2 + ab + b^2) \label{eq:diff-of-cubes} \nl a^3 + b^3 &= (a + b)(a^2 - ab + b^2) \period \label{eq:sum-of-cubes} \end{align}

EXAMPLE 4
For each of (i)–(iii), evaluate the limit.
  1. \(\ds \lim_{x \to -2} \frac{x^2 - 4}{x + 2} \)
  2. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 4} \frac{x^3 - 64}{x - 4} \)
  3. \(\ds \lim_{x \to -3} \frac{x^3 + 27}{x + 3} \)
  1. Trying direct substitution immediately gives the indeterminate form \(\indZero,\) so we try to find any common factors. The numerator is a difference of squares, \(x^2 - 2^2,\) so we use \(\eqRefer{eq:diff-of-squares}\) to get \[x^2 - 4 = (x + 2)(x - 2) \period \] Re-expressing the numerator and canceling out the common factor \((x + 2)\) provide \[ \ba \lim_{x \to -2} \frac{(x + 2)(x - 2)}{x + 2} &= \lim_{x \to -2} (x - 2) \nl &= \boxed{4} \ea \]
  2. An immediate direct substitution yields the form \(\indZero,\) so we try to expose common factors. Since the numerator is a difference of cubes, \(x^3 - 4^3,\) we use \(\eqRefer{eq:diff-of-cubes}\) to re-express the numerator as \[ (x - 4)(x^2 + 4x + 16) \period \] Then we get \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 4} \frac{(x^3 - 64)}{x - 4} &= \lim_{x \to 4} \frac{(x - 4)(x^2 + 4x + 16)}{x - 4} \nl &= \lim_{x \to 4} (x^2 + 4x + 16) \nl &= \boxed{48} \ea \]
  3. Since the limit is in the indeterminate form \(\indZero,\) our goal is to expose any common factors. Using \(\eqRefer{eq:sum-of-cubes},\) we rewrite the numerator as \[(x + 3)(x^2 - 3x + 9) \period \] The limit then becomes \[ \ba \lim_{x \to -3} \frac{(x + 3)(x^2 - 3x + 9 )}{x + 3} &= \lim_{x \to -3} (x^2 - 3x + 9) \nl &= \boxed{27} \ea \]

Rationalization

A limit in an indeterminate form may have no exposed common factors. But if the expression has a radical, then rationalization may expose a common factor. As an example, consider \[g(x) = \frac{\sqrt{2x + 5} - 1}{x + 2} \period \] The limit \(\lim_{x \to -2} g(x)\) is in the indeterminate form \(\indZero.\) The function \(g(x)\) has a radical in the numerator, so we try to rationalize it: We multiply the numerator and denominator each by the conjugate of \((\sqrt{2x + 5} - 1),\) which is \((\sqrt{2x + 5} + 1).\) Doing so yields \[ \ba g(x) &= \frac{\sqrt{2x + 5} - 1}{x + 2} \nl &= \frac{\sqrt{2x + 5} - 1}{x + 2} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{2x + 5} + 1}{\sqrt{2x + 5} + 1} \nl &= \frac{(2x + 5) - 1}{(x + 2) \left(\sqrt{2x + 5} + 1\right)} \nl &= \frac{2(x + 2)}{(x + 2) \left(\sqrt{2x + 5} + 1\right)} \nl &= \frac{2}{\sqrt{2x + 5} + 1} \comma x \ne -2. \ea \] We cleared the common factor \((x + 2)\) in the numerator and denominator, so \[ \ba \lim_{x \to -2} g(x) &= \lim_{x \to -2} \frac{2}{\sqrt{2x + 5} + 1} \nl &= \boxed{1} \ea \] Rationalizing and canceling the common factor \((x + 2)\) express \(g(x)\) as an equivalent function that returns values for all \(x \gt -5/2.\) The graph of \(y = g(x)\) is smooth for all \(x \gt -5/2\) but has a hole at \(x = -2.\) But our new function provides the \(y\)-value of the hole, and this value is \(\lim_{x \to -2} g(x).\)

EXAMPLE 5
For each of (i) and (ii), evaluate the limit.
  1. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 9} \frac{3x - 27}{\sqrt{x - 8} - 1} \)
  2. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 9/4} \frac{\sqrt{4x - 5} - 2}{16x - 36} \)
  1. The limit is in the indeterminate form \(\indZero.\) The denominator has a radical, so we try to rationalize the denominator. The conjugate of the denominator is \((\sqrt{x - 8} - 1),\) and we multiply the numerator and denominator each by this factor to get \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 9} \frac{3x - 27}{\sqrt{x - 8} + 1} &= \lim_{x \to 9} \frac{3x - 27}{\sqrt{x - 8} + 1} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{x - 8} - 1}{\sqrt{x - 8} - 1} \nl &= \lim_{x \to 9} \frac{(3x - 27)(\sqrt{x - 8} - 1)}{(x - 8) - 1} \nl &= \lim_{x \to 9} \frac{3(x - 9)(\sqrt{x - 8} - 1)}{x - 9} \nl &= \lim_{x \to 9} 3 \left(\sqrt{x - 8} - 1\right) \nl &= \boxed{6} \ea \]
  2. This limit is in the indeterminate form \(\indZero.\) The numerator has a radical, so we attempt to rationalize the numerator. We do so multiplying the numerator and denominator by \((\sqrt{4x - 5} + 2),\) obtaining \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 9/4} \frac{\sqrt{4x - 5} - 2}{16x - 36} &= \lim_{x \to 9/4} \frac{\sqrt{4x - 5} - 2}{16x - 36} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{4x - 5} + 2}{\sqrt{4x - 5} + 2} \nl &= \lim_{x \to 9/4} \frac{(4x - 5) - 4}{(16x - 36)(\sqrt{4x - 5} + 2) } \nl &= \lim_{x \to 9/4} \frac{4x - 9}{4(4x - 9)(\sqrt{4x - 5} + 2)} \nl &= \lim_{x \to 9/4} \frac{1}{4(\sqrt{4x - 5} + 2)} \nl &= \boxed{\frac{1}{16}} \ea \]

Trigonometric Identities

We now discuss limits of indeterminate forms that involve trigonometric expressions. We simplify limits using the following trigonometric identities: \begin{align} 1 &= \sin^2 x + \cos^2 x \label{eq:pythag-identity} \nl \sin 2x &= 2 \sin x \cos x \label{eq:sin-double-angle} \nl \cos 2x &= \cos^2 x - \sin^2 x \label{eq:cos-double-angle} \period \end{align}

EXAMPLE 6
For each of (i) and (ii), evaluate the limit.
  1. \(\ds \lim_{x \to \pi/2} \frac{\sin 2x }{\cos x} \)
  2. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1 - \cos x}{3 \sin x} \)
  3. \(\ds \lim_{x \to \pi/4} \frac{\cos 2x}{\cos x - \sin x} \)
  1. Using \(\eqRefer{eq:sin-double-angle},\) we rewrite \(\sin 2x\) as \(2 \sin x \cos x.\) Then \[ \ba \lim_{x \to \pi/2} \frac{\sin 2x}{\cos x} &= \lim_{x \to \pi/2} \frac{2 \sin x \cos x}{\cos x} \nl &= \lim_{x \to \pi/2} 2 \sin x \nl &= \boxed{2} \ea \]
  2. We multiply the numerator and denominator each by \((1 + \cos x),\) from which we find \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1 - \cos x}{3 \sin x} &= \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1 - \cos x}{3 \sin x} \cdot \frac{1 + \cos x}{1 + \cos x} \nl &= \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1 - \cos^2 x}{3 \sin x(1 + \cos x)} \period \ea \] Then using \(\eqRefer{eq:pythag-identity},\) we have \[\sin^2 x + \cos^2 x = 1 \implies \sin^2 x = 1 - \cos^2 x \period\] Substituting this new expression into the numerator yields \[ \ba &\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin^2 x}{3 \sin x(1 + \cos x)} \nl = &\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{3 (1 + \cos x)} \nl = &\frac{\sin(0)}{3\left(1 + \cos(0)\right)} \nl = &\; \boxed{0} \ea \]
  3. From \(\eqRefer{eq:cos-double-angle},\) we have \(\cos 2x = \cos^2 x - \sin^2 x.\) Since this expression is a difference of squares, we further rewrite it as \[(\cos x + \sin x)(\cos x - \sin x) \period\] Substituting in this expression, we find \[ \ba \lim_{x \to \pi/4} \frac{\cos 2x}{\cos x - \sin x} &= \lim_{x \to \pi/4} \frac{\left(\cos x + \sin x\right) \left(\cos x - \sin x\right)}{\cos x - \sin x} \nl &= \lim_{x \to \pi/4} \left(\cos x + \sin x \right) \nl &= \boxed{\sqrt{2}} \ea \]

Factoring, rationalization, and using trigonometric identities—these strategies allow us to manipulate many limits of the indeterminate form \(\indZero.\) It's important to remain vigilant for any common factors if a limit is of the indeterminate form \(\indZero.\)

  • We introduced limits of the indeterminate forms \(\indZero\) and \(\indInfty.\) Consider the limit \[L = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} \comma g(x) \ne 0 \period \]
    • If both \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) approach \(0\) as \(x\) approaches \(a,\) then \(L\) is of the indeterminate form \(\indZero.\)
    • If both \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) approach \(0\) as \(x\) approaches \(a,\) then \(L\) is of the indeterminate form \(\indInfty.\)
  • The limit of a rational expression at infinity is in the indeterminate form \(\indInfty.\) Consider only the highest-degree terms. The result agrees with the end behavior of the rational function.
  • Let \(P\) and \(Q\) be positive polynomials. Consider the limit \[L = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{P(x)}{Q(x)} \period\] Let \(\deg(P)\) denote the degree of \(P,\) and \(\deg(Q)\) denote the degree of \(Q.\)
    • If \(\deg(P) \gt \deg(Q),\) then \(L = \infty.\)
    • If \(\deg(P) = \deg(Q),\) then \(L = C,\) where \(C\) is the coefficient of the highest-degree term of \(P\) divided by the coefficient of the highest-degree term of \(Q.\)
    • If \(\deg(P) \lt \deg(Q),\) then \(L = 0.\)
  • Consider a function \(f(x)\) whose graph has vertical asymptote \(x = p.\) To determine whether \(f(x)\) approaches \(-\infty\) or \(+\infty\) on one side of \(x = p,\) consider the sign of \(f(x):\)
    • If \(f(x) \lt 0\) as \(x \to p^-,\) then \(\lim_{x \to p^-} f(x) = -\infty.\)
    • If \(f(x) \gt 0\) as \(x \to p^+,\) then \(\lim_{x \to p^+} f(x) = + \infty.\)
  • To evaluate a limit of the indeterminate form \(\indZero,\) try to cancel any common factors. Factoring polynomials, rationalizing, and using trigonometric identities are strategies to expose common factors.
  • When factoring a polynomial, recall the following formulas: \[ \ba a^2 - b^2 &= (a + b)(a - b) \nl a^3 - b^3 &= (a - b)(a^2 + ab + b^2) \nl a^3 + b^3 &= (a + b)(a^2 - ab + b^2) \period \ea \]
  • Recall the following trigonometric identities: \[ \ba 1 &= \sin^2 x + \cos^2 x \nl \sin 2x &= 2 \sin x \cos x \nl \cos 2x &= \cos^2 x - \sin^2 x \period \ea \]
