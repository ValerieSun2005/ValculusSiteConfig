6.4 — Integration by Partial Fractions

In this section, we discuss the method of integration by partial fractions. We discuss Decomposition with Linear Factors, Decomposition with Reducible Factors, and Decomposition with Irreducible Factors. This integration technique requires decomposing a rational expression into a simplier form: partial fractions. Consider \[ f(x) = \frac{P(x)}{Q(x)} \comma\] where \(P\) and \(Q\) are both polynomials. If the degree of \(P\) is greater than or equal to the degree of \(Q\)—that is, if \(\deg(P) \geq \deg(Q)\)—then \(f\) should first be simplified by polynomial division. The decomposition of \(f\) into partial fractions applies only if \(\deg(Q) > \deg(P).\)

WHEN TO DECOMPOSE INTO PARTIAL FRACTIONS
Consider the rational function \[f(x) = \frac{P(x)}{Q(x)} \period\]
  • If \(\deg(P) \geq \deg(Q),\) then \(f\) should first be simplified by polynomial division.
  • If \(\deg(Q) > \deg(P),\) then decomposing \(f\) into partial fractions is appropriate.
EXAMPLE 1
For each integral, determine whether it is appropriate to first perform partial fraction decomposition or polynomial division.
  1. \(\ds \int \frac{x^2 - 4x + 9}{3 - x} \, \dd x \)
  2. \(\ds \int \frac{x - 3}{(2x - 5)(9 - x)} \, \dd x \)
  3. \(\ds \int \frac{8 - x^2}{3x^3 - 9} \, \dd x \)
  1. The degree of the numerator is greater than the degree of the denominator. Thus, polynomial division must be performed first.
  2. The degree of the denominator is greater than the degree of the numerator. Thus, it is appropriate to first perform partial fraction decomposition.
  3. The degree of the denominator is greater than the degree of the numerator. Thus, it is appropriate to first perform partial fraction decomposition.

Decomposition with Linear Factors

Let's discuss the partial fraction decomposition of a rational expression involving linear factors. Consider the example \[f(x) = \frac{x^2 + 2}{(x - 2)(x + 3)} \comma \] where \((x - 2)\) and \((x + 3)\) are linear factors since they're of degree one and don't repeat. The degrees of the numerator and denominator are equal, so we first perform polynomial division to simplify \(f.\) Doing so gives \[f(x) = 1 + \frac{8 - x}{(x - 2)(x + 3)} \period\] Now we perform the partial fraction decomposition for \(\frac{8 - x}{(x - 2)(x + 3)}\) since the numerator has a smaller degree than does the denominator. We rewrite the expression as \[\frac{8 - x}{(x - 2)(x + 3)} = \frac{A}{x - 2} + \frac{B}{x + 3} \period \] The terms \(\frac{A}{x - 2}\) and \(\frac{B}{x + 3}\) represent our partial fractions. Our goal is then determining the values of the coefficients \(A\) and \(B.\) With some algebraic manipulation, we get \[ \ba \frac{8 - x}{(x - 2)(x + 3)} &= \frac{A}{x - 2} \left(\frac{x + 3}{x + 3}\right) + \frac{B}{x + 3} \left(\frac{x - 2}{x - 2}\right) \nl \frac{8 - x}{(x - 2)(x + 3)} &= \frac{A(x + 3) + B(x - 2)}{(x - 2)(x + 3)} \nl 8 - x &= A(x + 3) + B(x - 2) \period \ea \] To solve for \(A\) and \(B,\) we perform substitutions to equate either \(A(x + 3)\) or \(B(x - 2)\) to zero. Namely, letting \(x = 2\) gives \[6 = 5A + 0 \implies A = \frac{6}{5} \period\] Similarly, assigning \(x = -3\) yields \[11 = 0 - 5B \implies B = -\frac{11}{5} \period\] These substitutions are convenient because they permit us to solve for \(A\) and \(B\) immediately. The partial fraction decomposition is then \[ \frac{x^2 + 2}{(x - 2)(x + 3)} = 1 + \frac{6/5}{x - 2} - \frac{11/5}{x + 3} \period \] Notice the simplicity of integrating this expression! In evaluating \(\int f(x) \, \dd x,\) we attain \[ \int 1 + \frac{6/5}{x - 2} - \frac{11/5}{x + 3} \, \dd x = \boxed{x + \frac{6}{5} \ln|x - 2| - \frac{11}{5} \ln|x + 3| + C \period} \]

EXAMPLE 2
Evaluate \(\ds \int \frac{x}{x^2 - x - 12} \, \dd x .\)
Notice that the denominator factors to \((x - 4)(x + 3).\) Because the degree of the denominator, \(2,\) is greater than the degree of the numerator, \(1,\) we perform partial fraction decomposition. Doing so yields \[ \ba \frac{x}{(x - 4)(x + 3)} &= \frac{A}{x - 4} + \frac{B}{x + 3} \\[2ex] \frac{x}{(x - 4)(x + 3)} &= \frac{A(x + 3) + B(x - 4)}{(x - 4)(x + 3)} \period \ea \] Both sides have a common denominator, so we equate the numerators to get \[x = A(x + 3) + B(x - 4) \period\] At this point, to solve for \(A\) and \(B,\) we perform a series of substitutions. If \(x = -3,\) then we obtain \[ \ba -3 &= A(-3 + 3) + B(-3 - 4) \\ -3 &= -7B \nl \frac{3}{7} &= B \period \ea \] Similarly, let \(x = 4.\) We then have \[ \ba 4 &= A(4 + 3) + B(4 - 4) \\ 4 &= 7A \nl \frac{4}{7} &= A \period \ea \] These substitutions produce equations in which one of the unknowns \(A\) or \(B\) is eliminated. This strategy enables us to calculate \(A\) and \(B\) easily. Therefore, we have \[ \ba \int \frac{x}{(x - 4)(x + 3)} \, \dd x &= \int \frac{A}{x - 4} + \frac{B}{x + 3} \, \dd x \\ &= \int \frac{4/7}{x - 4} + \frac{3/7}{x + 3} \, \dd x \nl &= \boxed{\frac{4}{7} \ln|x - 4| + \frac{3}{7} \ln|x + 3| + C \period}\\ \ea \]

Decomposition with Reducible Factors

The following integrals often show up in integrating partial fractions. Therefore, you should remember them as reference.

COMMON INTEGRALS WITH PARTIAL FRACTIONS
\[ \ba &\int \frac{1}{ax + b} \, \dd x = \frac{1}{a} \ln|ax + b| + C \nl &\int \frac{1}{(ax + b)^n} \, \dd x = \frac{(ax + b)^{1 - n}}{a(1 - n)} + C \comma \, n \ne 1 \nl &\int \frac{1}{x^2 + a^2} \, \dd x = \frac{1}{a} \tan^{-1} \left(\frac{x}{a}\right) + C \period \ea \]

Let's also review the factoring of differences of squares, sums of cubes, and differences of cubes. The difference of the squares of two numbers \(a\) and \(b\) is \begin{equation} a^2 - b^2 = (a + b)(a - b) \period \end{equation} The sum of the cubes of \(a\) and \(b\) is \begin{equation} a^3 + b^3 = (a + b)(a^2 - ab + b^2) \comma \end{equation} and their difference is \begin{equation} a^3 - b^3 = (a - b)(a^2 + ab + b^2) \period \end{equation} Factoring a difference of squares yields two linear factors. In contrast, factoring a sum or difference of cubes produces a linear factor and an irreducible quadratic. If one of these forms is in a denominator whose degree is greater than the degree of the numerator, then it's appropriate to perform partial fraction decomposition using Equations \((1),\) \((2),\) and \((3)\) as needed.

EXAMPLE 3
Evaluate \(\ds \int \frac{2x^2 + 3}{(2x - 3)(4 - x)} \, \dd x .\)
In the integrand, the degree of the numerator is equal to the degree of the denominator. Thus, we must first perform polynomial division. Doing so gives \[\frac{2x^2 + 3}{(2x - 3)(4 - x)} = -1 + \frac{11x - 9}{(4 - x)(2x - 3)} \period\] We now determine the partial fraction decomposition of \(\frac{11x - 9}{(4 - x)(2x - 3)}.\) The expression contains two linear factors, \((4 - x)\) and \((2x - 3),\) so we have \[\frac{11x - 9}{(4 - x)(2x - 3)} = \frac{A}{4 - x} + \frac{B}{2x - 3} \period\] Multiplying both sides by \((4 - x)(2x - 3)\) produces \[11x - 9 = A(2x - 3) + B(4 - x) \period\] To solve for \(A,\) we let \(x = 4 \!:\) \[35 = 5A \implies A = 7 \period\] Similarly, assigning \(x = 3/2\) yields \[\frac{15}{2} = 0 + \frac{5}{2} B \implies B = 3 \period\] The new integral, therefore, is \[\int -1 + \left(\frac{7}{4 - x} + \frac{3}{2x - 3} \right) \, \dd x \period\] Our final answer is then \[\boxed{-x - 7\ln|4 - x| + \frac{3}{2} \ln|2x - 3| + C \period}\]
EXAMPLE 4
Evaluate \(\ds \int \frac{x - 1}{x(9 - x^2)} \, \dd x .\)
We factor the integrand as \[\frac{x - 1}{x(3 - x)(3 + x)},\] whose partial fraction decomposition is expressed by \[\frac{x - 1}{x(3 - x)(3 + x)} = \frac{A}{x} + \frac{B}{3 - x} + \frac{C}{3 + x} \period\] Multiplying both sides by \(x(3 - x)(3 + x)\) produces \[x - 1 = A(3 - x)(3 + x) + Bx(3 + x) + Cx(3 - x) \period\] We notice that two terms have the factor \(x,\) so we eliminate them by letting \(x = 0.\) Doing so allows us to solve for \(A\!:\) \[-1 = 9A \implies A = -\frac{1}{9} \period\] Likewise, letting \(x = 3\) gives \[2 = 0 + 18B + 0 \implies B = \frac{1}{9} \period\] Finally, we let \(x = -3\) to get \[-4 = -18C \implies C = \frac{2}{9} \period\] Thus, our partial fraction decomposition is \[\frac{-1/9}{x} + \frac{1/9}{3 - x} + \frac{2/9}{3 + x} \period\] Integrating, we obtain \[\int \frac{-1/9}{x} + \frac{1/9}{3 - x} + \frac{2/9}{3 + x} \, \dd x = \boxed{-\frac{1}{9} \ln|x| - \frac{1}{9} \ln|3 - x| + \frac{2}{9} \ln|3 + x| + C \period} \] (Note that this "\(+C \, \)" represents a constant of integration, not the coefficient \(C.\))

Decomposition with Irreducible Factors

Let's now discuss the decomposition of fractions involving irreducible factors. Partial fraction decomposition requires factoring the denominator completely, but in some cases the denominator has irreducible factors. Examples of irreducible factors include the forms \((ax + b)^k \comma\) \(ax^2 + bx + c \comma\) and \((ax^2 + bx + c)^k \period\) The following table shows the shape of partial fraction decompositions for various factors in the denominator.

FACTORS IN DENOMINATOR
Consider \(k\) to be a positive integer.
Term in Denominator Partial Fraction Shape
\(ax + b\) \(\ds \frac{A}{ax + b}\)
\((ax + b)^k\) \(\ds \frac{A_1}{ax + b} + \frac{A_2}{(ax + b)^2} + \cdots + \frac{A_k}{(ax + b)^k} \)
\(ax^2 + bx + c\) \(\ds \frac{Ax + B}{ax^2 + bx + c}\)
\((ax^2 + bx + c)^k\) \(\ds \frac{A_1 x + B_1}{ax^2 + bx + c} + \frac{A_2 x + B_2}{(ax^2 + bx + c)^2} + \cdots + \frac{A_k x + B_k}{(ax^2 + bx + c)^k} \)
EXAMPLE 5
Determine the partial fraction decomposition of \(\ds \frac{4x + 2}{x^3 - 1} \period \)
The denominator, \((x^3 - 1),\) is a difference of cubes. From equation \((3),\) we factor it as \[(x - 1)(x^2 + x + 1) \comma\] where \((x - 1)\) is a linear factor and \((x^2 + x + 1)\) is an irreducible quadratic. Therefore, we have \[ \frac{4x + 2}{x^3 - 1} = \frac{A}{x - 1} + \frac{Bx + C}{x^2 + x + 1} \period \] Multiplying both sides by \((x^3 - 1),\) we acquire \[ \ba 4x + 2 &= A(x^2 + x + 1) + (Bx + C)(x - 1) \\ 4x + 2 &= A(x^2 + x + 1) + Bx(x - 1) + C(x - 1) \period \ea \] Let's now solve for \(A,\) \(B,\) and \(C\) by performing substitutions. Letting \(x = 1\) eliminates terms involving \(B\) and \(C,\) yielding \[6 = 3A + 0 + 0 \implies A = 2 \period\] Moreover, letting \(x = 0\) gives \[ \ba 2 &= A + 0 - C \\ 2 &= 2 - C \\ C &= 0 \period \ea \] We now have \[4x + 2 = 2(x^2 + x + 1) + Bx(x - 1) \period\] We assign \(x\) any value to immediately solve for \(B.\) The most convenient assignment is \(x = 2,\) from which we get \[10 = 14 + 2B \implies B = -2 \period\] Thus, our partial fraction decomposition is \[ \frac{4x + 2}{x^3 - 1} = \boxed{\frac{2}{x - 1} - \frac{2x}{x^2 + x + 1} \period} \]
EXAMPLE 6
Evaluate \(\ds \int \frac{1}{x(x + 2)^2} \, \dd x. \)
The form for the partial fraction decomposition of the integrand is \[ \frac{1}{x(x + 2)^2} = \frac{A}{x} + \frac{B}{(x + 2)} + \frac{C}{(x + 2)^2} \comma \] or we could treat \((x + 2)^2\) as a quadratic by writing \[ \frac{1}{x(x + 2)^2} = \frac{A}{x} + \frac{Bx + C}{(x + 2)^2} \period \] (Either form is correct, but we'll solely focus on the first form for simplicity.) In the first form, multiplying each term by \(x(x + 2)^2\) gives \[ 1 = A(x + 2)^2 + Bx(x + 2) + Cx \period \] We now solve for \(A,\) \(B,\) and \(C\) using a series of substitutions. Letting \(x = 0,\) we get \[1 = 4A + 0 + 0 \implies A = \frac{1}{4} \period\] Another convenient substitution is letting \(x = -2.\) Doing so gives \[1 = 0 + 0 - 2 C \implies C = -\frac{1}{2} \period\] We now have \[1 = \frac{1}{4} (x + 2)^2 + Bx(x + 2) - \frac{1}{2} x \period\] At this point, the only unknown is \(B.\) Thus, we let \(x\) to be any value to immediately solve for \(B.\) An easy option is letting \(x = -1.\) Doing so produces \[1 = \frac{1}{4} - B + \frac{1}{2} \implies B = -\frac{1}{4} \period\] We have, therefore, \[ \ba \int \frac{1}{x(x + 2)^2} \, \dd x &= \int \frac{1/4}{x} - \frac{1/4}{x + 2} - \frac{1/2}{(x + 2)^2} \, \dd x \\ &= \boxed{\frac{1}{4} \ln|x| - \frac{1}{4} \ln|x + 2| + \frac{1}{2x + 4} + C \period} \ea \]
EXAMPLE 7
Evaluate \(\ds \int \frac{7 - 3x^2}{(x - 2)(2x - 6)(x^2 + 16)} \, \dd x.\)
Our integrand consists of two linear terms, \((x - 2)\) and \((2x - 6),\) and the irreducible quadratic \((x^2 + 16).\) Our partial fraction decomposition is then \[ \frac{7 - 3x^2}{(x - 2)(2x - 6)(x^2 + 16)} = \frac{A}{x - 2} + \frac{B}{2x - 6} + \frac{Cx + D}{x^2 + 16} \period \] Multiplying both sides by \((x - 2)(2x - 6)(x^2 + 16)\) yields \[ \small 7 - 3x^2 = A(2x - 6)(x^2 + 16) + B(x - 2)(x^2 + 16) + Cx(x - 2)(2x - 6) + D(x - 2)(2x - 6) \period \] All but one term contain the factor \((x - 2).\) Therefore, letting \(x = 2\) leaves only one term, producing \[-5 = -40A + 0 + 0 + 0 \implies A = \frac{1}{8} \period\] Similarly, all but one term include the factor \((2x - 6).\) Therefore, letting \(x = 3\) zeroes all of those terms, giving \[ -20 = 0 + 25B + 0 + 0 \implies B = -\frac{4}{5} \period \] At this point, we have \[ \small 7 - 3x^2 = \frac{1}{8}(2x - 6)(x^2 + 16) - \frac{4}{5} (x - 2)(x^2 + 16) + Cx(x - 2)(2x - 6) + D(x - 2)(2x - 6) \period \] Letting \(x = 0\) allows us to solve directly for \(D \! :\) \[ 7 = \frac{1}{8}(-6)(16) - \frac{4}{5}(-2)(16) + 0 + D(-2)(-6) \implies D = -\frac{11}{20} \period \\ \] Our equation is now \[ \small 7 - 3x^2 = \frac{1}{8}(2x - 6)(x^2 + 16) - \frac{4}{5} (x - 2)(x^2 + 16) + Cx(x - 2)(2x - 6) - \frac{11}{20} (x - 2)(2x - 6) \period \] We now assign \(x\) any value to immediately solve for \(C.\) A simple option is letting \(x = 1,\) which yields \[ 4 = -\frac{17}{2} + \frac{68}{5} + 4C - \frac{11}{5} \implies C = \frac{11}{40} \period \] We have, as a result, \[ \int \frac{7 - 3x^2}{(x - 2)(2x - 6)(x^2 + 16)} \, \dd x = \int \frac{\frac{1}{8}}{x - 2} - \frac{\frac{4}{5}}{2x - 6} + \frac{\frac{11}{40} x - \frac{11}{20} }{x^2 + 16} \, \dd x \] We split \(\int \frac{\frac{11}{40}x - \frac{11}{20}}{x^2 + 16} \, \dd x\) into \[ \int \frac{11x/40}{x^2 + 16} \, \dd x - \int \frac{11/20}{x^2 + 16} \, \dd x \period \] The right-hand integral, \(\int \frac{11/20}{x^2 + 16} \, \dd x,\) evaluates to \[\left(-\frac{11}{20} \right)\frac{1}{4} \tan^{-1} \left(\frac{x}{4}\right) = -\frac{11}{80} \tan^{-1} \left(\frac{x}{4}\right) \period\] The left-hand integral, \(\int \frac{11x/40}{x^2 + 16} \, \dd x,\) requires the substitution \(u = x^2 + 16.\) Doing so produces \[ \int \frac{\frac{11}{40}x}{x^2 + 16} \, \dd x = \frac{11}{80} \ln(x^2 + 16) \period \] \(\Big[\) \((x^2 + 16)\) is always positive, so we don't need any absolute value bars. \(\Big]\) Therefore, \[ \ba \int \frac{7 - 3x^2}{(x - 2)(2x - 6)(x^2 + 16)} \, \dd x &= \int \frac{\frac{1}{8}}{x - 2} - \frac{\frac{4}{5}}{2x - 6} + \frac{\frac{11}{40} x - \frac{11}{20} }{x^2 + 16} \, \dd x \\ &= \boxed{\small \frac{1}{8} \ln|x - 2| - \frac{2}{5} \ln|2x - 6| + \frac{11}{80} \ln(x^2 + 16) - \frac{11}{80} \tan^{-1} \left(\frac{x}{4}\right) + c \period} \ea \] (This \(c\) is the constant of integration, not a value in the partial fraction decomposition.)
  • Partial fraction decomposition is the process of splitting a rational expression into a sum of simplier fractions. Consider \(f(x) = P(x)/Q(x),\) where \(P\) and \(Q\) are polynomials.
    • If \(\deg(P) \geq \deg(Q),\) then we first use polynomial division to simplify \(f\) before we may perform partial fraction decomposition.
    • If \(\deg(Q) > \deg(P),\) then we may perform partial fraction decomposition.
  • The factor \((ax + b)\) on the denominator takes the partial fraction \[\frac{A}{ax + b} \period\]
  • The term \((ax + b)^n\) on the denominator results in the partial fraction decomposition \[ \frac{A_1}{ax + b} + \frac{A_2}{(ax + b)^2} + \cdots + \frac{A_n}{(ax + b)^n} \period \]
  • The factor \((ax^2 + bx + c)\) on the denominator takes the partial fraction \[\frac{Ax + B}{ax^2 + bx + c} \period\]
  • The term \((ax^2 + bx + c)^n\) on the denominator results in the partial fraction decomposition \[ \frac{A_1x + B_1}{ax^2 + bx + c} + \frac{A_2 x + B_2}{(ax^2 + bx + c)^2} + \cdots + \frac{A_n x + B_n}{(ax^2 + bx + c)^n} \period\]
