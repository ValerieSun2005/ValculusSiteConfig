10.3 — Infinite Geometric Series

Introduction: Infinite Geomtric Series

An infinite geometric series takes the form \begin{equation} \label{eq:geo-series} S = \sum_{n=k}^\infty ar^n = ar^k + ar^{k+1} + ar^{k+2} + \cdots \comma \end{equation} where \(r\) is called the common ratio. In words, we say that each term differs by a common multiple, the common ratio. The series \(S\) only converges if its terms are decreasing—that is, if \(|r| \lt 1.\) If \(r\) satisfies this condition, then \(\lim_{n \to \infty} ar^n = 0.\) By contrast, if \(|r| \leq 1,\) then \(\lim_{n \to \infty} ar^n \ne 0\) and so then \(S\) diverges by the Divergence Test.

A finite geometric series \(\sum_{i = 0}^n ar^i\) equals \[ S_n = \frac{a \left(1 - r^n\right)}{1 - r} \period \] But to find the value of an infinite geometric series, we consider \(\lim_{n \to \infty} S_n:\) Since we assume \(\abs{r} \lt 1,\) we get \[ \ba \sum_{n = 0}^\infty a r^n &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{a \left(1 - r^n\right)}{1 - r} \\ &= \frac{a}{1 - r}. \ea \] We either say \(\sum_{n = 0}^\infty a r^n\) equals \(a/(1 - r)\) or converges to \(a/(1 -r).\) Both statements represent the same idea. To apply this result to the general series of \(\eqRefer{eq:geo-series},\) we write \begin{equation} \label{eq:geo-series-result} S = \frac{ar^k}{1 - r} \period \end{equation} Because \(ar^k\) is the first term of \(S,\) \(\eqRefer{eq:geo-series-result}\) may be rewritten as \(S = (\textrm{FIRST TERM})/(1 - r).\)

FORMULA FOR AN INFINITE GEOMETRIC SERIES
If \(|r| \lt 1,\) then \[\sum_{n = k}^\infty ar^n = \frac{ar^k}{1 - r} \period \]
EXAMPLE 1
For each of (i)–(iii), determine whether the series converges or diverges. If the series converges, calculate its sum.
  1. \(\ds \sum_{n = 0}^{\infty} \left(\frac{1}{5} \right)^n \)
  2. \(\ds \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{2}{8^n} \)
  3. \(\ds \sum_{n = 1}^{\infty} 4^n \cdot e^{-n} \)
  1. We find \(r = 1/5,\) so the series converges since \(-1 \lt r \lt 1.\) Then by \(\eqRefer{eq:geo-series-result},\) the series evaluates to \[\frac{\textrm{FIRST TERM}}{1-r} = \frac{1(\frac{1}{5})^0}{1-\frac{1}{5}} = \boxed{\frac{5}{4}} \]
  2. We rewrite the sum as \[\sum_{n = 1}^\infty 2 \par{\frac{1}{8}}^n \period \] The common ratio is \(r = 1/8,\) which is less between \(-1\) and \(1.\) Thus, the series converges to the value \[ \ba \frac{\textrm{FIRST TERM}}{1 - r} &= \frac{2 \left(\frac{1}{8}\right)}{1 - \frac{1}{8}} \\ &= \boxed{\frac{2}{7}} \ea \]
  3. We rewrite the series as \[\sum_{n = 1}^\infty \left(\frac{4}{e}\right)^n \period \] The common ratio is \(r = 4/e \gt 1,\) so the series diverges.
EXAMPLE 2
Let \[S = \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \left(\frac{k^2 + 1}{5}\right)^n \period \] Find the values of \(k\) for which \(S\) converges. Then determine the value \(S\) in terms of \(k.\)
The common ratio of \(S\) is \(r = (k^2 + 1)/5.\) The series \(S\) converges if \(|r| \lt 1.\) But because \((k^2 + 1)/5 \gt 0,\) we have \(|r| = r.\) Then we get \[ \ba \frac{k^2 + 1}{5} \lt 1 \implies k^2 &\lt 4 \\[2ex] |k| &\lt 2 \\[2ex] -2 \lt k &\lt 2 \period \ea \] Therefore, \(S\) converges if \(-2 \lt k \lt 2.\) On this interval, we find \[ \ba S &= \frac{\textrm{FIRST TERM}}{1 - r} \\[2ex] &= \frac{(\frac{k^2+1}{5})^2}{1-\frac{k^2+1}{5}} \\[2ex] &= \boxed{\frac{(k^2+1)^2}{25-5(k^2+1)}} \ea \]

Disguised Geometric Series

To determine whether a series is geometric, use the property \(r = a_{n + 1}/a_n.\) In words, the property states that to find the common ratio, we divide any term by its preceding term. For example, the series \[S = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{8} + \frac{1}{16} + \cdots \] is geometric since \(r = \frac{1/4}{1/2} = 1/2.\) Then we have \[ \ba S = \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \par{\frac{1}{2}}^n &= \frac{1/2}{1 - \frac{1}{2}} \\ &= 1 \period \ea \]

EXAMPLE 3
For each of (i)–(iii), determine whether the series converges or diverges. If the series converges, calculate its sum.
  1. \(\ds \; 2 - \frac{2}{3} + \frac{2}{9} - \frac{2}{27} + \cdots\)
  2. \(\ds \; \frac{5}{16} + \frac{5}{64} + \frac{5}{256} + \frac{5}{1024} + \cdots \)
  3. \(\ds \; \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{2^{n - 2}}{3^n} \)
  1. To find the common ratio, we apply the property \(r = a_{n + 1}/a_n.\) Using the first two terms, we find \[r = \frac{-2/3}{2} = - \frac{1}{3} \period \] We then rewrite the series as \[S = \sum_{n = 0}^{\infty} 2 \Big(-\frac{1}{3}\Big)^n \period \] Since \(-1 \lt r \lt 1,\) \(S\) converges and so we apply \(\eqRefer{eq:geo-series-result}\) to get \[S = \frac{\textrm{FIRST TERM}}{1-r} = \frac{2(-\frac{1}{3})^0}{1-(-\frac{1}{3})} = \boxed{\frac{3}{2}}\]
  2. We rewrite the sum either as \[S = 5\sum_{n = 2}^\infty \left(\frac{1}{4} \right)^n \] or \[\frac{5}{16} \left(1 + \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{16} + \frac{1}{64} + \cdots \right) = \frac{5}{16} \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{1}{4^n} \period \] In either case we get \[\frac{5/16}{1 - \frac{1}{4}} = \boxed{\frac{5}{12}} \]
  3. Using exponent properties, we rewrite the sum as \[ \ba \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{2^n \cdot 2^{-2}}{3^n} &= \frac{1}{4} \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^n \\ &= \frac{1/4}{1 - \frac{2}{3}} \\ &= \boxed{\frac{3}{4}} \ea \]

Repeating Decimals as Fractions

We now discuss how to express repeating decimals in terms of fractions. Consider, for example, the repeating decimal \[ 0.5\overline{3} = 0.53333333333 \dots \period\] We express each digit of the decimal as its own term, so we construct an infinite geometric series of common ratio \(r = 1/10.\) We have \[0.5 + 0.03 + 0.003 + 0.0003 + \cdots = 0.5 + 0.03\sum_{n = 0}^\infty (0.1)^n \period \] Therefore, the series equals \[ \ba 0.5 + \frac{0.03}{1 - 0.1} &= \frac{1}{2} + \frac{3/100}{9/10} \\[1ex] &= \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{30} \\[1ex] &= \boxed{\frac{8}{15}} \ea \]

EXAMPLE 4
For each of (i)–(iii), express the repeating decimal as a fraction.
  1. \(0.7\overline{6}\)
  2. \(0.64\overline{1}\)
  3. \(0.1\overline{2}\)
  1. \[ \begin{aligned} 0.7\overline{6} &= 0.7 + 0.06 + 0.006 + 0.0006 + \cdots \\ &= 0.7 + 0.06\sum_{n = 0}^\infty \left(0.1\right)^n \\ &= 0.7 + \frac{0.06}{1 - 0.1} \\ &= 0.7 + \frac{6/100}{9/10} \\ &= \frac{7}{10} + \frac{1}{15} \\ &= \boxed{\frac{23}{30}}\\ \end{aligned} \]
  2. \[ \ba 0.64\bar{1} &= 0.64 + 0.001 + 0.0001 + 0.00001 + \cdots \\ &= 0.64 + 0.001\sum_{n = 0}^\infty \left(0.1\right)^n \\ &= 0.64 + \frac{0.001}{1 - 0.1} \\ &= 0.64 + \frac{1/1000}{9/10} \\ &= \frac{64}{100} + \frac{1}{900} \\ &= \boxed{\frac{577}{900}}\\ \ea \]
  3. \[ \ba 0.1\overline{2} &= 0.1 + 0.02 + 0.002 + 0.0002 + \cdots \\ &= 0.1 + 0.02\sum_{n = 0}^\infty \left(0.1\right)^n \\ &= 0.1 + \frac{0.02}{1 - 0.1} \\ &= 0.1 + \frac{2/100}{9/10} \\ &= \frac{1}{10} + \frac{1}{45} \\ &= \boxed{\frac{11}{90}}\\ \ea \]

  • An infinite geometric series is in the form \[S = \sum_{n = k}^\infty ar^n = ar^k + ar^{k + 1} + ar^{k + 2} + \cdots \period \]
    • The common ratio is \(r = a_{n + 1}/a_n.\) Use this fact to verify whether a series is a disguised geometric series.
    • \(S\) converges to \[\frac{ar^k}{1 - r} = \frac{\textrm{FIRST TERM}}{1 - r}\] if \(|r| \lt 1.\)
  • We write a repeating decimal as an infinite geometric series of common ratio \(1/10.\)
